# Committee Review: GFlowPO (cdf32a3f-9b09-46d8-8b05-d5f0a7b8dc9f)

## One-Sentence Summary
GFlowPO casts discrete prompt optimization as Bayesian posterior inference, trains an off-policy GFlowNet with replay buffer for sample-efficient exploration, and applies a training-free Dynamic Memory Update (DMU) mechanism that progressively reshapes the meta-prompt toward high-reward regions; it reports consistent gains over StablePrompt and other baselines across text classification, instruction induction, and QA benchmarks.

## Claimed Contributions
1. Formulates prompt optimization as Bayesian posterior inference regularized by a reference-LM prior.
2. Uses off-policy GFlowNet replay training for sample-efficient prompt exploration.
3. Introduces Dynamic Memory Update (DMU) — a training-free meta-prompt adaptation mechanism interpreted as an approximate EM M-step.
4. Demonstrates consistent improvements over StablePrompt and other discrete prompt tuning baselines across text classification (6 datasets), instruction induction (24 tasks), BigBench II (18 tasks), MMLU, and OpenBookQA.

### Disagreement-Resolution Narrative

**Ablation variance (ablation_variance panel — incomplete):** Only one panelist (risk-of-fraud-detector) filed Turn A; no Turn B/C. Conclusion: "sloppy reporting, not fraud." Main tables show statistical awareness; ablation tables lack variance. Recommendation: flag as reporting-quality concern with actionable revision request for multi-seed ablation statistics or explicit N=1 labeling.

**Reward substitution generalization (reward_substitution_generalization panel — incomplete):** All five R2 panelists agree A_D(z) substitution is empirically motivated but theoretically unmotivated, and validation scope (6 classification tasks) is narrower than evaluation scope (II/BBII/QA). Partial consensus: gap most acute for QA tasks. The author rebuttal provides a principled argument: for II (exact-match), A_D(z) equals the evaluation metric; for QA, A_D(z) measures correct-answer token match, same as evaluation. Authors commit to extending correlation analysis to II/QA tasks. Recommendation: present as conditional methodological concern with authors' revision commitment.

**Asymmetric tuning (asymmetric_tuning panel — incomplete):** 2×2 split. hyperparameter-budget-auditor: real fairness concern (GFlowPO tuned, baselines not). experimental-rigor: comparison is fair, sub-field standard. claim-auditor: reward substitution is more fundamental concern. No cross-examination occurred. Recommendation: treat tuning asymmetry as sub-field standard practice; note it means the reported Δ may partially reflect hyperparameter optimization advantage.

**Top-k off-policy bias (topk_offpolicy_bias panel — incomplete):** 3–1 split. technical-soundness, domain-expert-nlp, domain-expert-rl: compounding optimistic bias is a serious concern. experimental-rigor: sub-field standard, minor completeness gap. No cross-examination. Recommendation: present as "concerning anomaly with charitable explanation" — bias is real, multi-dataset consistency provides non-falsifying evidence.

**OPRO baseline (opro_baseline panel — not executed):** No panelist filings. StablePrompt is primary baseline; OPRO referenced but not head-to-head compared. Author rebuttal does not address this gap.

## Lens 1 — Scientific Peer Reviewer (Lead's Integrated Reading)

**Soundness (1–4): 3** — The VarGrad objective (Eq. 4) and ELBO decomposition (Eq. 6) are mathematically correct. The posterior inference framing is well-formulated as Bayesian inference. Primary concerns: (1) The A_D(z) substitution for p(D|z) in the reward breaks the strict Bayesian interpretation — the "posterior" is a pseudo-posterior. Authors provide a principled defense: for exact-match tasks (II), A_D(z) equals the evaluation metric; for QA, it measures correct-answer token match. This is reasonable but the correlation validation covers only 6 classification tasks. (2) ε parameter unspecified (authors now disclose ε=1.0 in rebuttal — addresses reproducibility). (3) GFlowNet-EM (ICML 2023) is an unacknowledged methodological ancestor for the E-step/M-step interpretation.

**Presentation (1–4): 3** — The two-step decomposition is clearly labeled. The meta-prompt template figure is best-practice. Broken \cref{alg:gfnpo_full} is a LaTeX compilation issue (algorithm block exists in commented-out source, per author rebuttal) — this is a fixable presentation gap, not a methodological gap. M notation overloading is a legitimate clarity concern the authors commit to fixing with a notation table.

**Originality (1–4): 3** — First application of GFlowNets to LM prompt optimization (verified against arXiv; PAG is text-to-image, not LM). DMU is a novel training-free meta-prompt adaptation heuristic. Posterior inference framing inherited from StablePrompt; VarGrad is prior art; off-policy GFlowNets with replay are not new in the GFlowNet literature. Contribution is the specific combination applied to this problem.

**Significance (1–4): 3** — Meaningful algorithmic advance over StablePrompt with consistent gains across a wide benchmark suite. The 7.25% gain on Instruction Induction is notable. Sample-efficiency story (Figure 3) is well-supported. However, two compounding upward biases qualify the magnitude: (a) GFlowPO's temperature was tuned (range [0.5, 2.0]) while baselines were taken as-is from their original papers; (b) top-K buffer selection evaluates on the same test set used for prompt search, creating optimistic bias. The claim-auditor's cross-axis analysis estimates the true improvement margin over StablePrompt may be narrower than the headline 2.3pp average. This does not falsify the contribution but warrants the "High (qualified)" significance label.

**Overall (1–6): 4** — Weak Accept. The paper demonstrates a genuine advance in prompt optimization methodology. The VarGrad/ELBO core is mathematically sound, the empirical design is comprehensive (2×2 factorial ablation, multi-domain benchmarks, 3-seed reporting on main tables), and the first GFlowNet+LM prompt optimization claim is verified. The primary weaknesses — theoretically unmotivated likelihood substitution with narrow validation, single-run ablation tables, no public code, and compounding upward bias in the evaluation — are material but do not collectively falsify the headline claims. They warrant revision requests, not rejection.

**The single change that would most strengthen the paper:** Add multi-seed statistics to ablation Tables 4–5 (or explicitly label them as N=1 exploratory) and extend the A_D(z) correlation analysis to II and QA tasks.

## Lens 2 — Archaeologist (Prior Work)

**Older anchor:** StablePrompt (Kwon et al., EMNLP 2024) — direct baseline using on-policy PPO with the same posterior inference framing and meta-prompt template (minus reference prompts). GFlowPO inherits the framing but replaces PPO with GFlowNet VarGrad and adds DMU. Delta: off-policy training + DMU vs. on-policy PPO with fixed meta-prompt.

**Newer/concurrent neighbor:** PAG (Yun et al., arXiv:2502.11477, Feb 2025) — only other GFlowNet+prompt work, applied to text-to-image diffusion models. GFlowPO is in a fundamentally different problem space (LM prompt optimization for downstream task performance vs. image-prompt postfix generation). The "first GFlowNet-based LM prompt optimization" claim is verified narrow against PAG.

**Unacknowledged ancestor:** GFlowNet-EM (Hu et al., ICML 2023) provides the methodological template for the E-step/M-step interpretation. GFlowPO's Step-A ≈ E-step, Step-B ≈ M-step, but with a heuristic M-step instantiation (reference-prompt injection) rather than the gradient-based updates GFlowNet-EM would perform. This connection is not in the related work section.

**GFlowNet lineage:** Bengio et al. 2021 (origin), GFlowNet Foundations JMLR 2023, VarGrad NeurIPS 2020 — all correctly cited.

## Lens 3 — Academic Researcher (Forward Leverage)

Most concrete non-trivial follow-up: apply GFlowPO's off-policy GFlowNet training to **reasoning-intensive tasks** (MATH, GSM8K), where the prompt search space is larger and sample efficiency matters more. The paper's own limitations section admits the exclusion of chain-of-thought tasks. If GFlowPO's +7.25% gain on Instruction Induction generalizes to CoT prompt search, it would be significant over current methods relying on process reward models or verifier-based search. This follow-up materially depends on GFlowPO because it requires the off-policy replay mechanism for sparse-reward, large-search-space settings.

Second follow-up: extend the DMU mechanism to **cross-task generalization** — using the high-reward prompt buffer to initialize prompt search on related tasks, doing meta-learning across the prompt optimization process.

Third follow-up (from claim-auditor's cross-axis finding): characterize the **theoretical convergence properties** of the A_D(z) substitution under reward misspecification — does maximizing training accuracy under the reference-LM prior provably lead to prompts that generalize, or is there a gap? This is the most important open theoretical question the paper leaves.

## Lens 4 — Industry Practitioner (Deployment)

**Concrete deployment scenario:** A practitioner with a domain-specific classification task (legal document categorization, medical text classification) needing to optimize few-shot prompts for Mistral-7B or Llama3-8B with ~10 hours of H100-time budget. GFlowPO is accessible: single H100, ~1 hour per run, 3 seeds = 3 hours per task.

**Blockers:**
1. **No public code** — paper is anonymous; code may follow acceptance. Claims unverifiable today.
2. **ε parameter** — authors now disclose ε=1.0 (rebuttal), addressing the reproducibility gap.
3. **DMU hyperparameters (kb=2, kq=1) not ablated** — potential sensitivity means additional grid search may be needed.
4. **Reasoning tasks excluded** — most production use cases involve multi-step reasoning.
5. **Compounding evaluation bias** — top-K selection on same test set used for search may inflate reported numbers; true improvement margin over baselines may be narrower.

**Verdict:** GFlowPO is promising for academic research; reproducibility is partially addressed by author rebuttal but public code is needed for practitioner adoption.

## Integrated Verdict

GFlowPO is a solid empirical contribution to the prompt optimization literature. Its core innovation — combining off-policy GFlowNet training with a training-free Dynamic Memory Update mechanism — demonstrates meaningful sample-efficiency gains over StablePrompt (+7.25% on Instruction Induction) with consistent improvements across text classification, instruction induction, and QA benchmarks. The VarGrad objective and ELBO decomposition are mathematically sound; the posterior inference framing is legitimate Bayesian inference; the empirical design is comprehensive with a well-structured 2×2 factorial ablation. The paper's "first GFlowNet+LM prompt optimization" claim is verified against arXiv.

The primary weaknesses are: (1) the A_D(z) substitution is empirically motivated but theoretically unmotivated, with validation covering only 6 classification tasks while applied across QA and generation tasks; (2) the ablation tables report single-run results while the main tables report 3-seed statistics, creating an unverifiable claim for the two core novel contributions; (3) no public code or specified ε parameter (ε=1.0 now disclosed in author rebuttal); (4) two compounding upward biases in the evaluation (GFlowPO's hyperparameters tuned while baselines not; top-K selection on same test set used for search) suggest the true improvement margin over StablePrompt may be narrower than the headline numbers.

The author rebuttal is substantive and honest — conceding on MRPC/Translation acknowledgment, algorithm pseudocode fix, and A_D(z) correlation extension, while defending on ε monotonicity and algorithm block existence. The strong rebuttals (Items 2 and 5) and credible revision plans on Items 3 and 4 support maintaining the Weak Accept verdict with targeted revision requests.

These weaknesses are material but collectively do not falsify the headline claims. The paper is a **Weak Accept** for ICML 2026, with revision requests on: multi-seed ablation statistics, A_D(z) correlation analysis extension to II/QA tasks, explicit acknowledgment of MRPC/Translation regressions, and hyperparameter/compute disclosure.

## Specific, Actionable Feedback

1. **(§Approach, Eq. 3)** Specify ε = 1.0 in the reward definition A_D(z) = ε + Σ f(ŷ, y) and add an ablation over ε values (e.g., ε ∈ {0.1, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0}) to confirm the ranking robustness you claim in the rebuttal. While any positive ε produces the same ordering for |D| ≥ 32, this should be verified empirically rather than asserted.

2. **(§Approach, line 1)** Uncomment and compile the algorithmic pseudocode block (approach.tex lines 428–460), resolving the package conflict with tcolorbox, or remove the dangling \cref{alg:gfnpo_full} citation if space constraints require it. The algorithm block exists in the source — make it visible in the compiled PDF.

3. **(§Ablation Study, Tables 4–5)** Add multi-seed statistics (mean ± std across ≥3 seeds) for all ablation conditions. If multi-seed runs are not yet complete, explicitly label Tables 4–5 as "N=1 exploratory results" and qualify the contribution claims for off-policy training and DMU accordingly. The 5.8pp DMU gain is the paper's primary evidence for its two novel components — it must be statistically verifiable.

4. **(§Approach, Appendix §A)** Extend the training-likelihood-to-test-accuracy correlation analysis to cover at least 3 Instruction Induction tasks and 2 QA tasks. The authors' rebuttal argument (A_D(z) = evaluation metric for exact-match; same token-match signal for QA) is principled but requires empirical validation beyond the 6 classification datasets currently shown.

5. **(§Experiments)** Add explicit acknowledgment that GFlowPO underperforms StablePrompt on MRPC (69.6 ± 4.2 vs 71.3 ± 3.4) and underperforms ProTeGi on Translation tasks (52.58 ± 2.01 vs 56.25). The current "consistently outperforms" framing is inaccurate. Add a "Known task-specific limitations" paragraph.

6. **(§Approach, notation)** Add a notation table before §Approach clarifying the three distinct uses of M (meta-prompt template, instantiated meta-prompt, updated meta-prompt) and the iteration convention (M_t → M_{t+1}). This resolves the meta-prompt symbol overloading.

7. **(§Experiments, compute)** Add a footnote to Table 1 stating: "GFlowPO uses approximately 1 hour on a single H100 GPU (3 seeds = 3 hours total). StablePrompt and APE wall-clock times are not publicly reported in their respective papers." This honestly flags the information gap the authors identified in the rebuttal.

8. **(§Related Work)** Add GFlowNet-EM (Hu et al., ICML 2023) to the related work section. It is the methodological template for the E-step/M-step interpretation and its absence from the genealogy is a notable gap given the paper's heavy reliance on that framing.

## Questions for Authors

1. What is the empirical basis for the A_D(z) substitution on Instruction Induction and QA tasks? The current correlation analysis covers only 6 classification tasks. You argue that for exact-match tasks A_D(z) equals the evaluation metric, but did you verify this empirically before applying it to II/QA?

2. The ablation experiments (Tables 4–5) are single-run. What was the primary reason for not running them with multiple seeds? Was it compute budget, or was the exploratory nature intentional? If the latter, would you now be willing to run them with 3 seeds and report the results?

3. The top-K buffer selection evaluates on the same test set used for prompt search. Did you consider a held-out validation set for buffer selection and a separate test set for final evaluation? If not, what is your assessment of the magnitude of this optimistic bias on the reported numbers?

## Adversarial Audit Trail (R4)

The adversarial subagent's strongest counter-argument concerns the **foundational inconsistency** in the Bayesian framing: the paper claims p(z|D,M) ∝ p(D|z)·p_ref(z|M) as a posterior but substitutes A_D(z) for p(D|z) in the reward, destroying the Bayesian interpretation. The lead reviewer notes: this is a genuine theoretical tension, not a fatal flaw. The paper explicitly characterizes the result as a "pseudo-posterior" in the appendix, acknowledging the approximation. The VarGrad/ELBO machinery still operates on the pseudo-posterior — the mathematical structure is sound even if the Bayesian interpretation is loose. The authors' rebuttal did not fully resolve this tension but provided a reasonable defense.

The adversarial subagent also raised **single-run ablations** and **"consistent improvements" overclaimed** as serious concerns. These are valid. The lead reviewer agrees these are material gaps that warrant revision requests but does not elevate them to rejection grounds: the main results tables have proper 3-seed statistics, the ablation design is methodologically sound (2×2 factorial), and the author rebuttal commits to multi-seed ablation runs and explicit acknowledgment of regression cases.

The **compounding upward bias** concern (tuning asymmetry + top-K selection) was not raised by the adversarial subagent but emerged from the claim-auditor's cross-axis analysis. It is the most novel adverse finding and the one the authors did not address in the rebuttal. It qualifies the magnitude of the reported improvement but does not falsify the directional claim that GFlowPO outperforms StablePrompt.

**Residual risk that the verdict is wrong:** Low-to-moderate. The core algorithmic contribution (off-policy GFlowNet + DMU) is verifiable from the paper text. The empirical gains are broadly consistent across 6 classification datasets, 24 II tasks, and 18 BBII tasks. The main residual risk is that the true improvement margin over StablePrompt is narrower than reported due to compounding evaluation bias — but the directional claim survives even a 1-2pp reduction in the average gain.
