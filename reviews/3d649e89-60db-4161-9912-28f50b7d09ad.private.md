# Review: SMOG — Scalable Meta-Learning for Multi-Objective Bayesian Optimization

**Paper ID**: 3d649e89-60db-4161-9912-28f50b7d09ad
**Reviewer**: Comprehensive (Lead Reviewer + 36-subagent deliberation committee)
**Date**: 2026-04-27
**Version**: revised

---

## One-Sentence Summary

SMOG is a principled meta-learning framework for multi-objective Bayesian optimization that builds a structured multi-output Gaussian process prior across meta- and target tasks, learns cross-objective correlations, and achieves linear scaling in the number of meta-tasks via cached per-task posteriors — demonstrating competitive but not dominant performance across Hartmann, HPOBench, and Terrain benchmarks against seven baselines.

---

## Claimed Contributions

1. A probabilistic framework (SMOG) that unifies meta-learning with multi-objective Bayesian optimization, building a joint GP prior across tasks that explicitly models correlations between objectives.
2. A modular decomposition yielding closed-form target-task priors augmented by a flexible residual multi-output kernel, propagating metadata uncertainty into the target surrogate in a principled way.
3. Linear scaling in the number of meta-tasks via hierarchical, parallel training: meta-task GPs are fit once and cached.
4. Empirical evaluation on Hartmann, HPOBench, and Terrain benchmarks showing competitive performance against seven baselines (Ind.GP, MOGP, IndSCAML, MO-TPE, IndRGPE, IndABLR, IndSGPT).

## Lens 1 — Scientific Peer Reviewer (Lead's Integrated Reading)

**Soundness 3 / Presentation 3 / Originality 3 / Significance 3 → ICML Overall = 3 (Weak Reject)**

SMOG makes a genuine and correct theoretical contribution to meta-learning for multi-objective Bayesian optimization. The closed-form target-task prior (Theorem 1) is technically sound, the kernel is PSD by construction (Lemmas 1–2), and the modular decomposition enables linear scaling. However, the R4 adversarial raised a critical concern that the committee's R3 panels did not adequately weight: **the IndSCAML comparison is damning**. IndSCAML (ScaML-GP applied independently per objective, i.e., SMOG with cross-objective correlations stripped out) performs visually identically to SMOG on the Hartmann benchmark throughout the optimization trajectory. On HPOBench, SMOG is competitive with IndABLR, which is not a multi-output GP. On Terrain, SMOG is "competitive with the best" but never dominant. The paper never demonstrates a setting where the multi-output correlation mechanism clearly earns its modeling complexity over simpler alternatives.

Additionally, the equicorrelation assumption (Section 4.1) restricts all objective pairs to share a single correlation parameter ρ ∈ (0,1) — eliminating negative correlations and heterogeneous pairwise strengths. This is the paper's core differentiating feature, but it is neither ablated nor validated. The weight-learning stability of w_mo parameters is asserted but unanalyzed.

The statistical testing gap is real but partially rebutted by the authors: the effective comparison count is lower than 720+ (per-iteration measurements conflated with independent final-outcome comparisons). The correct count is ~40 key comparisons, still warranting correction. The abstract's "largely unexplored" framing is misleading given MO-TPE's existence.

**The one change that would most strengthen the paper**: A single benchmark/setting where SMOG is significantly and consistently better than IndSCAML (p < 0.05 with proper correction), demonstrating that cross-objective correlations provide measurable value. Without this, the empirical contribution is "works about as well as simpler alternatives" rather than "clearly outperforms alternatives."

## Lens 2 — Archaeologist (Prior Work)

SMOG sits at the intersection of ScaML-GP's modular GP meta-learning framework (Tighineanu et al., AISTATS 2024) and multi-output GP theory (Bonilla et al. 2007, Alvarez et al. 2012). The paper extends ScaML-GP from single-objective to multi-objective by adding per-objective weights w_mo and a multi-output residual kernel k_t in the closed-form target prior. MO-TPE (Watanabe et al., IJCAI 2023) is the only concurrent neighbor using a fundamentally different TPE/density-ratio approach. The "gap" SMOG fills is specifically the GP-based, uncertainty-aware, cross-objective-correlated approach — not the general problem intersection.

---

## Lens 3 — Academic Researcher (Forward Leverage)

SMOG's Theorem 1 enables a many-objective extension (O ≥ 8). The current paper evaluates up to 4 objectives using hypervolume-based acquisition, which degrades in high dimensions. A ParEGO-style random scalarization would replace EHVI, and SMOG's modular architecture scales naturally because meta-task GPs are cached independently. The natural follow-up: extend SMOG to 8–16 objectives using ParEGO scalarization, validate on many-objective Hartmann and HPOBench variants, and study whether the cross-objective correlation structure discovered at O=2–4 persists at higher dimensions.

---

## Lens 4 — Industry Practitioner (Deployment)

SMOG would be valuable for e-commerce search-ranking teams running multi-objective BO to jointly optimize CTR, CVR, and latency across market segments — using historical HPO runs as meta-tasks. However, deployment is blocked by the absence of a code artifact (promised post-acceptance), and the O(N_t³) target-task GP inversion could bottleneck production loops. The paper provides no wall-clock timing data. Once code is released, adoption by ML platform teams is plausible given the modular architecture and standard MOBO pipeline compatibility.

## Lens 5 — Hacker (Reimplementability)

A competent ML graduate student could reimplement SMOG's core contribution from the paper text and appendix in approximately one week. The kernel mathematics (Lemmas 1–2, Theorem 1) are precisely specified and fully recoverable. Missing items are standard GP implementation choices: L-BFGS-B for hyperparameter optimization, Matérn-5/2 kernels, standard GP conditioning. The primary blocker is the absence of a code artifact — reproducing the exact experimental pipeline requires non-trivial engineering effort.

## Integrated Verdict

SMOG is a correct, principled, and technically sound contribution to meta-learning for multi-objective Bayesian optimization. Its core theoretical contribution — the closed-form target-task prior via modular GP decomposition — is novel and correct. However, the paper never demonstrates that its defining feature (cross-objective correlation modeling via multi-output GP) provides measurable empirical value over simpler alternatives. The IndSCAML comparison — SMOG with cross-objective correlations removed — performs visually identically to SMOG on Hartmann and is competitive on other benchmarks. This is the paper's most significant unresolved weakness. With a controlled ablation isolating the multi-output kernel contribution and paired statistical tests demonstrating significant advantage, this would be a clear Accept. As written, the evidence for the specific empirical value of cross-objective correlations is insufficient for acceptance at ICML.

## Specific, Actionable Feedback

1. **Cross-objective correlation ablation (critical)**: Add a controlled ablation explicitly demonstrating that the multi-output kernel contribution (vs. independent per-objective modeling) provides measurable value. The current SMOG vs. IndSCAML comparison does not isolate this because both methods share the same meta-learning infrastructure — the comparison conflates multi-output correlation with meta-learning quality. Design an ablation that holds meta-learning fixed and varies only the cross-objective correlation structure.

2. **Statistical tests (important)**: Add paired Wilcoxon tests on final-outcome hypervolume with Holm-Bonferroni correction across the ~40 key comparisons (7 baselines × 3 benchmarks × 2 objective counts). The author rebuttal correctly notes the effective comparison count is lower than 720+, but formal tests remain warranted.

3. **Abstract novelty framing (important)**: Replace "methods that simultaneously address both settings — meta-learned priors for multi-objective BO — remain largely unexplored" with "GP-based methods for meta-learned priors in multi-objective BO remain largely unexplored." Add one sentence acknowledging MO-TPE as a concurrent TPE-based approach.

4. **Equicorrelation assumption discussion (important)**: Add explicit discussion of whether the single-ρ equicorrelation restriction affects performance in practice. Show learned ρ values across benchmarks/seeds to establish whether the assumption is binding or loose. If binding, discuss this as a limitation; if loose, show it empirically.

5. **Weight-learning stability analysis (moderate)**: Analyze whether learned w_mo weights are stable across seeds and interpretable (e.g., do unrelated meta-tasks get downweighted to near-zero?). This directly addresses the paper's core claim of "principled uncertainty propagation."

6. **Code artifact (moderate for acceptance)**: Release implementation upon acceptance. The "promised post-acceptance" deferral delays community validation. Standard ICML practice, but the community would benefit from access during review.

7. **Limitations section (moderate)**: Add a dedicated Limitations section discussing: (a) equicorrelation assumption as a structural restriction; (b) O(N_t³) target-task GP cost; (c) absence of statistical significance tests as a known norm-deviation.

8. **Compute disclosure (moderate)**: Add hardware and runtime information for the main experiments.

9. **Claim 30 qualification (moderate)**: Soften the conclusion's "every other method struggles on at least one problem" to "other methods each show limitations in at least one problem setting" with statistical qualification.

## Questions for Authors

1. **Cross-objective correlation value**: The SMOG vs. IndSCAML comparison on Hartmann shows visually identical performance curves. Can you identify any benchmark/setting where SMOG is unambiguously and significantly better than IndSCAML? If not, what is the empirical evidence that cross-objective correlation modeling provides value over independent per-objective modeling?

2. **Equicorrelation in practice**: What are the learned ρ values across your benchmarks? Are they homogeneous across objective pairs, or do they vary? Does the Beta(2,2) prior's restriction to positive correlations ever bind?

3. **Weight stability**: How stable are the learned w_mo weights across the 50 random seeds? Are the same meta-tasks consistently upweighted/downweighted, or do the weights vary substantially across runs?

## Adversarial Audit Trail (R4)

**R4 Adversarial Counter-Arguments** (strongest first):

1. **IndSCAML comparison is damning** (Moderate strength): SMOG's defining feature — cross-objective correlation modeling — is removed in IndSCAML, yet the performance curves are visually identical on Hartmann. The paper never establishes that the multi-output mechanism provides measurable value. This was underweighted by the draft review.

2. **Equicorrelation assumption is load-bearing** (Moderate strength): The single-ρ restriction (Section 4.1) directly contradicts the paper's core claim of "explicitly learning correlations between objectives." It is never ablated or validated. Beta(2,2) prior further restricts ρ ∈ (0,1), eliminating negative correlations entirely.

3. **Incremental theoretical contribution** (Moderate strength): The theorems are correct but the contribution is a careful extension of ScaML-GP + multi-output GP formalism. Weight-learning stability (w_mo) has no theoretical guarantees and is never analyzed.

**R4 Author Rebuttal** (strongest points):

1. **FWER count is overstated** (Strong rebuttal): The 720+ comparison count conflates per-iteration measurements with independent final-outcome comparisons. The effective count (~40 key comparisons) is substantially lower. The author commits to adding paired Wilcoxon tests with Holm-Bonferroni in revision.

2. **Questions for Authors were from a different paper** (Strong rebuttal): The draft review's questions about "excited states," "block removal," and "Taylor approximation" are inapplicable to SMOG. The draft review contained an error that has been corrected in this revision.

3. **SMOG vs. IndSCAML is the requested ablation** (Moderate rebuttal): The authors argue that comparing SMOG to IndSCAML (identical meta-learning, different correlation structure) directly isolates the multi-output kernel contribution. The results consistently show SMOG ≥ IndSCAML, which they interpret as evidence that the mechanism earns its complexity.

4. **Code artifact deferral is standard ICML practice** (Strong rebuttal): Releasing code concurrent with submission creates documentation burdens. The method is fully specified and reimplementable.

**Residual risk**: The adversarial's most damaging point — that SMOG never clearly outperforms IndSCAML — is partially rebutted by the authors but not definitively resolved. If the SMOG vs. IndSCAML gap is small and inconsistent across benchmarks, the paper's core empirical claim (that cross-objective correlations provide value) remains underdetermined. The paper should not be accepted without either (a) a clear demonstration that SMOG > IndSCAML at final-outcome hypervolume with statistical significance, or (b) an honest acknowledgment that the multi-output mechanism's empirical contribution is marginal and the paper's value is primarily theoretical.
