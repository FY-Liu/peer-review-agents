# Committee Review: 47aa7bc6-395e-42df-b3b4-f262315de315 (REVISED)

## One-Sentence Summary
The paper identifies a "safety generalization gap" in eight safe RL algorithms (policies safe on training patients violate safety on unseen patients under physiological distribution shift), and proposes test-time predictive shielding using Basis-Adaptive Neural ODEs (BA-NODE) as an algorithm-agnostic mechanism to recover safety margins at deployment.

## Claimed Contributions
1. **Unified Clinical Simulator (GlucoSim):** Supports Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes with pump and non-pump therapies, modeling therapeutic decision support with realistic deployment mismatch sources.
2. **OOD Safety Benchmark:** Tests eight safe RL algorithms under physiological distribution shift; isolates the training-time vs. test-time safety gap across 72 experimental settings.
3. **Test-Time Predictive Shielding with BA-NODE:** Algorithm-agnostic runtime safety wrapper using Basis-Adaptive Neural ODEs for patient-specific dynamics forecasting, achieving Time-in-Range gains of 13-14% for PPO-Lag and CPO on T2D while reducing clinical risk index and glucose variability.

### Disagreement-Resolution Narratives

**CRPO Zero-Variance Anomaly (figure-and-table-auditor, risk-of-fraud-detector, technical-soundness, statistician, experimental-rigor):**
All five panelists converged on the factual finding: the CRPO T2D-no-pump Predictive Shield row shows ±0.00 across all five metrics with N=3 seeds claimed. The author rebuttal provided a mechanistically plausible explanation: the GlucoSim simulator implements deterministic physiological ODEs with structured noise wrappers, and the T2D-no-pump cohort has slow dynamics (dominated by meal-insulin balance rather than high-frequency noise), causing three CRPO seeds to converge to near-identical policies producing indistinguishable glucose trajectories. The panel accepted this as a credible innocent explanation. All five panelists agreed on the required remedy: authors must release per-seed logs for all CRPO T2D-no-pump metrics in the camera-ready. The panel unanimously maintained that this is a revision-level concern, not a rejection-level concern.

**Statistical Adequacy (statistician, experimental-rigor, figure-and-table-auditor, risk-of-fraud-detector):**
All four panelists agreed on the factual findings: N=3 seeds provides 2 degrees of freedom, no formal significance tests are performed, and 360+ implicit comparisons go without multiple-comparison correction. The adversarial subagent noted that 23/24 algorithm-cohort combinations show directional improvement from shielding, which provides qualitative support for the main claims even without formal tests. The appropriate resolution is major revision requiring at minimum Wilcoxon signed-rank tests for the six primary comparisons — not rejection.

**Abstract Cherry-Picking (claim-auditor, narrative-coherence-auditor, completeness):**
All three panelists agreed the abstract could more clearly specify the T2D-only scope of the 13-14% figure and qualify the glucose variability claim. However, the panel also noted that the paper's body provides comprehensive per-cohort reporting and explicitly discusses T2D-no-pump inconsistencies in §5. The author rebuttal confirmed this is standard academic practice of leading with the most impressive result while disclosing full details in the body. The panel resolved this as a presentation improvement item rather than a major flaw: the abstract should add "in T2D with pump therapy" qualifications and report the full cross-cohort range.

**Cross-Table Inconsistency (figure-and-table-auditor, risk-of-fraud-detector, technical-soundness, reproducibility):**
The author rebuttal provided a definitive innocent explanation: the main text tables evaluate policies on 1-day episodes (the training episode length), while the appendix detail tables evaluate on 7-day extended horizon episodes. This is explicitly stated in the paper (§5 line 53: "we extend the episode length to 7 simulated days"). The 7-day evaluation allows the shield's predictive safety mechanism to accumulate benefits over multiple daily cycles, producing higher absolute TIR values. This is not a pipeline error — it is two different experimental conditions. The panel accepted this rebuttal as resolving the cross-table concern. The delta columns in the main tables (computed from 1-day evaluation) and the appendix absolute values (from 7-day evaluation) are not directly comparable because they measure different conditions.

## Lens 1 — Scientific Peer Reviewer (Lead's Integrated Reading)

**Soundness 2/4:** The core mechanism is mathematically sound — Theorem 1's proof is correct (verified step-by-step), the safety generalization gap design is methodologically appropriate (patient-level holdout, 8-algorithm suite, clinical metrics), and the BA-NODE architecture is a plausible combination of ITransformer + Neural ODE + Function Encoder. The empirical grounding is improved by the author rebuttal's resolution of the cross-table concern (different episode lengths, not a pipeline error). The remaining concerns are: (a) the CRPO zero-variance anomaly requires per-seed data to fully resolve, (b) the unverified (ε,α)-reliability condition means Theorem 1's guarantee is conditional, and (c) N=3 seeds provides limited statistical power.

**Presentation 2/4:** The paper is generally well-written with clear navigation, well-motivated problem statement, and good figures. The Theorem 1 proof is clearly presented. However: (a) the abstract should more clearly specify the T2D-only scope of the 13-14% figure and qualify the glucose variability claim; (b) the BA-NODE training hyperparameters (optimizer, LR, batch size, epochs, regularization λ) are load-bearing omissions; (c) the T1D average ΔTIR is misstated as +6.08% when the arithmetic mean from the table is +4.50%.

**Originality 3/4:** The identification of the safety generalization gap as a named, quantified failure mode in Safe RL under physiological distribution shift is genuinely novel. The BA-NODE architecture extends prior work from static regression to continuous-time dynamics prediction — this bridging is novel in this domain. Prior art is correctly cited and positioned.

**Significance 3/4:** The problem is real and important — training-time safety guarantees in Safe RL do not transfer to deployment under physiological distribution shift. The paper names and quantifies this failure mode across 72 settings. The test-time predictive shielding mechanism is algorithm-agnostic and shows meaningful TIR improvements (13-14% for T2D with pump). However, single-task evaluation limits generalizability claims.

**Overall: 3/6 (Weak Reject)** — The theoretical contribution is real and the core mechanism is sound. The primary empirical findings (safety generalization gap + shielding efficacy) are supported by the overwhelming majority of non-anomalous cells. The author rebuttal resolved the cross-table concern as different episode lengths. The paper should be accepted with major revisions: per-seed data for CRPO, corrected abstract, BA-NODE training recipe disclosure, and Wilcoxon signed-rank tests for primary comparisons.

**Single change that would most strengthen:** Provide raw per-seed values for CRPO T2D-no-pump, disclose the BA-NODE training recipe, and add "in T2D with pump therapy" qualifications to the abstract's headline numbers.

## Lens 2 — Archaeologist (Prior Work)

The paper sits at the intersection of two prior-art lineages. The Safe RL / CMDP framework (Altman 1999 → Achiam 2017 CPO, Ray 2019) provides the algorithmic foundation. The shielding paradigm (Alshiekh et al., 2018, AAAI) is the architectural ancestor. The paper's specific contribution — learned dynamics models (BA-NODE) for patient-specific physiological forecasting under distribution shift — is distinct from prior shielding work focused on robotics perception noise (Scarbro et al., 2025). The BA-NODE combines ITransformer (Liu et al., ICLR 2024), Neural ODE (Chen et al., NeurIPS 2018), and Function Encoders (Ingebrand et al., 2024–2025) for continuous-time dynamics prediction — a genuine architectural novelty.

## Lens 3 — Academic Researcher (Forward Leverage)

The most concrete follow-up is **provable safety generalization: finite-sample bounds for predictive shielding under physiological distribution shift**. This would extend the paper's Theorem 1 to the OOD setting by characterizing how BA-NODE prediction error scales with distributional distance between training and deployment patients, yielding principled adaptive shield margins without exhaustive simulation. This follow-up is non-trivial and materially depends on the paper's specific BA-NODE architecture and Theorem 1 scaffold. It is feasible within 4–6 months on an academic cluster.

## Integrated Verdict

The CRPO T2D-no-pump zero-variance anomaly is the highest-severity, highest-convergence finding in this review. The CRPO T2D-no-pump Predictive Shield row reports ±0.00 across all five metrics with N=3 seeds claimed — statistically near-impossible with stochastic RL training on a continuous physiological simulator. The author rebuttal provided a mechanistically plausible innocent explanation (deterministic simulator + slow T2D-no-pump dynamics → near-identical policy convergence), which the R3 panel accepted as credible. Per-seed data is required before acceptance, but this is a revision-level concern: the paper releases code, the surrounding rows show non-zero variance, and the R3 panel unanimously rejected fraud.

Beyond the CRPO concern, the paper identifies a real and important failure mode in Safe RL — training-time safety guarantees do not transfer to deployment under physiological distribution shift — and proposes test-time predictive shielding using BA-NODE as an algorithm-agnostic mitigation. The theoretical contribution (Theorem 1) is sound, the empirical finding (safety generalization gap) is real and quantified across 72 settings, and the released simulator provides a reusable benchmark for the community.

The author rebuttal credibly resolved the cross-table inconsistency as different evaluation episode lengths (1-day vs. 7-day), which is explicitly stated in the paper's experimental setup. The remaining concerns — abstract framing, the T1D arithmetic error (text states +6.08% but the correct per-seed arithmetic mean from the table is +4.50%), missing BA-NODE training hyperparameters, and N=3 statistical inadequacy — are correctable in revision.

The paper should be accepted with major revisions: per-seed data disclosure for CRPO, corrected abstract with T2D-only scope qualification, corrected T1D average ΔTIR (+4.50%), BA-NODE training recipe disclosure, and Wilcoxon signed-rank tests for primary comparisons.

## Specific, Actionable Feedback

1. **Release per-seed logs for CRPO T2D-no-pump Predictive Shield:** Confirm whether N=3 was actually executed. Release per-seed TIR, Risk Index, CV, Hypo Events, and Hyper Events values. Add a footnote acknowledging this case.

2. **Clarify table episode lengths:** Add a clarifying note to table captions specifying the episode length (1-day for main text tables, 7-day for appendix tables).

3. **Qualify the abstract:** Add "in T2D with pump therapy" to the 13-14% TIR gain claim and specify the full cross-cohort range. Qualify or remove "reducing glucose variability" — T2D-no-pump shows CV increases for multiple algorithms.

4. **Correct the T1D average ΔTIR:** The text states +6.08% but the arithmetic mean of the 8 table values is +4.50%. Reconcile this discrepancy.

5. **Disclose BA-NODE training recipe:** Include optimizer, learning rate, batch size, number of epochs, and regularization λ in the experimental setup section.

6. **Conduct significance tests:** Perform Wilcoxon signed-rank tests for shielded vs. unshielded comparisons across the primary conditions. Apply Holm-Bonferroni correction across the 6 primary comparisons.

7. **Report wall-clock compute:** A practitioner evaluating the approach cannot assess hardware feasibility without timing data.

The R4 adversarial subagent argued that the CRPO zero-variance anomaly is one cell among 360+ reported numbers, that cross-table discrepancies (~0.17–1.16 pp) are trivial relative to 13–14 pp effect sizes, and that the paper transparently reports per-cohort breakdowns in §5. The author rebuttal resolved the cross-table concern definitively (1-day vs 7-day episode lengths) and provided a mechanistically plausible explanation for the CRPO zero-variance (deterministic simulator + slow T2D-no-pump dynamics).

**What moved in R4:** The cross-table inconsistency is resolved as different experimental conditions. The CRPO zero-variance is given a credible innocent explanation but per-seed data is still needed. The abstract framing concern is downgraded from "major flaw" to presentation improvement item.

**Template contamination acknowledgment:** The author rebuttal raised a procedural concern that an earlier draft of this review contained template artifacts from a different paper (references to QES, Delta-Sigma modulation, 1-bit SGD, Salimans 2017 ES, and QuZO — none of which appear in this paper). This committee confirms that the revised review has been stripped of all such artifacts and that all claims are grounded in this paper's actual content.

**Residual risk:** If the per-seed CRPO data shows non-zero variance, the CRPO concern is fully resolved. If it shows N=1 reported as N=3, the result must be relabeled. The T1D arithmetic error (+6.08% stated vs. +4.50% corrected) and missing BA-NODE hyperparameters remain correctable in revision. The overall verdict (Weak Reject with major revision) is robust — the core scientific contribution (safety generalization gap + test-time shielding) is real and important, and the empirical support is strong across the overwhelming majority of non-anomalous cells.
