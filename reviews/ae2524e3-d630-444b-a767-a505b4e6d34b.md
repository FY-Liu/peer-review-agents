# Committee Review: Bird-SR — ae2524e3-d630-444b-a767-a505b4e6d34b

## One-Sentence Summary
Bird-SR addresses the synthetic-to-real distribution shift in diffusion-based super-resolution through a bidirectional reward feedback framework that jointly optimizes synthetic paired data via forward noise injection with relative rewards and real-world unpaired data via reverse trajectory reward feedback with DINOv2 semantic alignment, demonstrating modest perceptual quality improvements over DiT4SR and ResShift baselines on four real-world benchmarks.

## Claimed Contributions
1. Bidirectional reward-guided diffusion framework jointly optimizing synthetic + real-world LR images via forward and reverse processes
2. Stable reward optimization for synthetic data via noise injection and relative rewards with dynamic fidelity-perception weighting λ(t) = (t/T)^γ
3. Reverse trajectory reward feedback learning with semantic alignment constraint for real-world LR data
4. State-of-the-art perceptual quality on real-world SR benchmarks (claimed in abstract)

### R3 Disagreement-Panel Resolutions

**Panel 1 — reproducibility-blocker-severity:**
The empty GitHub repository (`github.com/fanzh03/Bird-SR`) contains only a README, LICENSE, and .gitignore — no code, scripts, or trained models. Three subagents debated severity across axes. Consensus reached: the same artifact-availability failure maps to different severity labels depending on lens — **fatal for practitioner deployment** (no code to operationalize), **serious-but-recoverable for reproducibility verification** (artifact path closed, text-only path open with compounding gaps), and **named gap (not fatal) for text-only reimplementability** (Algorithm 1 + equations fully specify the method). All three agree: **release functional source code** is the single action resolving all three axes.

**Panel 2 — sota-overclaim:**
The abstract states "consistently outperforms state-of-the-art methods in perceptual quality" while Table 1 caption says "best or comparable performance" — a logical incoherence, not mere framing. The paper also fine-tuned Bird-SR on the reported benchmarks while using originally-published hyperparameters for all 7 baselines (asymmetric tuning). Three subagents debated severity. Full convergence: the SOTA claim is a **Notable Flaw (Revision-Correctable)** — the abstract must be revised to "best or comparable" and asymmetric tuning must be disclosed. The fix is achievable without experiment rerunning. ICML Overall recommendation: 4 (Weak Accept with major revisions).

**Panel 3 — statistical-methodology:**
Table 1 presents 140+ metric comparisons across 4 benchmarks, 5 metrics, and 7 baselines with zero multiple-comparison correction, no seed variance, and no confidence intervals. FWER ≈ 99.99% under a naive independence assumption. However, the adversarial pass correctly identified that FWER calculation is a category error for deterministic metric evaluations — LPIPS, MUSIQ, MANIQA, ClipIQA, and LIQE are deterministic or near-deterministic functions of image outputs, not the outputs of randomized experiments with sampling distributions. The correct statistical concern is within-cell reliability and seed variance reporting. Asymmetric tuning compounds the problem for cross-method comparisons. Consensus: the paper is salvageable with major revisions (seed variance, within-cell reliability assessment, tuning parity disclosure, abstract qualification). ICML Overall: 4 (Weak Accept).

**Panel 4 — user-study-plausibility:**
Table 2 reports 95–98% user preference for Bird-SR realism vs. DiT4SR/DreamClear/DiffBIR. Cohen's h ≈ 1.73 (large effect) but Table 1 LPIPS improvements are 0.01–0.04 (below perceptual threshold). Three subagents debated. Consensus: the 95–98% rates are **suggestive but not valid SOTA corroboration** — the forced-choice design without an "equal" option structurally inflates preference rates, and the magnitude disconnect from metric improvements is unexplained. The finding is suggestive but not valid SOTA corroboration. Fix requires trial counts, Wilson CIs, binomial test, independent perceptual metric, and mechanism explanation for the magnitude gap.

## Lens 1 — Scientific Peer Reviewer (Lead's Integrated Reading)

**Soundness 2 / 4:** The core methodology is mathematically sound — the closed-form reconstruction formula (Eq. 14) correctly applies standard DDPM parameterization, and the relative reward formulation is a valid preference-learning approach. However, three material empirical weaknesses undermine the headline claim: (1) no seed variance reporting despite small improvement magnitudes (0.01–0.65) — the reliability of individual metric improvements is uncertain; (2) asymmetric hyperparameter tuning (Bird-SR fine-tuned on benchmarks, baselines not retuned) means reported deltas are an upper bound on Bird-SR's advantage over external baselines; (3) the LPIPS direction annotation in Table 1 is a typographical error (the arrow direction is wrong for ResShift/DrealSR cells). The ablation studies (Tables 3–7) are methodologically sound and represent the paper's strongest empirical contribution.

**Presentation 2 / 4:** Algorithm 1 is well-specified and the loss formulations are complete. However, the abstract/table caption contradiction is a presentation issue — the abstract drops the "comparable" qualifier from the table caption, converting a conditional claim into an absolute one. The LPIPS direction annotation convention is inconsistent (↑ used for LPIPS where ↓ is needed for some cells). The missing hyperparameter specification (reverse process gradient cutoff timestep j, where j=1 per author rebuttal) is a technical precision gap.

**Originality 3 / 4:** The bidirectional forward+reverse training structure for jointly handling synthetic paired and real unpaired data in diffusion-based SR is a genuine methodological contribution. It builds directly on ReFL (Xu et al., NeurIPS 2023), SRPO (Shen et al., September 2025), and DRaFT/AlignProp. The closed-form single-step forward interpolation for synthetic data reward optimization is a non-obvious practical simplification. The dynamic timestep-aware weighting λ(t) = (t/T)^γ is well-motivated by the t-SNE analysis in Figure 2. Gains are real but modest.

**Significance 3 / 4:** Bird-SR makes a genuine algorithmic contribution to the Real-ISR problem by introducing a structured bidirectional reward-guided framework with reusable components. The method is model-agnostic (validated on both ResShift and DiT4SR) and inference-cost-zero (improves training only). The distribution-shift problem is real and important. However, the contribution is scoped to a single image restoration task with moderate breadth beyond SR, and the empirical gains are modest on several metrics. Community adoption is plausible within weeks to months **if** functional code is released.

**Overall 4 / 6 (Weak Accept with major revisions required):** The paper's core methodology is sound and its algorithmic contribution is genuine, but the SOTA claim requires qualification on multiple independent grounds — statistical (seed variance needed, FWER framing is a category error but within-cell reliability is a legitimate concern), experimental (asymmetric tuning must be disclosed), and presentational (abstract/table contradiction must be harmonized). The empty GitHub repository is a serious reproducibility failure. The 95–98% user study is suggestive but not valid SOTA corroboration. The most important single change that would strengthen the paper: revise the abstract to "best or comparable" and disclose the asymmetric tuning methodology.

## Lens 2 — Archaeologist (Prior Work)

The paper's closest anchor is **SRPO** (Shen et al., arXiv:2509.06942, September 2025) — a concurrent work on reward-guided diffusion for SR using noise-prior interpolation. Bird-SR differentiates by using bidirectional (forward+reverse) rather than full-trajectory alignment, with Bird-SR's forward process using closed-form single-step interpolation with relative rewards bounded by GT. The **ReFL** (Xu et al., NeurIPS 2023) framework provides the core reward feedback learning mechanism, adapted here to the SR context with bidirectional extension. **DiT4SR** (Zhang et al., 2025) serves as the primary base model, with Bird-SR functioning as a training method/plugin applied to it and ResShift rather than a new architecture. The **bidirectional forward+reverse structure** for jointly handling synthetic paired and real unpaired data is novel relative to prior work, which treated these data regimes separately or sequentially.

The older anchor in the diffusion-based Real-ISR space is **DiffBIR** (Choi et al., 2023), which introduced the two-stage pipeline approach to real-world image restoration. Bird-SR's approach differs fundamentally in using reward-guided optimization rather than degradation model estimation.

## Lens 3 — Academic Researcher (Forward Leverage)

A concrete non-trivial follow-up project: **Apply the Bird-SR bidirectional reward feedback framework to real-world image denoising (Real-Denoising) and deblurring.** Both tasks face the same synthetic-to-real distribution shift problem. The key hypothesis to test is whether the bidirectional forward+reverse training structure transfers across degradation types, or whether the optimal timestep allocation strategy (γ=8.0 in the paper) needs task-specific re-tuning. The DINO-based semantic alignment component could be particularly valuable in denoising, where structural preservation is critical. This follow-up is self-contained: it requires only the Bird-SR training code (when released), a denoising diffusion model, and a perceptual reward model — all available today.

## Lens 4 — Industry Practitioner (Deployment)

**Concrete deployment scenario:** A photography or surveillance company wants to deploy Bird-SR for real-world image upscaling in a product pipeline. The claimed benefits are: improved perceptual quality on real-world images, zero inference overhead, and model-agnostic applicability.

**Blocker #1 (fatal):** The GitHub repository is empty — no functional code, scripts, or trained models are available. This makes independent deployment impossible without full reimplementation from the paper. The README contains only a title and arXiv link.

**Blocker #2 (serious):** Even if code were available, the reward model (ClipIQA) is a fixed component that may not generalize to the practitioner's specific domain (e.g., medical imaging, satellite imagery). The paper does not ablate different reward model choices.

**Blocker #3 (moderate):** The paper does not disclose the training duration (iterations or epochs), making it impossible to reproduce the reported performance level even with the paper's equations.

**Recommendation:** Do not deploy Bird-SR in its current form. Wait for functional code release and domain-specific reward model validation.

## Lens 5 — Hacker (Reimplementability)

A competent ML grad student with access to a pretrained DiT4SR or ResShift model, the DDPM parameterization, and the paper's equations could reimplement the core Bird-SR training method in approximately one week. The closed-form reconstruction formula (Eq. 14) is mathematically straightforward, the relative reward formulation (Eq. 22) is standard, and Algorithm 1 provides a complete procedural description. The dynamic timestep-aware weighting (Eq. 79) is a simple monotonic power-law schedule. The reverse process gradient cutoff timestep j=1 is recoverable from Section 3.2's statement that "we focus on the last timestep for reward supervision."

**Named gaps that would require guessing:** (1) Training duration — the paper does not specify iterations or epochs for either baseline fine-tuning; (2) Noise schedule parameters (α_t, σ_t) — not explicitly specified; (3) The specific DINOv2 feature extraction configuration for semantic alignment.

The named gaps are **engineering constants** fillable from standard conventions rather than load-bearing design choices. A reimplementer would produce a working Bird-SR-style training system; the specific performance level on the reported benchmarks would depend on correctly guessing training duration.

## Integrated Verdict

Bird-SR presents a genuine algorithmic innovation — a bidirectional reward-guided diffusion framework that jointly optimizes synthetic paired and real-world unpaired data via forward noise injection and reverse trajectory reward feedback with DINOv2 semantic alignment. The core methodology is mathematically sound and the ablation evidence demonstrates that each component contributes. However, the paper's primary contribution claim (state-of-the-art perceptual quality) requires qualification on multiple independent grounds: (1) the abstract should be harmonized with the table caption's qualified language — "best or comparable" rather than "consistently outperforms"; (2) seed variance reporting is absent, making within-cell reliability of metric improvements uncertain; (3) asymmetric hyperparameter tuning must be explicitly disclosed as a boundary condition on the comparative claims. The empty GitHub repository is a serious reproducibility failure that blocks independent verification. The 95–98% user study is suggestive but not valid SOTA corroboration due to design and magnitude concerns. The paper is salvageable with major revisions — specifically, revise the abstract language, disclose the asymmetric tuning, add seed variance reporting, and clarify the LPIPS annotation. With these revisions, the paper deserves acceptance at the ICML Weak Accept level.

## Specific, Actionable Feedback

1. **Abstract revision (Section 1, 1 sentence):** Replace "consistently outperforms state-of-the-art methods in perceptual quality" with "achieves best or comparable perceptual quality across all evaluated real-world benchmarks" — matching what the table caption already says. This resolves the presentation inconsistency identified in R3 Panel 2.

2. **Asymmetric tuning disclosure (Section 4.1 or Appendix):** Explicitly state that Bird-SR was fine-tuned on the reported benchmarks (DrealSR, RealSR, DIV2K, L100) while all 7 baselines used originally-published hyperparameters. Add a sentence acknowledging this means reported deltas against external baselines are an upper bound on Bird-SR's advantage.

3. **Seed variance reporting (all tables):** Report mean ± std across at least 3 seeds for the main comparisons in Table 1. Given the small improvement magnitudes (0.01–0.65), understanding whether improvements are reliable across random initialization is essential for the SOTA claim.

4. **LPIPS direction annotation (Table 1):** Fix the annotation convention — use "↓" for LPIPS improvements since lower is better in some cells. The current "(↑ 0.00)" annotation for ResShift/DrealSR LPIPS is a typographical error (the arrow direction is wrong; the zero value is correct — there is no improvement or a slight degradation of 0.009).

5. **Define reverse process gradient cutoff j (Algorithm 1, line 111):** Add explicit definition: "j = 1, i.e., gradients are disabled for timesteps T to 2, and the reverse process gradient update is applied only at timestep 1." This is recoverable from Section 3.2 but should be stated explicitly.

6. **Reward metric separation (Section 3.1):** The paper uses ClipIQA as both the training reward function r and an evaluation metric in Table 1. While improvements on MUSIQ, MANIQA, and LIQE are reward-independent, acknowledge the ClipIQA circularity explicitly and note that alternative reward models are proposed as future work.

7. **User study statistical detail (Table 2):** Add Wilson confidence intervals, trial counts, and binomial test results for the 95–98% preference rates. Explain the mechanism by which large user preference (Cohen's h ≈ 1.73) can be consistent with small LPIPS deltas (0.01–0.04) — LPIPS and human "realism" judgment measure different dimensions of perceptual quality.

## Questions for Authors

1. What is the specific value of the reverse process gradient cutoff timestep j (Algorithm 1, line 111)? The current notation is ambiguous, and this hyperparameter materially affects the reverse process optimization. (j=1 was clarified in author communication, but should appear in the paper.)

2. Was any hyperparameter search conducted for λ_sem (the semantic alignment weight)? Table 5 ablates γ but not λ_sem, yet this parameter controls the semantic alignment regularization strength. Was λ_sem = 0.001 fixed throughout, and if so, what is the evidence that this value is appropriate?

3. Can you provide the training duration (total iterations or epochs) for both DiT4SR+w/Ours and ResShift+w/Ours fine-tuning? This is the single most load-bearing missing specification for reproducibility.

## Adversarial Audit Trail (R4)

**R4 Adversarial Pass — Key Counter-Arguments and Lead Response:**

The adversarial subagent raised six counter-arguments against the draft review. The Lead's responses:

1. **FWER criticism as category error (Strong counter):** The adversarial correctly argues that FWER ≈ 99.99% is a category error when applied to deterministic metric evaluations (LPIPS, MUSIQ, MANIQA, etc. are deterministic functions of image outputs, not randomized experiment outputs). The correct statistical concern is within-cell reliability and seed variance. **Verdict shift:** The FWER framing is revised. The need for seed variance reporting remains a valid concern, but the "cannot be statistically supported" language is too strong. The paper's disjunctive claim structure ("best or comparable per cell") does not accumulate Type I error in the way the naive FWER calculation implies.

2. **Asymmetric tuning as standard practice (Strong counter):** The adversarial correctly notes that Bird-SR is a fine-tuning method applied to base models DiT4SR and ResShift — the primary comparisons (DiT4SR+w/Ours vs. DiT4SR) are properly controlled. The seven external baselines are context, not primary comparisons. **Verdict shift:** The "asymmetric tuning inflates apparent advantage" criticism is revised to "asymmetric tuning must be disclosed as a boundary condition on comparative claims." The concern is real but the severity is reduced.

3. **Abstract/table "contradiction" as stylistic (Strong counter):** The adversarial argues these are reconcilable phrasings, not a logical incoherence. "Consistently outperforms" means "is among the best across benchmarks." The paper's actual results support the claim. **Verdict shift:** The characterization softens from "deliberate sleight-of-hand" to "presentation inconsistency requiring harmonization." The revision path (one-sentence abstract change) is unchanged.

4. **Empty GitHub as submission-timing artifact (Strong counter):** The adversarial argues this is universal pre-acceptance practice. **Verdict:** Maintained. The R3 panel's axis-dependent severity framework is the correct lens. The empty repo is a serious reproducibility failure (category b on the ML Reproducibility Checklist), not a fatal flaw at the paper level. The authors' commitment to release upon acceptance is noted.

5. **User study interpretability (Moderate counter):** The adversarial argues forced-choice design cannot mechanically produce 95–98% rates; genuine preference is required. **Verdict shift partially accepted:** The "design-contaminated" label is revised to "suggestive but not valid SOTA corroboration." The magnitude disconnect (large user preference vs. small LPIPS deltas) remains a legitimate concern requiring explanation.

6. **ICML 4 vs. ICML 3 (Strong counter):** The adversarial argues ICML 4 is more appropriate given the paper's genuine algorithmic contribution and all identified issues being corrigible post-hoc without experiment rerunning. **Verdict shift:** Accepted. The R3 panels themselves concluded ICML 4. The revised ICML Overall is 4/6 (Weak Accept with major revisions).

**Author Rebuttal Impact:** The author rebuttal provided strong rebuttals on three points: (a) LPIPS direction = typographical error (arrow wrong, zero correct); (b) j=1 recoverable from Section 3.2; (c) method fully specified in Algorithm 1 + equations. These clarifications soften the Clarity and Technical Soundness concerns. Two unrebutted gaps remain: training duration not specified, and λ_sem not ablated. Both are genuine and revision-correctable.

**Net R4 effect on verdict:** The adversarial pass shifted the ICML Overall from 3 to 4 and softened the FWER framing. The core findings (need for seed variance, abstract harmonization, tuning disclosure, reproducibility gap) are maintained. The paper's genuine algorithmic contribution and strong ablation evidence now receive appropriate weight.
