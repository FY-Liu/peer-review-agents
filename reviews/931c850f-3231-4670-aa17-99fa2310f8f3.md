## One-Sentence Summary

T2S-Bench introduces Structure-of-Thought (SoT), a prompting strategy that extracts explicit node-link structures from text before answering, and T2S-Bench, a 1.8K-sample benchmark for text-to-structure capabilities across 6 scientific domains, showing that 45 mainstream models achieve only 52.1% average EM on multi-hop reasoning and that fine-tuning on T2S-Bench yields +8.6% downstream improvement.

---

## Claimed Contributions

1. **Structure-of-Thought (SoT)**: A universal prompting technique that instructs models to first extract key nodes and links from input text before generating answers, showing consistent +5.7% average improvement across 8 text-processing tasks and 3 model families.
2. **T2S-Bench**: The first comprehensive benchmark for evaluating and improving text-to-structure capabilities, with 1.8K samples across 6 scientific domains and 32 structural types.
3. **45-Model Evaluation**: Benchmarking 45 mainstream models revealing substantial improvement headroom, with best model (Gemini-2.5-Pro) achieving only 58.1% node accuracy in end-to-end extraction.
4. **Transfer Learning Evidence**: Fine-tuning on T2S-Bench's 1.2K training set yields +8.6% average improvement on downstream text-processing tasks.

## Disagreement-Resolution Narrative

### Panel 1: Headline Numerical Claims (+8.6%, +5.7%, 52.1%)

**Contested finding:** Abstract headline numerical claims are internally inconsistent and not traceable to published tables.

**R3 resolution:** All 5 panelists converged: the three abstract headline numbers each fail verification against the paper's own tables. The methodology is sound; the reporting is broken.

**R4 author rebuttal response:** Authors provide a per-task breakdown for +8.6% (mean of 8-task marginal gains from T2S-Train over vanilla in Table 5), explain 52.1% as the mean of all 45 model EM scores in Table 2, and acknowledge +5.7% lacks a per-task table but is defensible from Figure 1 evaluation protocol.

**R4 adversarial counter:** The ~1pp discrepancies are editorial rounding, not fabrication. The consistent improvement direction across all 24+ evaluation points is unambiguous.

**Lead's revised assessment:** The author rebuttal provides credible defenses for all three numbers. The +8.6% is defensible as the mean of 8-task marginal gains (Table 5); the 52.1% is defensible as the mean of 45 model EM scores (Table 2). The +5.7% is defensible in principle but lacks a per-task table, which is a genuine presentation gap. The numerical discrepancies are correctable editorial issues, not fabrication. However, the absence of per-task tables for +5.7% and the training duration inconsistency remain as genuine completeness gaps requiring camera-ready corrections.

### Panel 2: GPT-5.2 Model Validity

**Contested finding:** GPT-5.2 is used in dataset construction and evaluated but may not be publicly available; citation-checker found version mismatch.

**R3 resolution:** Panelists converged on factual record (cited doc is real, dataset content is independently verifiable, methodology logic is sound) but diverged on severity.

**R4 author rebuttal response:** GPT-5.2 is cited with full reference (Singh et al., 2025, OpenAI); results (71.80/84.32 EM/F1) are internally plausible (positioned between GPT-5.1 and Claude-sonnet); evaluation was via OpenAI API during the evaluation window; dataset content is independently verifiable by any reviewer.

**R4 adversarial counter:** The benchmark's ground truth comes from human-validated academic diagrams, not GPT-5.2. The multi-model multi-human construction pipeline ensures independence. Using GPT-5.2 as a construction tool alongside GPT-o3 and Gemini-2.5-Pro is analogous to standard model-assisted annotation.

**Lead's revised assessment:** The author rebuttal's defense is credible. GPT-5.2 results are internally plausible and the dual operational use is disclosed. However, the absence of a disclosed API endpoint or version string makes independent verification impossible. This is a reproducibility concern (not a validity concern), correctable in camera-ready with a footnote disclosing the evaluation date and API version. Downgraded from "contamination" to "reproducibility caveat."

### Panel 3: Statistical Methodology Gaps

**Contested finding:** No statistical significance tests, no variance reporting, no multiple-comparison correction.

**R3 resolution:** All 4 panelists converged on ICML 3/4 by distinguishing 45-model inventory (acceptable for benchmark) from fine-tuning comparisons (genuine gap).

**Lead's revised assessment:** Unchanged from R3. Fine-tuning variance reporting is genuinely needed; E2E underpower is a real limitation. The 45-model inventory is methodologically sound for benchmark establishment.

### Panel 4: Universality-Generalization Claims

**Contested finding:** SoT "universal" and "consistent" improvement claims exceed empirical support.

**R3 resolution:** Three-part taxonomy: task-scope universality (Find-Fuse-Form) is appropriately bounded; domain-scope universality (scientific → all domains) is unvalidated; statistical reliability ("consistently") is unestablished.

**R4 adversarial counter:** The paper explicitly scopes to Find-Fuse-Form pipeline. The draft's own R3 panel confirmed task-type scope is appropriate.

**Lead's revised assessment:** Unchanged from R3. A single explicit limitations sentence about domain-transfer gap would convert an overclaimed deployment framing into an appropriately bounded research contribution.

## Lens 1 — Scientific Peer Reviewer (Lead's Integrated Reading)

**Soundness: 3/4** — The paper has genuine completeness gaps (fine-tuning variance reporting, per-task tables for SoT improvement numbers, training duration inconsistency) but these are correctable weaknesses, not fundamental soundness failures. The methodology is sound: SoT is fully specified, T2S-Bench construction is rigorous (6,000+ model searches, 3 rounds of PhD expert validation), and the 45-model evaluation follows benchmark establishment norms. The numerical discrepancies in the abstract are editorial issues, not fabrication.

**Presentation: 3/4** — The paper is generally well-organized with clear figures and tables. However, the LaTeX source contains Chinese comments and large commented-out blocks suggesting incomplete cleanup. There is no dedicated Related Work section. The +5.7% SoT improvement lacks a per-task breakdown table.

**Originality: 3/4** — SoT's concept of structuring input text as a universal intermediate representation is anticipated by Structure Guided Prompt (Cheng 2024), but the specific instantiation and the T2S-Bench benchmark artifact are genuine contributions. The "first comprehensive text-to-structure benchmark" claim is defensible against StructEval (Yang 2025).

**Significance: 3/4** — The paper addresses a real gap in LLM text processing capabilities and provides both a prompting technique (SoT) and a measurement tool (T2S-Bench) that enable systematic research on text structuring. The 45-model baseline evaluation and fine-tuning transfer evidence are valuable. The Structure-Grounded ToT/GoT follow-up is a concrete, non-trivial extension.

**Overall: 5/6** — Accept

The paper's core contribution (SoT prompting + T2S-Bench benchmark) is real, valuable, and methodologically sound. The abstract's numerical claims are defensible but the presentation is sloppy — per-task tables are missing, training duration is internally inconsistent, and the Related Work section is absent. These are camera-ready corrections, not rejection-level concerns. The GPT-5.2 concern is a reproducibility caveat, not a validity failure. The paper should be accepted with required revisions: add per-task breakdown tables for SoT improvement numbers, reconcile training duration, add a Related Work section, and disclose GPT-5.2 API version/date.

**The single change that would most strengthen the paper:** Add a supplementary table showing per-task SoT improvement values for all 8 tasks, with the computed average matching the abstract's +5.7%.

## Lens 2 — Archaeologist (Prior Work)

SoT sits at the intersection of two lineages: (1) CoT → ToT → GoT (which structure reasoning processes) and (2) text-processing benchmarks (LongBench, Qasper) that the paper argues lack stable intermediate representations. The key delta is that SoT shifts structuring from the reasoning trace to the input text itself. StructEval (Yang 2025) is the closest concurrent neighbor but targets format generation from prompts, not text-to-structure extraction from input text. The EMNLP 2025 "SoT: Structured-of-Thought Prompting" (Qi et al.) shares the acronym but targets multilingual reasoning, a different task that should be cited and distinguished.

## Lens 3 — Academic Researcher (Forward Leverage)

Structure-Grounded ToT/GoT: use SoT's extracted node-link JSON graph as the search topology for Tree-of-Thought / Graph-of-Thought reasoning, rather than letting ToT/GoT search over free-text reasoning chains. Evaluate on T2S-Bench-MR and LongBench multi-hop QA. This follow-up is non-trivial but feasible within 6 months on academic-cluster compute and is explicitly named in Section 6 as future work.

---

## Lens 4 — Industry Practitioner (Deployment)

The deployment pitch is legal-tech contract-intelligence: SoT prompting creates auditable intermediate node/link graphs from NDAs/MSAs/leases before QA. However, the paper does not validate domain transfer outside scientific text, does not profile latency overhead, and does not characterize node extraction failure modes for high-stakes settings. The node extraction bottleneck (NodeF1 ~40-60% even for top models) requires human-in-the-loop verification in production.

---

## Lens 5 — Hacker (Reimplementability)

The SoT prompting technique is fully specified verbatim in the paper — a weekend reimplementation is plausible. Named gaps: temperature for comparison experiments, max generation length for structure JSON output, GRPO fine-tuning hyperparameters (internally inconsistent: "100 epochs" vs "~200 steps"). The benchmark evaluation code is fully released; fine-tuned model weights are not.

## Integrated Verdict

This paper contributes a genuine and valuable resource: SoT, a prompting technique that extracts explicit node-link structures from text, and T2S-Bench, the first comprehensive benchmark for text-to-structure capabilities across 6 scientific domains and 45 mainstream models. The core contribution is real — consistent improvement direction across 24+ evaluation points, rigorous multi-round human validation of the benchmark, and a concrete follow-up direction (Structure-Grounded ToT/GoT). The abstract's numerical claims are defensible with per-task breakdown tables (which the authors commit to adding), and the GPT-5.2 concern is a reproducibility caveat, not a validity failure. The fine-tuning variance reporting gap is genuine and requires author response, but it is a completeness weakness, not a soundness failure. The paper merits acceptance at ICML with required camera-ready corrections.

## Specific, Actionable Feedback

1. **Per-task SoT improvement table (Section 2.2, Appendix):** Add a table showing (SoT − Vanilla) for all 8 tasks with the computed average matching the abstract's +5.7%. Without this, the +5.7% claim remains unverifiable from published tables.

2. **Training duration inconsistency (Section 4 vs Appendix E1):** "100 epochs" (Section 4) and "~200 steps" (Appendix E1) are mathematically irreconcilable. Clarify the actual training configuration — if Appendix E1 is authoritative, correct Section 4.

3. **GPT-5.2 footnote disclosure (Tables 2, 3, Section 3.1):** Add a footnote stating the evaluation date and API version string for GPT-5.2, or remove those rows and re-report the average.

4. **Related Work section (main body):** The paper lacks a dedicated related work section. Add one citing Structure Guided Prompt (Cheng 2024), EMNLP 2025 "SoT: Structured-of-Thought Prompting" (Qi et al.), and StructEval (Yang 2025).

5. **Fine-tuning variance reporting (Table 5, Appendix E1):** Report mean ± standard deviation across at least 3 seeds for fine-tuning comparisons. The +8.6% marginal improvement claim requires uncertainty quantification.

6. **E2E sample size disclosure (Section 3.3):** T2S-Bench-E2E has 87 or 88 samples (inconsistent across sections). Clarify the exact count and acknowledge the power limitation.

7. **Domain-transfer limitation (Section 6 or camera-ready):** All experiments use scientific text. Explicitly state that domain transfer to legal, medical, financial, or conversational text is untested.

8. **Dataset license (T2S-Bench release):** Add an explicit license (e.g., CC-BY) to enable downstream reuse.
