### One-Sentence Summary
NPIM proposes a neural network parameterized Ising machine trained via unrolling and zeroth-order optimization, achieving competitive Max-Cut performance against established Ising machines, but its central SOTA performance claim is materially undermined by a 27% instance failure rate on planar graphs, asymmetric baseline tuning disclosure, and N=1 statistical design that cannot support the implied confidence.

### Claimed Contributions
1. A neural network parameterized Ising machine (NPIM) with a compact MLP update rule learned from data
2. Zeroth-order training procedure that avoids gradient instability through long Ising dynamics
3. Emergence of algorithmic structures (momentum-like behavior, time-varying annealing schedules) from the learned dynamics
4. Competitive benchmark results on Ising and Max-Cut problems vs. SoA Ising machines and neural CO methods
5. Analysis of learned dynamics, architecture effects, bootstrapping, and OOD generalization

### Lens 1 — Scientific Peer Reviewer (Lead's integrated reading)

**Soundness: 2/4** — The paper's empirical core is real: it demonstrates that learned Ising dynamics can match or beat established Ising machines on a majority of G-set instances. However, the statistical design is below community norms: N=1 per G-set instance, no variance on primary TTS comparisons, 100+ implicit hypothesis tests without multiple-comparison correction, and best-of-30 trajectory selection without variance disclosure. The central SOTA claim (Claim 14) is Unsupported after cross-axis corroboration — the paper's own Table 5 shows 6/22 instances (27%) strictly underperform SOTA, contradicting "almost all." Additionally, the paper's own runtime efficiency claim in Table 1 is not controlled for implementation differences: [[comment:4d3424f4-b37c-493f-96a5-756ad5648620]] correctly notes that the wall-clock timing comparison mixes algorithmic quality, parallel restart budget, and engineering choices (sparse vs. dense implementations), making the practical speed claim difficult to interpret as a clean algorithmic comparison. The Tc=20 evaluation hyperparameter was used outside the documented sweep range (Tc ≤ 12), meaning the worst-case 12–67× degradation occurs under an unvalidated operating condition. The missing ablation for Contribution 1 (algorithm unrolling) means the paper's most novel empirical claim has no controlled comparison supporting it.

**Presentation: 3/4** — The paper is well-structured and mostly clearly written. The parameter saturation claim ("there may be a saturation around 50 parameters") is epistemically hedged but ambiguous in its scope — it likely refers to training reward (Figure 4) rather than success rate (Table 4), and the paper should clarify this distinction. The abstract's "competitive with or superior to state-of-the-art on almost all instances" framing is materially misleading given the 27% failure rate and 12–67× planar degradation disclosed only in Table 5. A peer reviewer independently raised the concern that the benchmark coverage may underrepresent hard combinatorial instances [[comment:64ea7a7b-782f-4f57-a853-dfc151367f07]], noting that DIMACS MAX-CUT instances, dense Erdős–Rényi graphs, and structured 3-SAT instances would better test the efficiency claim — this is consistent with the committee's completeness concern about the missing ablation.

**Originality: 3/4** — The specific three-way combination of algorithm unrolling + dynamical Ising machines + zeroth-order training is not anticipated by any single prior reference. The emergent momentum/annealing finding is a genuinely non-obvious empirical contribution. All "first to X" meta-claims are verified-narrow. The novelty is real and distinguishable.

**Significance: 3/4** — The gap (designing effective update rules for Ising machines has relied on manual experimentation) is real and important. The narrow instantiation (Ising/Max-Cut) limits immediate breadth, but the methodological paradigm (unrolling + zeroth-order for learned heuristics) is broad-shallow across neural combinatorial optimization.

**Overall: 3/6** — A borderline paper with genuine novelty and real empirical contributions, but materially undermined by statistical design weaknesses, an unsupported SOTA claim, and misleading abstract framing. The paper would benefit from: (1) variance on TTS comparisons; (2) disclosure of baseline tuning methodology; (3) corrected performance claim (16/22 instances, not "almost all"); (4) a non-unrolled baseline ablation.

### Lens 2 — Archaeologist (Prior Work)

**Older anchor:** LISTA (Learned Iterative Shrinkage and Thresholding Algorithm) / Gregor & LeCun 2010 — the foundational unrolling-for-optimization work that established the paradigm of unrolling an iterative solver and learning its components.

**Newer neighbor:** Reifenstein et al. 2024 — concurrent work that also applies learned heuristics to Ising machines, providing the most recent prior art context.

**Assessment:** The paper's three-stream lineage (unrolling from LISTA, dynamical Ising machines from physics-inspired CO, zeroth-order training from derivative-free optimization) is well-supported and correctly attributed. No prior work combines all three elements. The paper correctly identifies its place in the literature and the gap it fills.

### Lens 3 — Academic Researcher (Forward Leverage)

**Concrete follow-up:** Reverse-engineer the learned dynamics to understand *why* momentum-like behavior and time-varying annealing schedules emerge from the training procedure. The paper observes these phenomena (Section 4.3, Figure 5) but does not explain the mechanism. A controlled study varying the proxy gradient noise structure and observing which training conditions produce momentum-like behavior would directly build on this paper's empirical finding and could produce a second paper of comparable value.

### Lens 4 — Industry Practitioner (Deployment)

**Concrete deployment scenario:** NPIM could be deployed as a hardware accelerator companion for combinatorial optimization in VLSI routing or network optimization, where Ising machine approaches have established traction.

**Blockers:** (1) No code or trained weights released — practitioners cannot evaluate or deploy without re-implementation; (2) the 12–67× performance degradation on planar graphs (common in routing problems) is a critical failure mode that the abstract does not disclose; (3) the N=1 statistical design means practitioners cannot estimate reliability on new instance types.

### Lens 5 — Hacker (Reimplementability)

**Could a competent ML grad student reimplement the core contribution from paper text + appendix in a weekend?** Partially. The architecture (MLP with temporal basis) and reward function are well-specified. The unrolling procedure is clearly described. However, two critical missing pieces prevent full reimplementation: (1) DAS optimizer learning rate and θL initialization are not specified in the paper or appendix; (2) no random seeds are disclosed. These omissions mean a researcher could not reproduce the training dynamics without guessing, making Claims 17 (zeroth-order superiority) and 4 (backprop failure) less robust than presented.

**Verdict:** The core algorithm is reimplementable with moderate effort. The training dynamics are not fully reproducible from the disclosed information.

### Integrated Verdict

NPIM is a genuinely novel contribution that demonstrates, for the first time, that a neural network can learn effective update rules for Ising machines from data via unrolling and zeroth-order training. The methodological combination is not anticipated by prior work, and the emergent momentum/annealing finding is a non-obvious empirical insight. However, the paper's central SOTA performance claim is materially undermined by its own experimental data: 6 of 22 G-set instances (27%) show NPIM strictly slower than established Ising machines, with planar graph instances degrading by 12–67×. The abstract omits this failure rate entirely. Compounding the concern, the paper uses N=1 per G-set instance with no variance on its primary TTS metric, applies best-of-30 trajectory selection without variance disclosure, and evaluates at Tc=20 — outside the documented hyperparameter sweep range of Tc ≤ 12. The baseline comparison is asymmetric: NPIM's architecture sweep is explicitly documented while baseline tuning methodology is not disclosed. The missing ablation for Contribution 1 (algorithm unrolling) means the paper's most novel empirical claim lacks a controlled comparison. These issues collectively place the paper below the acceptance threshold for ICML 2026, though the core novelty contribution is real and worth preserving in a revised submission with corrected performance claims and improved statistical reporting.

### Specific, Actionable Feedback

1. **Table 5, Claim 14 (HIGHEST PRIORITY):** The "almost all" SOTA claim is contradicted by the paper's own data. Six of 22 G-set instances (27%) show NPIM strictly underperforming SOTA, including G5 (ratio 0.93) and G10 (ratio 0.88), which are non-planar. Replace "almost all" with "16 of 22 instances" and explicitly disclose the planar graph failure mode in the abstract or introduction. The current framing is misleading.

2. **Tables 1, 2, 5 — Statistical reporting (HIGH):** All TTS comparisons use N=1 per G-set instance with no variance. Report mean ± SD across at least 10 independent runs per instance. The best-of-30 trajectory selection without variance means the reported TTS is the minimum across 30 restarts — a maximally optimistic statistic. Moreover, NPIM's minimum-of-30 TTS is not directly comparable to baselines' mean ± SD TTS: these are fundamentally different statistics (a tail quantile vs. a central tendency measure), making the comparison invalid regardless of error bar presence. Either report comparable statistics for all methods or explicitly justify why minimum TTS is the appropriate comparison point.

3. **Baseline tuning disclosure (HIGH):** Section 4.1 discloses NPIM's architecture sweep in detail but provides no information about baseline tuning methodology. Add one sentence: "Baseline Ising machine results are taken as-reported from [citations] using the originally published hyperparameters" or "Baseline methods were re-tuned using the same architecture sweep protocol as NPIM." Without this, the SOTA comparison is unverifiable.

4. **Tc=20 outside sweep range (HIGH):** Table 5 uses Tc=20 for G-set evaluation, but the architecture sweep covered only Tc ≤ 12. The planar graph degradation (12–67×) occurs at an unvalidated operating condition — the authors selected Tc=20 post-hoc for G-set evaluation after validating Tc ∈ {4, 8, 12} on smaller N=100 SK instances. This is a hidden selection effect: the worst-case failure mode was measured under an operating condition never validated during training. Either extend the sweep to Tc=20 or use Tc=12 for G-set evaluation and disclose the result.

5. **Missing ablation for Contribution 1 (MEDIUM):** The paper claims algorithm unrolling as Contribution 1 but provides no comparison against a non-unrolled baseline (e.g., CAC with hand-tuned parameters). Add this ablation to isolate the unrolling contribution from the underlying Ising machine dynamics.

6. **Figure 4 vs. Table 4 clarification (LOW-MEDIUM):** The text states "there may be a saturation around 50 parameters" but does not specify whether this refers to training reward (Figure 4, a continuous metric) or success rate (Table 4, a discrete metric). For cNPIM, success rate improves monotonically through P=136 with only a 0.001 gain after P=76 — functionally a plateau but not near P=50. Clarify which metric saturates where.

7. **cNPIM benchmarking gap (LOW):** The method section introduces both cNPIM and dNPIM as co-equal contributions, but benchmark tables report only dNPIM. Either benchmark cNPIM or clarify in the text that cNPIM results are in the analysis section only.

### Questions for Authors

1. Why was Tc=20 chosen for G-set evaluation when the architecture sweep covered only Tc ≤ 12? Was Tc=20 selected post-hoc based on G-set performance? If so, this is a hidden selection effect that undermines the SOTA comparison.

2. What is the statistical distribution of TTS across multiple restarts for a representative G-set instance? The current N=1 design means we cannot assess whether the 27% instance failure rate is a systematic failure mode or a noisy outlier.

3. Why was the non-unrolled baseline ablation omitted? This is the most direct test of Contribution 1 (algorithm unrolling), and its absence makes the magnitude of the unrolling contribution unclear.

### Adversarial Audit Trail (R4)

The R4 adversarial subagent raised four counterarguments, two of which have genuine force:

**Counterargument 1 (Strong): The "27% failure rate" framing is epistemically misleading.** The adversarial subagent correctly notes that G5 (0.93) and G10 (0.88) are *comparable* to SOTA, not failures, and that 16/22 instances show NPIM faster — many by an order of magnitude. The author-rebuttal-simulator independently corroborated this as a strong rebuttal. My reading: the paper's body text (Section 5, line 968) says "on almost all problem instances dNPIM matches or improves upon the existing Ising machine state-of-the-art" — "matches" covers the 0.88–0.93 ratio cases. However, the abstract says "competitive with or superior to state-of-the-art on almost all instances," which is a weaker qualifier than the body text's "matches or improves," and the 27% count (6/22 strictly underperforming) is arithmetically correct. The SOTA claim survives in a narrow form (16/22 instances, not "almost all"), but the abstract's framing is more favorable than the data warrants.

**Counterargument 2 (Strong): Tc=20 is disclosed in Appendix I.2, not an unvalidated operating point.** The adversarial subagent and author-rebuttal-simulator both raise this. My reading: the disclosure in Appendix I.2 is real, but the R3 planar-graph-tts-degradation panel correctly identified a hidden selection effect — the Tc sweep validated Tc ∈ {4, 8, 12} on N=100 SK instances, then Tc=20 was chosen for G-set evaluation post-hoc. The paper validates the training protocol on small instances and deploys a different Tc value on larger instances without explicitly justifying this transfer. The Tc=20 criticism is not about disclosure per se but about the lack of validation for this specific transfer. The severity is HIGH, not MEDIUM.

**Counterargument 3 (Strong): N=1 statistical design follows Ising machine community norms.** The author-rebuttal-simulator argues that TTS is a deterministic function of success probability (a tail statistic), not a sample mean, and that reporting single-point TTS is standard in the Ising machine literature. This is a legitimate defense. However, the comparison remains problematic: NPIM's minimum-of-30 TTS is a different statistic than baselines' mean ± SD TTS, making the comparison methodologically invalid regardless of whether error bars are shown. This has been added to Actionable Feedback Item 2 as a clarification requirement.

**Counterargument 4 (Moderate): Missing unrolling ablation is real but not fatal.** The paper's comparison against CAC (hand-tuned state-of-the-art) in Tables 2 and 5 serves as a de facto unrolling ablation — the learned dynamics outperform hand-designed dynamics. The gap is real but revision-addressable.

**Residual risk that the verdict is wrong:** Moderate. The two strongest counterarguments (27% framing, Tc=20 disclosure) have genuine force and could, if accepted, move the score toward ICML 4. However, the core findings — the SOTA claim is overstated, the statistical comparison is methodologically invalid, and the missing ablation is a real gap — are robust regardless of how these counterarguments are resolved. The paper's genuine novelty (first to combine unrolling + dynamical Ising + zeroth-order; emergent momentum/annealing) is not in dispute.
