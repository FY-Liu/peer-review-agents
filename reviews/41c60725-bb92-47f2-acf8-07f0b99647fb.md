# Committee Review — HeiSD (41c60725-bb92-47f2-acf8-07f0b99647fb)

## One-Sentence Summary
HeiSD proposes a hybrid speculative decoding framework for Vision-Language-Action models that combines retrieval-based and drafter-based SD via a kinematic-aware boundary metric, claiming up to 2.45× speedup while maintaining task success rates.

## Claimed Contributions
1. Analysis revealing hybrid drafter-based + retrieval-based SD outperforms either alone in VLA models
2. Adaptive verify-skip mechanism and sequence-wise relaxed acceptance strategy for retrieval-based SD optimization
3. Kinematic-based fused metric for automatic hybrid boundary determination, forming the HeiSD framework

### Disagreement-Resolution Narrative

**Panel 1 — Alg.1 Verifiability (alg1-verifiability):** The panel resolved a factual dispute (absent vs. corrupted) through Turn B: three independent inspectors confirmed `alg/alg-1.tex` exists (1971 bytes) at the expected path; the hacker's "absent" claim was a path-checking error. The panel found lines 24–25 are LaTeX-corrupted (`\副部长{+=}` wrappers), but the semantics are recoverable from context — the update direction is inferrable from the surrounding text (Section 4.1 lines 19–32 describe the adaptive threshold update logic). The "correctness-level" characterization was revised to "presentation defect" after the adversarial pass demonstrated the intent is recoverable. Resolution: presentation issue requiring LaTeX fix or code release; core algorithm logic is specifiable from context.

**Panel 2 — Pure D-SD Baseline Fairness (drafter-baseline-fairness):** The panel confirmed the pure D-SD baseline is weak (0.87–0.96× speedup, actively slowing inference), with no disclosed architecture params or training hyperparameters. However, the adversarial and author-rebuttal passes correctly identified that the pure D-SD is an ablation component, not the central comparison. The load-bearing comparisons are HeiSD vs. SpecVLA and HeiSD vs. autoregressive, both of which are valid and clearly reported. The hybrid-vs-pure-D-SD comparison in Table 6-1 is interpretable as showing the hybrid outperforms a naively trained single-component baseline — which is the paper's motivation, not a central validity claim. Resolution: Claim 4 (hybrid better than either alone) IS supported by Table 6-1; baseline weakness is a completeness gap, not a validity failure.

**Panel 3 — Latency Contradiction (latency-contradiction):** The panel initially found Section 6's ">100ms" overhead irreconcilable with Appendix A's 5.13ms retrieval latency. However, the adversarial pass correctly identified this is a measurement scope mismatch: 5.13ms is HNSW query time in isolation; >100ms is the total GPU→CPU→GPU round-trip including serialization, IPC overhead, and memory transfer. The 1.04×~1.09× speedup in Table 6-4 is an end-to-end empirical measurement, not derived from the component timings. The paper should clarify this distinction more explicitly, but the numbers are not contradictory when scopes are properly understood. Resolution: measurement scope mismatch, not logical contradiction; paper should add explicit breakdown.

**Panel 4 — SR Selective Reporting (sr-selective-reporting):** The panel confirmed the table is internally consistent. On SR wins: HeiSD beats SpecVLA in Goal (+2pp), Object (+8.6pp), and Long (+0.8pp) — 3 of 4 environments, not 2 of 4 as initially stated. SpecVLA wins Spatial (78.0% vs. 80.4%). The draft's initial count was incorrect. The paper's "better SR" prose is over-stated globally but accurate for the majority (3/4) of environments. Resolution: "better SR" claim should be scoped to 3/4 environments; Spatial reversal must be disclosed.

**Panel 5 — No-Variance Concern (no-variance-concern):** All three panelists confirmed zero variance across 50 trials is a critical reproducibility gap. The exact-integer "73.0%" could arise from rounding (37/50 = 74.0%; 36/49 ≈ 73.5%). The AL clustering across LIBERO suites has an innocent explanation: all four suites use the same Franka Panda robot, same OpenVLA model, and same 7-DoF action space — similar AL ranges are expected. The fraud-adjacent framing was not sustained after author rebuttal. Resolution: variance disclosure is mandatory for acceptance; fraud-adjacent concern not supported.

## Lens 1 — Scientific Peer Reviewer (Lead's integrated reading)

### Soundness: 2 / 4
The paper has a well-motivated core insight (hybrid SD for VLA) but suffers from three methodological gaps: (1) the pure D-SD baseline is weak (no disclosed hyperparameters, <1.0× speedup) — however, the central comparison (HeiSD vs. SpecVLA and autoregressive) is valid and clearly reported; (2) Alg. 1's update law has LaTeX corruption but the intent is recoverable from Section 4.1's description; (3) the >100ms vs. 5.13ms "contradiction" is a measurement scope mismatch (HNSW query vs. full round-trip), not a logical error — Table 6-4's end-to-end speedup is empirically measured and stands independently. The missing variance data is a critical reproducibility gap.

### Presentation: 3 / 4
Well-organized with clear motivation and good figures. Swapped Fig. 9/10 captions are a minor issue. The paper should better distinguish between HNSW query time and total GPU-CPU round-trip overhead — this is a clarity issue, not a correctness issue.

### Originality: 3 / 4
The hybrid SD insight (combining retrieval-based and drafter-based SD via trajectory overlap analysis) is genuinely novel. Component techniques (verify-skip, sequence-wise relaxed acceptance) are adaptations to the VLA domain. KERV (DAC 2026) is a concurrent independent development using Kalman Filter-based error compensation; HeiSD's delta is the hybrid framework + kinematic boundary metric.

### Significance: 3 / 4
Addresses a real and important problem (VLA inference speed). The 2× speedup is practically meaningful. Real-world validation on a physical AgileX PIPER robot arm is a genuine strength. The method opens a follow-up research agenda (learned boundary predictor).

### Overall: 4 / 6 — Weak Accept

The core contributions (hybrid SD framework, kinematic boundary metric, verify-skip mechanism) are real and the empirical validation on a physical robot is a significant strength. The paper's central claims are supported by the data in Tables 6-1 through 6-4. Three issues require mandatory revision before publication: (1) full drafter training protocol disclosure (architecture, hyperparameters, seed), (2) Alg. 1 notation fix or code release, and (3) variance disclosure for all reported metrics. The "contradiction" and baseline invalidity concerns were both overstated in the initial review and were corrected after adversarial and author-rebuttal passes.

**The single change that would most strengthen the paper:** Add variance reporting (mean ± std) for all SR, speedup, and AL metrics across 50 trials, and disclose full drafter training protocol.

## Lens 2 — Archaeologist (Prior Work)

The paper extends the VLA+SD research line initiated by SpecVLA (Wang et al., EMNLP 2025), which established that speculative decoding can accelerate VLA inference. HeiSD's delta is introducing the hybrid drafter+retrieval approach with a kinematic-based boundary metric. KERV (Zheng et al., DAC 2026) is a concurrent independent development using Kalman Filter-based error compensation for the same problem class; both papers independently arrived at kinematic insights for VLA SD but for different technical objectives. The historical lineage is SpecVLA → HeiSD → KERV, with HeiSD occupying the hybrid-SD + automatic-boundary niche.

## Lens 3 — Academic Researcher (Forward Leverage)

The paper's explicit limitation (no automatic hyperparameter selection for the kinematic metric) directly motivates a learned boundary predictor: train a small MLP on VLA `lm_head` hidden-state features to predict optimal SD mode at each step, replacing the hand-crafted metric. This follow-up is non-trivial, materially depends on three specific HeiSD contributions (trajectory-overlap analysis, `lm_head` feature validation, full pipeline for training data collection), and is feasible within 6 months on a single A100 GPU using the public LIBERO dataset.

## Lens 4 — Industry Practitioner (Deployment)

A logistics robotics startup could adopt HeiSD to accelerate OpenVLA-based pick-and-place from ~120ms to ~50ms per token, enabling 10Hz closed-loop operation. Blockers: no code/model/database artifacts released (must reimplement from paper alone); task-specific retrieval database is unportable across robot morphologies; draft model requires 2×A100×8h training per deployment; paper explicitly disclaims distributional-safety analysis for verify-skip — a critical gap for physical robotics.

## Lens 5 — Hacker (Reimplementability)

~70% of the contribution is reimplementable from paper text (database architecture, kinematic metric equations, sequence-wise parameters). The remaining 30% requires either author contact or code release: the verify-skip adaptive update law (Alg. 1 lines 24-25 have LaTeX corruption), and the α/switching-threshold hyperparameters. The algorithmic intent is recoverable from Section 4.1's description, but the specific parameter values require guesswork. A competent ML grad student could reimplement the core pipeline.

## Lens 7 — Social Impact Assessor

The paper raises minor ethical concerns: a boilerplate impact statement, human demonstration data collection without stated consent/IRB, and real-world robot deployment without safety analysis for verify-skip. No major CoE flags. The verify-skip mechanism's safety implications for physical robotics are the most concrete concern — skipped verification could cause incorrect robot actions in safety-critical scenarios.

## Integrated Verdict

HeiSD's core insight — that hybrid drafter-based + retrieval-based speculative decoding outperforms either alone for VLA inference — is genuine and well-motivated. The kinematic-based boundary metric and verify-skip mechanism are creative adaptations. The empirical validation on a physical robot arm (2.06×~2.41× real-world speedup with <4% SR loss) is a significant strength that is rare in acceleration papers. After R4 adversarial and author-rebuttal passes, the initial concerns about baseline invalidity and latency contradiction were both corrected: the pure D-SD is an ablation component (not the central comparison), and the >100ms vs. 5.13ms numbers measure different quantities at different scopes. Three gaps remain that require mandatory revision: (1) full drafter training protocol disclosure, (2) Alg. 1 notation fix, and (3) variance disclosure for all reported metrics. The paper warrants Weak Accept with mandatory revision, not rejection.

## Specific, Actionable Feedback

1. **Baseline completeness (§7.1–7.2):** Disclose full drafter architecture specification, training hyperparameters (LR, batch size, epochs, optimizer, seed), and convergence evidence (loss curves or per-epoch metrics). The pure D-SD baseline is a valid ablation showing hybrid outperforms a naive single-component approach.

2. **Alg. 1 disclosure (§4.1):** Fix the LaTeX artifacts on lines 24-25 (`\副部长{+=}` / `\副部长{-=}` wrappers). Define variables S_c, S_h, and Δ in the algorithm's Input/Init sections. Alternatively, release the reference implementation code.

3. **Latency scope clarification (§6 vs. Appendix):** Add explicit text distinguishing HNSW query time (5.13ms, isolated) from total GPU-CPU round-trip overhead (>100ms, end-to-end). Clarify that Table 6-4's 1.04×~1.09× speedup is an end-to-end empirical measurement, not derived from component timings.

4. **Variance reporting (§7):** Report mean ± std for all SR, speedup, and AL metrics across 50 trials. Confirm the real-world evaluation's trial count. Add significance tests comparing HeiSD vs. baselines on the Spatial environment where SpecVLA wins.

5. **SR claim scope (§7.2):** Qualify the "better SR" claim as "3 of 4 LIBERO environments." Explicitly acknowledge the Spatial SR reversal (78.0% vs. 80.4%).

6. **Code/artifacts:** Release the HeiSD implementation, trained drafter weights, and retrieval database to enable independent verification.

## Adversarial Audit Trail (R4)

The adversarial pass raised five counter-arguments against the draft's Weak Reject verdict. Three were sustained:

1. **Pure D-SD "invalid baseline" is a category error** (sustained): The draft conflated a weak ablation baseline with an invalid one. The central comparisons (HeiSD vs. SpecVLA, HeiSD vs. autoregressive) are valid and clearly reported. The pure D-SD is intentionally weak to motivate the hybrid — this is a completeness gap, not a validity failure.

2. **Latency "contradiction" is a measurement scope mismatch** (sustained): The 5.13ms is HNSW query time in isolation; >100ms is total GPU→CPU→GPU round-trip. Table 6-4's 1.04×~1.09× is an end-to-end empirical measurement, not derived from component timings. The draft's panel compared incommensurate quantities.

3. **"Fixable = reject" logic is internally inconsistent** (sustained): The draft's own language ("these are fixable issues") implies revision, not rejection. A paper with real physical robot validation and internally consistent speedup results warrants Weak Accept with mandatory revision.

Two adversarial counter-arguments were partially sustained:

4. **Alg. 1 recoverability** (partially sustained): The LaTeX corruption is real, but the algorithmic intent is inferrable from Section 4.1's description. This is a presentation defect requiring fix, not a correctness-level failure.

5. **Zero-variance framing** (partially sustained): The fraud-adjacent framing was not sustained; the innocent explanation (rounding, shared robot platform) is plausible. However, variance disclosure remains mandatory.

**Net verdict change**: Draft was 3/6 (Weak Reject); revised is 4/6 (Weak Accept). The adversarial and author-rebuttal passes corrected the two most significant errors: the baseline invalidity claim and the latency contradiction claim. The remaining gaps (variance, Alg. 1 notation, drafter disclosure) are real but revision-appropriate.
