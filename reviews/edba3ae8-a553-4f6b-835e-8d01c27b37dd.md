### One-Sentence Summary

TP-GRPO addresses reward sparsity and misaligned credit assignment in flow-based GRPO by computing step-level incremental rewards via ODE completion and assigning aggregated long-term rewards to "turning points" — steps where the local reward trend flips to align with the overall trajectory — demonstrating consistent improvements over Flow-GRPO across three text-to-image generation tasks, with the core mathematical apparatus sound but experimental reporting below ICML standards.

### Claimed Contributions

1. Identifies reward sparsity and step-level misalignment from propagating outcome-based rewards to intermediate denoising steps; addresses via step-wise reward differences capturing each SDE update's "pure" gain.
2. Introduces turning points — steps that flip local reward trend to match overall trajectory — with a strict sign-based criterion for identification and aggregated long-term reward assignment.
3. First work to explicitly model implicit interaction in Flow-based GRPO.
4. Proposes TP-GRPO framework combining both innovations; demonstrates effectiveness on text-to-image generation tasks.

**Disagreement-Resolution Narratives from R3 Panels:**

**Panel 1 — Ablation Gap (ablation-gap):** All four panelists (claim-auditor, completeness, experimental-rigor, technical-soundness) unanimously agreed the two core innovations (step-level incremental rewards + turning-point aggregation) are never independently evaluated — the paper evaluates only their conjunction. The paper's own Section 5 frames these as *orthogonal* problem decompositions, creating an experimental obligation the paper fails to meet. Mathematical soundness of Eq. 7 and Eq. 8 is confirmed but cannot substitute for empirical validation. Severity split unresolved: experimental-rigor called it "fatal"; claim-auditor, completeness, and technical-soundness called it "genuine but non-fatal." **Recommendation:** Combined result is supported by 24/24 directional improvements; neither component's independent contribution is validated. The ablation gap is a genuine experimental gap warranting revision, not rejection.

**Panel 2 — Hyperparameter-Free Claim (hyperparameter-free):** All four panelists (claim-auditor, completeness, deployment, hyperparameter-budget-auditor) converged: the *narrow* form of the claim is true (turning-point detection criterion itself requires no threshold/magnitude hyperparameter), but the *broad* form ("our method is efficient and hyperparameter-free") is misleading. TP-GRPO retains standard GRPO hyperparameters: β, α, G, T, learning rate, LoRA rank, window size. Section 6.3's sensitivity analysis on window size and α directly contradicts the broad "hyperparameter-free" framing. **Recommendation:** Reframe as "turning-point detection is hyperparameter-free; the method inherits the standard GRPO hyperparameter stack." Authors fully conceded this in author rebuttal.

**Panel 3 — Reward Model Confound (reward-model-confound):** All four panelists (experimental-rigor, figure-and-table-auditor, risk-of-fraud-detector, statistician) agreed: the reward model version confound is real (Appendix B discloses TP-GRPO uses newer/better checkpoints than Flow-GRPO), the disclosure is in Appendix B not near Table 1 (completeness gap), and Table 1 numbers are accurate and real. Severity split: statistician holds "empirically unverified absent matched-checkpoint ablation"; experimental-rigor holds "meaningful but not fatal"; figure-and-table-auditor holds "numbers are real but uninformative about causal question." **Recommendation:** Results are conditionally valid — headline claims hold only if reward model version effect is small relative to algorithmic effect, a condition not demonstrated. Flag for author rebuttal. Authors partially rebutted: the confound affects absolute reward scale but the relative ordering within groups (which drives advantage computation via Eq. 3) is partially preserved.

**Panel 4 — Statistical Validity (statistical-validity):** All four panelists converged on "significant gap, not fundamental flaw." The statistician revised from "Fundamentally compromised" to "Significant gaps — revision required" after accepting that the controlled re-implementation and directional consistency across 24/24 cells constitute genuine (if informal) evidence. FWER≈71% across 24+ uncorrected pairwise comparisons is a real structural risk but is misapplied as a worst-case independence bound — the effective false-positive risk under the actual correlation structure is far lower. The 24/24 directional improvements constitute a sign-test with p ≈ 6×10⁻⁸ under the null, providing meaningful informal evidence. **Recommendation:** ICML disposition is revision, not rejection. Mandatory requirements: disclosed N and seed count, variance across ≥3 seeds, significance tests with multiple-comparison correction, and controlled comparison using the same reward model version.

### Lens 1 — Scientific Peer Reviewer (Lead's integrated reading)

**Soundness 2.5 / 4:** The paper's theoretical apparatus is sound — Eq. 7 (step-level incremental reward), Eq. 8 (aggregated long-term reward), and Lemmas C.1–C.3 (sign consistency proofs) are correctly derived and verified by the technical-soundness subagent. The experimental evidence shows consistent directional improvement across all 24/24 cells in Table 1, which under a sign-test yields p ≈ 6×10⁻⁸ — meaningful informal evidence even absent variance reporting. The training curves show visible separation between TP-GRPO and Flow-GRPO that grows and persists, not noise-like oscillation. However, three material gaps remain: (1) no multi-seed statistics or significance tests; (2) the two core contributions are never independently evaluated; (3) the reward model version confound (Appendix B) means the primary comparison is not strictly controlled. These are reporting and experimental design gaps that warrant revision, not rejection — but they are real.

**Presentation 3 / 4:** The paper is clearly written with well-motivated problem diagnosis and good figures (particularly Figure 1's reward oscillation illustration). Section 3 has notation overload. The "hyperparameter-free" framing is imprecise — the authors conceded this in the author rebuttal and offered a precise correction. The absence of a dedicated Limitations section is a completeness gap the authors also conceded.

**Originality 3 / 4:** The turning-point concept (sign-change-based detection of steps that flip local reward trend + aggregated long-term reward assignment) is genuinely novel and credibly first-of-its-kind in the Flow-GRPO literature. The step-level incremental reward mechanism is a modest variation on DenseGRPO's concurrent work (ICLR 2026), but the turning-point mechanism is the paper's distinctive contribution.

**Significance 3 / 4:** The paper addresses a real and timely credit-assignment problem in flow-based RL. The consistent directional improvements across three diverse tasks (compositional generation, OCR, human preference) and two architectures (SD3.5-M, FLUX.1-dev) provide genuine (if statistically thin) evidence of effectiveness. The main limitations are ceiling-effect metrics (GenEval at 96%+), compute overhead (~10x), and sub-field specificity (tightly coupled to flow matching + GRPO).

**Overall recommendation: ICML 4 (Weak Accept).** The paper's core innovation — the turning-point mechanism — is mathematically sound and conceptually novel. The empirical evidence, while statistically underreported, shows consistent directional improvement across all 24/24 cells and two architectures. The experimental gaps (variance reporting, factorial ablation, reward model control) are correctable in revision and do not undermine the core finding that TP-GRPO outperforms Flow-GRPO. A revision addressing these gaps would be a strong accept.

**The single change that would most strengthen the paper:** Run a factorial ablation (A: step-level rewards only, B: turning-point aggregation only, C: both, D: Flow-GRPO baseline) with ≥3 seeds and report mean±std for all conditions. This directly addresses the primary experimental gap and would substantially improve the experimental rigor rating from Fair to Good.

### Lens 2 — Archaeologist (Prior Work)

The archaeologist's R1+R2 analysis established the prior-art landscape: Flow-GRPO (Liu et al., 2025) is the primary baseline with uniform terminal reward assignment. DenseGRPO (Deng et al., ICLR 2026) is concurrent work that also addresses sparse rewards in Flow-GRPO via a *learned* reward predictor — TP-GRPO's use of ODE-completion-based reward differences is mathematically distinct. TempFlow-GRPO (He et al., 2025) addresses temporal non-uniformity via trajectory branching and noise-aware weighting — orthogonal to TP-GRPO's reward assignment mechanism. DanceGRPO (Xue et al., 2025) is another GRPO variant for flow matching, also using uniform terminal rewards.

The "first to model implicit interaction in Flow-based GRPO" claim (line 258) is **plausibly correct** — no prior work uses the turning-point/sign-change/aggregated-reward framework. The step-level incremental reward component overlaps conceptually with DenseGRPO's dense reward computation, but the specific mathematical formulation (Eq. 7 as a difference, not a learned prediction) and the turning-point mechanism are unique to TP-GRPO.

### Lens 3 — Academic Researcher (Forward Leverage)

The forward-leverage subagent identified two concrete follow-up projects: (1) **Adaptive SDE noise scheduling** — using turning-point detection to modulate noise schedules during inference, directly depending on both the sign-change criterion and the aggregated reward formulation; (2) **Extension to diffusion models** — the turning-point framework is model-agnostic and could transfer to DDPM-style discrete denoising models where implicit cross-step interactions are even more pronounced. The paper enables both follow-ups; neither is self-contained.

### Lens 4 — Industry Practitioner (Deployment)

The deployment subagent's assessment is Poor. TP-GRPO requires ~10x more reward-model inference per training step (O(N×T²) vs. O(N×T) for Flow-GRPO), demands 32×H20 GPUs, and the released code is demo-quality with three concrete breakages. The "hyperparameter-free" framing is particularly harmful for practitioners who would budget zero tuning time. The most deployable component (turning-point detection without full incremental rewards) is also the less-novel component relative to DenseGRPO. Adoption horizon: 6–18 months for large labs; impractical for most practitioners.

### Integrated Verdict

TP-GRPO introduces a mathematically sound and conceptually novel mechanism — turning-point detection via sign changes in incremental rewards, with aggregated long-term reward assignment — that addresses a genuine credit-assignment problem in flow-based GRPO. The empirical evidence shows consistent directional improvement across all 24/24 cells in Table 1 (sign-test p ≈ 6×10⁻⁸), providing meaningful informal evidence of effectiveness. The theoretical contributions (Eq. 7, Eq. 8, Lemmas C.1–C.3) are verified correct.

However, three experimental gaps limit confidence in the magnitude and causal attribution of the improvement: (1) no multi-seed statistics or significance tests; (2) the two core contributions are never independently evaluated; (3) the reward model version confound is disclosed in Appendix B but not prominently near Table 1. Additionally, the "hyperparameter-free" framing is imprecise and the paper lacks a dedicated Limitations section — both conceded by the authors in rebuttal.

These gaps are correctable in revision. The paper's core claim — that TP-GRPO as a whole outperforms Flow-GRPO — is supported by the directional consistency and the sound mathematical apparatus. The appropriate ICML disposition is **Weak Accept (ICML 4)**, with revision addressing the experimental reporting gaps.

### Specific, Actionable Feedback

1. **§5 (Ablation gap — highest priority):** Run a factorial ablation with four conditions: (A) step-level rewards only (terminal rewards + turning-point detection disabled), (B) turning-point aggregation only (applied to terminal rewards, not step-level differences), (C) both components (TP-GRPO full), (D) Flow-GRPO baseline. Report mean±std across ≥3 seeds for all conditions. This directly addresses the primary experimental gap.

2. **§6 / Table 1 (Statistical reporting):** Disclose evaluation sample size N. Report mean±std across ≥3 seeds for all Table 1 entries. Add significance tests with appropriate multiple-comparison correction (Bonferroni or Holm-Bonferroni) for the 24+ pairwise comparisons. Add variance bands to Figures 4, 6, 7, 8.

3. **Appendix B → §6 (Reward model confound):** Move the reward model version disclosure from Appendix B to a footnote immediately adjacent to Table 1. Ideally, rerun Flow-GRPO with the same newer checkpoint version used by TP-GRPO; if not possible, acknowledge the confound as a controlled variable and discuss its potential magnitude.

4. **§5 / contributions list (Hyperparameter-free claim):** Replace "our method is efficient and hyperparameter-free" with: "turning-point detection requires no threshold or magnitude hyperparameter; TP-GRPO inherits the standard GRPO hyperparameter stack (β, α, G, T, window size)." This one-sentence change accurately represents the contribution.

5. **§6.3 / new section (Limitations):** Add a dedicated Limitations section covering: (a) computational overhead of per-step ODE completion (~10x vs. Flow-GRPO); (b) sensitivity to window size and α (Figures 6–7); (c) ODE-sampling-as-proxy assumption (Section 3.1); (d) remaining hyperparameter stack (β, α, G, T, window size, learning rate, LoRA rank).

6. **§4.2 / Figure 1 (Turning-point frequency):** Quantify turning-point frequency across the sampled trajectories (e.g., "turning points occur in X% of trajectories, with a mean of Y turning points per trajectory"). The current claim "turning points are common" is illustrated with only 3 visual examples.

7. **§6.2 / Figure 5 (Reward hacking):** Add quantitative analysis of reward hacking (e.g., reward distribution statistics, diversity metrics, per-category GenEval breakdown) to support the claim "no indication of reward hacking." The current qualitative illustration is insufficient.

### Questions for Authors

1. What is the frequency of turning points across your training trajectories? The motivation rests on turning points being "common in practice," but this is illustrated with only 3 examples from Figure 1. If turning points are rare, what is the scope of the method?

2. Did you evaluate Flow-GRPO with the same newer reward model checkpoints used by TP-GRPO? If not, what is your estimate of the fraction of the observed improvement attributable to the better reward model vs. the algorithmic novelty?

3. The paper claims "turning points are identified solely by sign changes... making our method efficient and hyperparameter-free" (line 244). The narrow claim (turning-point detection criterion is hyperparameter-free) is correct. Why was this framed as applying to the full method rather than specifically to the detection criterion?

### Adversarial Audit Trail (R4)

The adversarial reviewer argued that the ICML 3 recommendation is internally inconsistent with the draft's own findings — the draft concedes the "combined result is supported," the "core innovation is sound and novel," and "empirical evidence shows consistent directional improvement," yet recommends rejection. The adversarial's strongest points: (1) 24/24 directional improvements constitute extraordinary informal evidence (sign-test p ≈ 6×10⁻⁸); (2) the FWER≈71% figure misapplies a worst-case independence bound to correlated comparisons; (3) the ablation gap is a revision-level concern, not a rejection-level flaw; (4) the reward model confound affects absolute scale but partially controls for relative comparisons via Eq. 3's group normalization. The author rebuttal conceded the "hyperparameter-free" framing and missing Limitations section, provided indirect ablation evidence (Figures 6–7, two TP-GRPO variants, FLUX.1-dev), and argued the sign-test logic provides meaningful informal evidence even without variance reporting.

**Residual risk:** The most significant unresolved concern is the reward model confound — if the newer checkpoint materially advantages TP-GRPO's training signal, some fraction of the observed improvement is attributable to the confound rather than the algorithmic novelty. The matched-checkpoint ablation would definitively resolve this. Until then, the headline claim should be qualified as "TP-GRPO (under condition X) outperforms Flow-GRPO (under condition Y)." The absence of factorial ablation means neither component's independent contribution is quantified, leaving the causal mechanism of improvement opaque.
