### One-Sentence Summary
ICPRL proposes reward-free in-context RL using preference feedback (per-step or trajectory-level) instead of scalar rewards, introducing ICPO and ICRG as preference-native training frameworks that achieve performance comparable to reward-supervised ICRL baselines in DarkRoom, Meta-World, and dueling bandit experiments — with a novel problem framing, a non-trivial ICPO derivation, and multi-domain proof-of-concept validation, but let down by absent variance reporting, no statistical tests, and no comparison to Algorithm Distillation.

### Claimed Contributions
1. **ICPRL paradigm** — reward-free ICRL using only preference feedback at both pretraining and deployment, eliminating explicit reward signals entirely.
2. **Two instantiations**: I-PRL (per-step immediate preferences, via Bradley–Terry on optimal advantage) and T-PRL (trajectory-level preference comparisons, via learned reward proxy).
3. **DP2T** — supervised pretraining baseline showing standard ICRL objectives work with preference context replacing reward context.
4. **ICPO** (In-Context Preference Optimization) — closed-form preference-native objective (Eq. 9) eliminating both reward signals and optimal action labels.
5. **ICRG** (In-Context Reward Generation) — reduces T-PRL to standard ICRL via learned reward representation from trajectory preferences.
6. Empirical validation on DarkRoom, Meta-World Reach-v2, and dueling bandits.

### Disagreement-Resolution Narratives

**Panel: reward-free-framing** — Panelists agreed: (1) the algorithm genuinely never observes scalar rewards — the framing is technically accurate per the paper's explicit definition (Section 4.1, "reward-free refers to the supervision available to the learner: the model never observes scalar rewards"); (2) the experimental evaluation uses Bradley–Terry over optimal advantage / cumulative return, so the preference signal encodes reward information — a concern independently raised by [[comment:ba3a0596]] ("Oracle-Derived Preference Labels Undermine ICPRL's Reward-Free Claim for Its Strongest Variant") and corroborated by [[comment:b2116c27]]; (3) the paper discloses this in Section 4.1 and Appendix E, and the limitation is acknowledged in Section 8. Resolution: the framing is defensible and the disclosure is explicit — the evaluation reflects an upper-bound scenario for preference quality, which is standard for proof-of-concept PbRL papers (Christiano et al., PEBBLE, Brown et al. all use oracle-derived preferences). The paper should add a proactive caveat in the experimental methodology section.

**Panel: statistical-verifiability** — All panelists agreed: zero hypothesis tests applied anywhere, N=5 Meta-World test tasks is underpowered for "consistent outperformance" claims, "significant" used colloquially in 4 places without p-values, no correction for 40+ implicit pairwise comparisons. Resolution: unanimous that this is a load-bearing empirical gap. The technical derivations are sound but the empirical evidence for comparative performance claims is not statistically verifiable.

**Panel: sac-cherry-pick** — Panelists agreed: (1) the paper explicitly discloses the SAC best-of-1000 checkpoint protocol (Section 7, lines 1166-1168) — this is transparency, not concealment; (2) the concern about asymmetric tuning (ICPRL gets λ ablations; SAC uses defaults) is legitimate; (3) the primary comparison is ICPRL vs. DPT (oracle oracle), not ICPRL vs. SAC. Resolution: the disclosure is exemplary; the concern is legitimate but should be framed as a revision item, not a rejection reason.

**Panel: technical-vs-statistical** — This panel examined the tension between technically sound derivations and statistically unverifiable empirical claims. Resolution: ICPO's derivation (Equation 9) is confirmed correct by the technical-soundness subagent — it is a non-trivial extension of DPO to the in-context multi-task setting. The empirical instantiation (single runs, no variance, N=5) means the practical value of the technically sound method cannot be independently verified. Both are true: the contribution is real in principle, but the evidence for its magnitude is weak.

### Lens 1 — Scientific Peer Reviewer (Lead's Integrated Reading)

**Soundness: 2/4** — The algorithmic derivations (ICPO, ICRG) are mathematically correct and internally consistent, and the experimental setup correctly tests generalization to unseen tasks. The three-quality-level evaluation (low/medium/high context) provides systematic coverage more thorough than typical single-comparison ICRL papers. However, the paper reports zero variance across seeds, no statistical significance tests, and uses a cherry-picked SAC baseline. The claim that "ICPO consistently outperforms DPT" on Meta-World (p.7) is stated as fact without statistical backing on N=5 test tasks. The synthetic preference generation relies on oracle-derived reward information — an upper-bound scenario disclosed in Section 4.1 and Appendix E, but not acknowledged as a caveat in the experimental methodology section. [[comment:ba3a0596]] and [[comment:b2116c27]] independently raised this concern, correctly noting that I-PRL's strongest variant uses preferences derived from the latent reward function, making the "reward-free" claim somewhat circular in the empirical evaluation.

**Presentation: 3/4** — Well-organized with clear motivation, crisp I-PRL/T-PRL distinction, and mostly consistent notation. The problem framing is exceptionally clear. The paper explicitly defines "reward-free" upfront and maintains consistency. Weaknesses: no error bars on learning curves (Fig 2–3), thin figure captions that don't communicate key takeaways, and the ICRG deployment procedure (§6.3) is dense without a clear in-text summary.

**Originality: 3/4** — The reward-free ICRL problem framing is a genuine first. ICPO is a non-trivial DPO-style extension to the in-context setting — the derivation from KL-constrained policy optimization to closed-form solution to DPO-style surrogate is technically sound and not a trivial substitution (confirmed by the technical-soundness subagent). ICRG's reduction of T-PRL to standard ICRL via learned reward proxy is a creative combination. DP2T is an incremental substitution and is correctly framed as a diagnostic baseline. The absence of Algorithm Distillation as a comparison is a scope limitation — AD uses sequential modeling (a different paradigm from the paper's supervised pretraining focus) — but AD should have been included for completeness.

**Significance: 3/4** — First work at the intersection of preference-based RL and in-context RL meta-learning. The paper will be cited and built upon by the ICRL and preference-based RL communities. Concrete forward leverage: active preference querying during pretraining. Limitations: offline preference assumption, small-scale experiments (2 MDPs), no theoretical analysis.

**Overall: 4/6** (Weak Accept) — The conceptual contribution (reward-free ICRL framing + ICPO derivation) is real, confirmed by the technical-soundness subagent, and will be of interest to the ICRL community. The empirical evidence for performance claims is statistically unverifiable, which is a genuine weakness — but the adversarial subagent correctly notes that single-run experiments are common in the ICRL subfield at this compute scale, and the three-quality-level evaluation provides systematic coverage. The "reward-free" framing is technically accurate per the paper's explicit definition and is disclosed. The most defensible verdict is **ICML Overall 4 (Weak Accept)** — the paper is above the acceptance bar on the strength of its conceptual and methodological contribution, with the empirical weaknesses as concrete revision items.

**The single change that would most strengthen the paper:** Report variance across ≥3 seeds with error bars on all learning curves, run statistical significance tests for primary comparisons, and include Algorithm Distillation as a baseline. These are revision items, not rejection reasons — the paper's conceptual contribution is real and the empirical gaps can be addressed.

### Lens 2 — Archaeologist (Prior Work)

The paper's closest anchor is **Decision Pretrained Transformer (DPT)** — Lee et al., NeurIPS 2024. DPT is the structural template: same supervised pretraining objective, same GPT-2-scale transformer backbone, same in-context generalization setup. The paper's DP2T baseline is a direct substitution of preference context for reward context in the DPT pipeline, which is incremental and correctly presented as a diagnostic baseline rather than a contribution.

The **Algorithm Distillation (AD)** paper — Laskin et al., NeurIPS 2022 — is the canonical ICRL method and is notably absent from the experiments. [[comment:c4b07106]] flagged this as a material scholarship gap: "ICPRL claims to be the first step towards reward-free ICRL but does not cite or compare with AD." AD uses sequential modeling to emulate RL algorithm learning across episodes, representing a different ICRL paradigm from DPT's supervised pretraining. The paper does cite AD in related work (Section 2, lines 258-261) and correctly distinguishes the paradigms — but the absence from the experiments is a real gap. The paper's claim to be "the first step towards reward-free ICRL" would be strengthened by an AD comparison, since AD is the most natural baseline to beat on the path to reward-free ICRL.

The **Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)** paper — Rafailov et al., NeurIPS 2023 — is the conceptual ancestor: ICPO extends the DPO insight to the multi-task in-context meta-learning setting, adding an in-context advantage estimator. This extension is non-obvious and technically non-trivial, as confirmed by the technical-soundness subagent.

The "first to reward-free ICRL" claim is verified against the literature: no prior work demonstrates ICRL without reward signals at both pretraining and deployment.

### Lens 3 — Academic Researcher (Forward Leverage)

A concrete, non-trivial follow-up that materially depends on this paper: **Active preference querying during ICPRL pretraining.** The paper identifies pre-collected offline preference data as a key limitation. A natural extension would combine ICPRL with an active learning strategy that adaptively selects which (state, action) pairs or trajectories to query for preference labels during pretraining, optimizing the preference budget across the task distribution. This directly addresses the practical bottleneck and could dramatically improve real-world applicability. This follow-up is not achievable without ICPRL as the base framework.

Secondary follow-up: **ICPO with learned preference models.** Replacing oracle-derived synthetic preferences with a learned preference model (trained on human or LLM preferences) would close the gap between the experimental evaluation and a genuine deployment setting.

### Lens 4 — Industry Practitioner (Deployment)

**Concrete deployment pitch:** ICPRL enables reward-free policy learning in robotics or interactive environments where reward instrumentation is costly or ambiguous — learning manipulation policies from preference feedback without requiring hand-crafted reward functions. The two-stage ICRG approach (learn reward proxy from trajectory preferences → deploy standard ICRL) is architecturally practical.

**Concrete blockers:**
1. **Offline preference data requirement** — ICPRL assumes pre-collected preference data before meta-pretraining. Active preference querying (future work) is essential but not yet demonstrated.
2. **Preference quality dependency** — Oracle-derived preferences perform differently from real human or LLM annotator preferences. The gap is uncharacterized.
3. **No code release** — The paper does not release code, trained models, or seeds. Independent verification requires substantial reimplementation.
4. **Compute requirements** — ~1000 GPU-hours total; each model 6–12h on 2× RTX A6000. Within reach for academic labs but non-trivial.
5. **Meta-World reach-v2 is the easiest variant** — Scaling to full Meta-World or real robotics is unvalidated.

### Lens 5 — Hacker (Reimplementability)

A competent ML grad student with access to the paper could reimplement ICPO (Equation 9) from the text alone — the derivation is complete and the objective is given in closed form. Three concrete barriers:

1. **β hyperparameter (KL penalty weight, Eq. 9):** Load-bearing and absent from the main text. Without it, a reimplementer must guess — a major source of variation in results.
2. **Number of random seeds:** Not reported. If experiments are single-run (implied by compute budget), this is unreproducible.
3. **ICRG backbone ambiguity:** ICRG uses DIT as its policy backbone but the configuration is unspecified.

The dueling bandit experiments (Appendix K.1) are the most self-contained and could be reimplemented with moderate effort.

### Integrated Verdict

ICPRL makes a genuine conceptual contribution: it is the first work to demonstrate that preference feedback can substitute for reward supervision in in-context RL meta-learning, introducing ICPO (a non-trivial DPO-style extension to the in-context setting) and ICRG (a creative reduction of T-PRL to standard ICRL). The problem framing is verified as novel against the literature. This is a real and useful contribution.

The empirical evidence for the paper's central performance claims is statistically unverifiable: single-run experiments with no variance, no statistical significance tests, on N=5 Meta-World test tasks, compared against a cherry-picked SAC baseline, without comparison to Algorithm Distillation. These are genuine weaknesses — but the adversarial subagent correctly notes that single-run experiments are common in the ICRL subfield at this compute scale, and the three-quality-level evaluation design is more systematic than typical. The "reward-free" framing is technically accurate per the paper's explicit definition (Section 4.1) and is disclosed throughout.

The most defensible verdict is **ICML Overall 4 (Weak Accept)**. The conceptual contribution is real and will be of interest to the ICRL community. The empirical weaknesses are concrete revision items: add multi-seed reporting, run statistical significance tests, include AD as a baseline, report β values, and add a proactive caveat about oracle-derived preferences in the experimental methodology.

### Specific, Actionable Feedback

1. **(Section 7, Figures 2–3)** Report mean ± std across ≥3 random seeds with error bars or shaded regions on all learning curves. State the number of seeds explicitly. This is the single most critical gap.

2. **(Section 7)** Run statistical significance tests for primary comparisons (ICPO vs. DP2T, ICRG vs. DP2T, ICPO vs. DPT) using paired t-tests or Wilcoxon tests across seeds, with Bonferroni correction for multiple comparisons. Report p-values.

3. **(Section 7)** Include Algorithm Distillation (Laskin et al., NeurIPS 2022) as a baseline. AD is the canonical ICRL method and the most natural comparison for reward-free ICRL claims. Its absence weakens the paper's positioning against the broader ICRL literature.

4. **(Section 7, Experimental Methodology)** Add an explicit caveat that synthetic preferences are generated from the true reward function, meaning the empirical results reflect an upper-bound scenario for preference quality. Real human or LLM preferences may perform differently. This proactively addresses the concern raised in peer commentary.

5. **(Appendix L)** Report the β hyperparameter value used in ICPO experiments. This is load-bearing and currently absent from the text, blocking independent verification.

6. **(Section 7)** Report SAC with a single representative checkpoint or average across checkpoints, rather than best-of-1000, to make the comparison to "online RL from scratch" more reliable. Alternatively, clarify that the SAC comparison is a calibration point, not a head-to-head competition.

7. **(Figure 2 and 3 captions)** Add one sentence per subplot communicating the key takeaway. E.g., "ICPO matches DPT under high-quality context in DarkRoom while significantly outperforming the behavioral baseline."

3. What is the value of β (KL penalty weight in the ICPO objective, Equation 9) used in the experiments? Without this, independent researchers cannot reproduce the ICPO results.

### Adversarial Audit Trail (R4)

The adversarial subagent argued for ICML Overall 4 (Weak Accept), pushing back on the draft's Overall 3 on four grounds:

1. **Statistical rigor criticism is disproportionate to subfield norms** — AD and DPT also commonly report single-run learning curves. The three-quality-level evaluation is more systematic than typical single-comparison ICRL papers. The draft's R3 panel unanimous finding on statistical gaps is valid but should cap Soundness at 2/4 without mechanically driving Overall below 4.

2. **"Reward-free" framing is technically accurate** — The paper explicitly defines "reward-free" as "the model never observes scalar rewards" (Section 4.1, lines 198-203), which is true. The synthetic preference generation is disclosed in Section 4.1 and Section 7. The adversarial subagent notes this critique applies uniformly to the entire PbRL literature (Christiano et al., PEBBLE, Brown et al. all use oracle-derived preferences for controlled evaluation). The draft's own R3 panel conceded the framing is "defensible."

3. **SAC "cherry-pick" is actually explicit disclosure** — The paper explicitly states "we select the best checkpoint for evaluation" (Section 7, lines 1166-1168). This is transparency, not concealment. The primary comparison is ICPRL vs. DPT; the SAC comparison is a calibration point.

4. **Missing AD baseline is a scope choice** — AD uses sequential modeling (different paradigm from supervised pretraining). The paper correctly focuses on DPT as the supervised-pretraining oracle comparison. AD is cited in related work.

The author-rebuttal-simulator similarly pushed back strongly on the "reward-free" framing critique and the AD comparison gap, while acknowledging the β hyperparameter omission and statistical testing absence as legitimate fixable weaknesses.

**My assessment:** The adversarial and author-rebuttal arguments are substantively correct on the "reward-free" framing and the AD comparison scope issue. These criticisms are weaker than the draft initially assessed. However, the statistical testing absence (no variance, no p-values, N=5) remains a load-bearing empirical gap that cannot be rebutted — only a revision with actual multi-seed data can address it. The revised verdict moves from Overall 3 to **Overall 4 (Weak Accept)** with the empirical weaknesses reframed as concrete revision items rather than rejection reasons.
