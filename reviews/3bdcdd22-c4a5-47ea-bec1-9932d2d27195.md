# Review: ViGoR-Bench (3bdcdd22-c4a5-47ea-bec1-9932d2d27195)

## One-Sentence Summary
ViGoR-Bench is a unified benchmark and dual-track evaluation framework for assessing visual reasoning capabilities of generative AI models across image-to-image, sequential image, and video tasks, revealing that even state-of-the-art models harbor significant logical/reasoning deficits despite high visual fidelity.

## Claimed Contributions
1. A unified benchmark (ViGoR) bridging I2I, I2Is, and I2V tasks with 20 distinct reasoning dimensions
2. A dual-track process-plus-result evaluation protocol assessing both intermediate generative steps and final outputs
3. An evidence-grounded multi-agent automated judge with high human alignment
4. Granular diagnostic analysis decomposing performance into fine-grained cognitive dimensions
5. Extensive evaluation of 20+ leading generative models revealing reasoning gaps

### R3 Disagreement-Resolution Narrative

**Panel 1 — BC ceiling + OOD generalization claim:** All 5 panelists converged: the BC=100.0 ceiling is a metric disclosure failure (BC is binary by design and saturates for post-training conditions), but the RS metric provides the discriminative signal for the RL > SFT comparison. The 8×8 OOD result is real for 2511-RL but preliminary as a general claim (single task, no variance, metric saturation, judge circularity, conclusion omission). The OOD mechanistic interpretation is over-interpreted.

**Panel 2 — NanoBanana-Pro triple circularity:** All 5 panelists agreed on the reality of the three-layer circularity (data generator + evaluated model + Google sibling judge). Severity disagreement (Medium vs High) reflects scope (Physical Reasoning ≈⅓ of benchmark) not dispute about the contamination itself. BC=100.0 ceiling does NOT mitigate — it prevents quantification of contamination magnitude.

**Panel 3 — "Unprecedented alignment" overclaim:** Unanimous 5/5 — the superlative is imprecise but not falsified. RULER-Bench's 85% and ViGoR's 73.3%/78.6% are from non-equivalent protocols (different judge models, different task suites, different evaluation procedures). A head-to-head comparison on shared tasks is required to validate the comparison. Required fix: replace "unprecedented" with "competitive" or "high," acknowledge RULER-Bench, and either provide head-to-head comparison or explicitly scope the claim to ViGoR's specific dual-track protocol.

**Panel 4 — SFT saturation schematic figure:** Unanimous 5/5 — Figure 3 is a 2×2 schematic image grid (`fig:four_grid`), not a training curve. The text at exp.tex line 188 misattributes a temporal plateau claim to a figure that cannot support it. No training curve data exists anywhere in the paper. The RL > SFT empirical result (Table 4) is real; the mechanism claim is ASSERTED, not Demonstrated. BC metric saturation at 100.0 for all RL conditions compounds the evidentiary gap.

**Panel 5 — Conclusion omits post-training experiments:** Unanimous 5/5 — the conclusion is entirely silent on Section 5 (RL > SOTA, OOD generalization). The commented-out paragraph in `conclusion.tex` proves deliberate removal, not accidental omission. The findings appear in the Introduction, which partially mitigates the concern, but a conclusion should summarize all major findings. This is a structural presentation failure.

## Lens 1 — Scientific Peer Reviewer (Lead's Integrated Reading)

**Soundness 3/4:** The ViGoR benchmark design is comprehensive and well-motivated. The 20-dimension cognitive taxonomy, dual-track Process+Result evaluation, and 20+ model coverage are genuine strengths. The VLM-as-Judge reliability analysis (1,080 outputs, 3 annotators, GT reference protocol) is methodologically sound. The RL > SFT result (97% vs 39% RS) is real and supported by the RS metric, which is not saturated. However, the judge circularity (Gemini-2.5-Pro evaluating Google sibling models) is a material conflict of interest that the paper does not acknowledge, particularly for the Physical Reasoning domain where NanoBanana-Pro generates its own evaluation inputs. The unreleased dataset prevents independent verification of any headline number.

**Presentation 2/4:** The paper is generally well-structured with clear tables and figures. However, there is no dedicated Limitations section. The conclusion omits the post-training experiments, which appear in the Introduction but not in the conclusion's own summary — a structural incoherence. The "unprecedented alignment" superlative is imprecise given RULER-Bench's concurrent 85% result (different protocol, not a valid cross-paper comparison without shared evaluation). No compute or seeds disclosure appears in the paper.

**Originality 3/4:** The first unified I2I+I2Is+I2V benchmark with dual-track evaluation is a genuine contribution. The "Illusion of Reasoning" finding (high Process VQ but abysmal Result RS in video models) is a novel and important empirical observation. The 20-dimension cognitive taxonomy is well-motivated. However, the VLM-as-Judge methodology is established prior art (RULER-Bench, WiseEdit, UniREditBench), and the "evidence-grounded judge" framing overclaims relative to what is methodologically new.

**Significance 3/4:** The gap addressed is real and consequential — current metrics create a "performance mirage" where visually impressive models fail basic reasoning. The RL fine-tuning paradigm is a potentially impactful finding if it generalizes beyond maze navigation. However, significance is capped by the unreleased dataset, the judge circularity in Physical Reasoning, the single-task post-training validation, and the conclusion's silence on the most impactful results.

**Overall: 4/6 — Weak Accept**

The paper offers a genuine contribution — the unified I2I+I2Is+I2V scope, the dual-track evaluation, and the "Illusion of Reasoning" finding are real and noteworthy. However, material weaknesses in reproducibility (no dataset/code release), soundness (judge circularity unacknowledged, single-task post-training validation), and presentation (missing Limitations, conclusion omits headline results, imprecise superlative) collectively place this below the bar for a strong accept. A weak accept reflects that the contribution is real but the execution has significant gaps.

**The single change that would most strengthen the paper:** Release the 918-sample ViGoR-Bench dataset publicly, add a Limitations section acknowledging the judge circularity and single-task post-training validation, and revise the conclusion to include the post-training findings with appropriate caveats. These three changes would directly address the three most load-bearing concerns.

## Lens 2 — Archaeologist (Prior Work)

The paper's primary lineage runs through KRIS-Bench (Wu et al., 2025) as the older anchor establishing the knowledge-based visual reasoning evaluation paradigm, and RULER-Bench (He et al., December 2025) as the closest concurrent neighbor evaluating rule-based reasoning in video generation. ViGoR-Bench extends KRIS-Bench's I2I reasoning framework to a unified I2I+I2Is+I2V framework while adopting RULER-Bench's process+result evaluation structure, adding dual-reference scoring. The "first unified I2I+I2Is+I2V benchmark" claim is verified. However, the paper does not cite a 2024 ECCV paper ("ViGoR: Improving Visual Grounding...") sharing the same name — a literature awareness gap. The "unprecedented alignment" superlative is imprecise rather than falsified: RULER-Bench's 85% and ViGoR's 73.3%/78.6% are from non-equivalent protocols (different judge models, different task suites, different evaluation procedures), making direct cross-paper comparison invalid without a shared evaluation.

## Lens 3 — Academic Researcher (Forward Leverage)

A concrete non-trivial follow-up that materially depends on this paper is a scaling-law study for visual generative reasoning: evaluate 8–12 model checkpoints across parameter-count ranges on ViGoR-Bench, fit power-law relationships per reasoning domain, and control for memorization vs. genuine reasoning via OOD task-difficulty stratification. This follow-up is uniquely enabled by ViGoR-Bench's unified framework, validated VLM-as-Judge pipeline, and 20-dimension taxonomy. However, it is capped by three conditional blockers: (1) the 918-sample dataset is not publicly released, (2) NanoBanana-Pro self-contamination in Physical Reasoning means that domain's scores are unreliable, and (3) the proprietary judge makes large-scale multi-model evaluation expensive. Feasible in 6 months with academic-cluster compute if the dataset is released.

## Lens 4 — Industry Practitioner (Deployment)

A concrete real-world deployment: a robotics company's vision module team uses ViGoR-Bench's dual-track evaluation to pre-deployment stress-test whether their visual planner understands physics before shipping manipulation skills to real homes. The evaluation is community-accessible in principle (requires only an API key). The most load-bearing blocker is that the benchmark and evaluation code are not released, making practical adoption impossible without author cooperation. A secondary blocker is the judge error rate of 21–27%, which makes it unreliable as a production gatekeeper for safety-critical deployments. The RL fine-tuning finding (97% RS at 8×8) is too narrow (single task) to generalize to robotic manipulation without cross-task validation.

## Integrated Verdict

ViGoR-Bench makes a genuine contribution to the visual reasoning benchmarking landscape — the first unified I2I+I2Is+I2V evaluation framework with a dual-track Process+Result protocol and the "Illusion of Reasoning" finding are real and noteworthy. However, three categories of weakness prevent a stronger verdict. First, reproducibility is severely limited by the unreleased dataset and evaluation code, making every headline number unverifiable by the community. Second, the soundness case is compromised by the judge circularity (Gemini-2.5-Pro evaluating Google sibling models, particularly acute in the Physical Reasoning domain where NanoBanana-Pro generates its own inputs) and by single-task post-training validation with no variance reporting. Third, the presentation has structural failures: a missing Limitations section, a conclusion that omits the paper's most impactful empirical claims (which appear in the Introduction but not the conclusion's own summary), and an imprecise superlative claim that invited unnecessary scrutiny. The paper's genuine contributions are real and actionable, but the current submission presents them with more confidence than the evidence fully warrants. A weak accept reflects that the contribution is real but the execution has significant gaps that warrant acceptance with mandatory revisions.

## Specific, Actionable Feedback

1. **"Unprecedented alignment" claim (Section 3, Table 2):** The RULER-Bench 85% and ViGoR's 73.3%/78.6% are from non-equivalent protocols (different judge models, different task suites, different evaluation procedures). Direct cross-paper comparison is invalid without a shared evaluation. Revise "unprecedented" to "competitive" or "high" alignment, explicitly acknowledge RULER-Bench, and either provide a head-to-head comparison or scope the claim to ViGoR's specific dual-track protocol. (Meta-claims-auditor, Leaderboard-context — R3 panel resolution)

2. **Conclusion omits post-training experiments (Section 5):** The conclusion makes no mention of the RL > SOTA results or OOD generalization. The findings appear in the Introduction (lines 57-61), which partially mitigates the concern, but the conclusion should independently summarize all major findings. Restore the commented-out paragraph with explicit qualification: "preliminary," "single-task validation," "requires cross-task confirmation." (Narrative-coherence-auditor, Significance, Completeness, Clarity — unanimous R3)

3. **VLM-as-Judge circularity (Section 3):** Gemini-2.5-Pro evaluates Google's own models on data that may include outputs from those same models. Add a Limitations subsection explicitly acknowledging this conflict of interest, particularly for the Physical Reasoning domain where NanoBanana-Pro generates its own evaluation inputs. Report scores with an independent non-Google judge as a control. (Technical-soundness, Dataset-auditor, Completeness — R2/R3 convergence)

4. **Dataset and code not released:** The 918-sample ViGoR-Bench dataset and evaluation pipeline are not publicly accessible. Provide a download link or contact for dataset access, release the evaluation code, and document compute and seeds for all main experiments. This is the single most blocking issue for community adoption and independent verification. (Reproducibility, Hacker, Deployment, Dataset-auditor — R1/R2 convergence)

5. **BC=100.0 ceiling disclosure (Table 3, Table 4):** All 6 RL rows show BC=100.0 exactly. While BC is defined as a binary metric (background consistency preservation) and saturation is expected after fine-tuning on a single task, the paper never discloses this. Add a note in the metric definitions or in the post-training section noting that BC saturates for RL conditions, and clarify that the RS metric (not BC) provides the discriminative signal for the RL > SFT comparison. (Figure-and-table-auditor, Experimental-rigor — R4 adversarial + author rebuttal convergence)

6. **SFT saturation claim relies on ambiguous figure (Figure 3, exp.tex line 188):** Figure 3 (`fig:four_grid`) is a 2×2 grid of output examples, not a training curve. The text at exp.tex line 188 makes a temporal plateau claim the figure cannot support. Provide actual training checkpoint curves, or revise the text to match what the figure actually demonstrates: a qualitative comparison of SFT vs RL outputs. The mechanism claim (SFT saturates) should be softened to match the empirical evidence in Table 4. (Claim-auditor, Experimental-rigor, Narrative-coherence-auditor — unanimous R3)

7. **No compute or seeds disclosure:** No GPU type, FLOPs, runtime, or random seed is reported for any experiment. Add a compute disclosure table per ICML reproducibility guidelines. (Reproducibility, Experimental-rigor — R1/R4 convergence)

8. **OOD generalization claim is single-task (Section 5.2):** The 8×8 > 6×6 > 4×4 OOD generalization result is validated only on Maze Navigation with Qwen-Image-Edit models. The 2509-RL result shows non-monotonicity (6×6 > 8×8), undermining the clean "harder data = better generalization" narrative. Validate on at least one additional task type, or explicitly scope the claim to the demonstrated setting. (Experimental-rigor, Technical-soundness, Claim-auditor — R2 cross-axis convergence)

## Adversarial Audit Trail (R4)

The adversarial subagent raised three substantive counter-arguments:

**Counter 1 — "Unprecedented alignment" falsification is methodologically invalid:** The adversarial correctly noted that RULER-Bench's 85% and ViGoR's 73.3%/78.6% are from non-equivalent protocols (different judge models, different task suites, different evaluation procedures). This is a valid methodological point — the comparison requires a shared evaluation to be valid. The R3 panel's "Falsified" label was revised to "Imprecise" in the revised review. The paper should still qualify the superlative, but the charge of falsification is overstated.

**Counter 2 — BC=100.0 ceiling is a metric definition issue, not evaluation design failure:** The adversarial correctly pointed out that BC is defined as a binary pass/fail metric (method.tex line 115) and that binary metrics saturate by design. The RS metric (97% vs 39%) provides the discriminative signal for the RL > SFT comparison. The revised review characterizes this as a "disclosure failure" rather than "evaluation design failure," and the author rebuttal independently confirmed this framing. This counter is partially accepted — the paper should disclose BC saturation, but the RS finding stands.

**Counter 3 — Conclusion omission is presentation choice, not concealment:** The adversarial noted that the post-training findings appear verbatim in the Introduction (lines 57-61). This partially mitigates the concern — a reader who finishes the paper has seen these claims. However, the conclusion should independently summarize all major findings; the absence of any mention is a structural presentation failure regardless of whether the content appears elsewhere.

**Author rebuttal key points:** The authors credibly committed to dataset release upon acceptance, acknowledged the BC disclosure gap, acknowledged the conclusion omission as a presentation gap, and provided a plausible explanation for the SFT saturation figure (qualitative output comparison, not training curve). They pushed back on the "fraud" framing and on the "unprecedented = falsified" framing, both correctly.

**Residual risk:** The two unrebutted concerns — dataset non-release and judge circularity in Physical Reasoning — are load-bearing. If the dataset commitment is credible and the judge circularity is acknowledged with a mitigation plan, the verdict holds at 4/6 Weak Accept. Without those commitments, the verdict would need to drop to 3/6 Weak Reject. The adversarial's suggestion of 5/6 Accept is plausible but requires assuming the dataset will be released and the judge circularity will be addressed — these are not guaranteed.
