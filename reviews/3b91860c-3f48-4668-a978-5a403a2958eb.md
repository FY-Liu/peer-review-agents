### One-Sentence Summary

APRIL introduces a 260,000-example supervised dataset pairing erroneous Lean proofs with compiler diagnostics, corrected proofs, and natural-language diagnoses; demonstrates that supervised finetuning on this data yields a 25× improvement in single-shot proof repair accuracy (1.1% → 27.4% for Qwen3-4B) and that a finetuned 4B model marginally exceeds the 32B Goedel-Prover-V2 baseline under the same evaluation protocol (27.4% vs 26.8%); the natural-language diagnosis component — a co-equal stated contribution — is not systematically evaluated for quality.

### Claimed Contributions

1. **APRIL dataset** (260,125 examples): erroneous Lean proof + Lean compiler diagnostic → corrected proof + natural-language diagnosis, constructed via a systematic four-operator mutation pipeline (theorem substitution, tactic swapping, line-level LLM-elicited completion, multi-line LLM-elicited completion).
2. **Joint repair+explanation training paradigm**: dual-task objective conditioning both tasks on the same compiler feedback signal.
3. **Empirical demonstration**: supervised finetuning on APRIL substantially improves single-shot repair accuracy across multiple model families; a finetuned Qwen3-4B marginally outperforms Goedel-Prover-V2-32B (27.4% vs 26.8%).

**Disagreement-Resolution Narrative:**

The R3 panel on the 4B-vs-32B framing (4 panelists, one incomplete Turn C) reached cross-axis consensus that: (1) APRIL is the paper's genuine, robust contribution; (2) the 25× improvement (1.1% → 27.4%) is the load-bearing empirical result; (3) the 4B-vs-32B comparison is valid as an internal ablation but compromised as a general model-comparison claim by two compounding issues — Goedel evaluated in an unfamiliar single-shot protocol that structurally underestimates its capability, and a 0.6pp margin without variance reporting; (4) the framing concern is a real, material experimental-rigor and clarity/completeness gap, not a fabrication or a novelty problem. The panel recommended specific editorial revisions: reframe the 4B-vs-32B claim as an ablation of compiler-feedback-conditioned single-shot repair, add variance reporting, and move the protocol caveat into the same sentence as the comparison claim.

The R4 adversarial pass identified three concerns that survived the author rebuttal: (a) the 27.4% success rate is the absolute empirical claim with no nearest-neighbor baseline to establish a task-difficulty floor; (b) the natural-language diagnosis task — a co-equal stated contribution — is essentially unevaluated; (c) the contamination risk between APRIL source corpora and baseline pretraining data is unresolved. The author rebuttal was strongest on the 4B-vs-32B framing (caveat present in §1), the synthetic error distribution (mutation design is realistic, downstream validation supports transfer), and the iterative evaluation design choice (deliberate to isolate repair capability). The author rebuttal conceded the variance reporting gap and the iterative evaluation expectation gap.

### Lens 1 — Scientific Peer Reviewer (Lead's Integrated Reading)

**Soundness: 3/4** — The experimental methodology is sound: compiler-verified objective metric, theorem-level split preventing leakage, multi-model family comparison, and one proper ablation (explanation loss). Three confirmed gaps reduce rigor below the "rigorous" threshold: (1) no multi-seed variance reporting — the 1.3pp margin (27.4% vs 26.8%) is uninterpretable as evidence of superiority, and the per-category subsets (144–200 examples) are too small for stable point estimates; (2) structurally unfair baseline comparison — the headline "4B outperforms 32B" compares a task-specific finetuned model against a general proof-generation model under a non-standard single-shot protocol; (3) explanation quality unevaluated — the natural-language diagnosis is a co-equal stated contribution but has no systematic quality evaluation (human or automatic), making the joint repair+explanation training claim partially unsupported. The paper discloses the §1 caveat but the abstract's ordering precedes the qualification.

**Presentation: 3/4** — The paper is clearly written with a well-motivated introduction arc, thorough methodology section, informative figures, and strong reproducibility disclosures. Minor presentation issues: Table 1 missing column headers, Table 2 label placed before caption, red-shaded Table 3 cells without a color legend, and no standalone Limitations paragraph in the main text. The appendix's "Unsuccessful Attempts" section compensates substantially.

**Originality: 3/4** — APRIL is the first large-scale supervised Lean proof-repair dataset with joint repair+explanation objectives. The underlying supervised error-correction paradigm draws from DrRepair (Yasunaga & Liang, ICML 2020) and Tfix (Berabi et al., ICML 2021) in software engineering; the delta is the formal verification domain, domain-specific mutation operators, and the dual repair+explanation objective. No novel algorithm, architecture, or theoretical framework is introduced.

**Significance: 3/4** — The paper addresses a real and central gap in neural theorem proving: the lack of feedback-conditioned training data for agentic provers. The demonstrated 25× improvement from supervised finetuning is substantial and credible within the paper's own evaluation framework. The single-shot evaluation setting does not match the paper's own stated motivation (iterative refinement is 32–128× more efficient per §1), which limits the practical significance demonstration for the specific use case the paper argues is most important. The dataset artifact is immediately useful to the community.

**Overall: 4/6** — A solid Weak Accept. The APRIL dataset is a genuine and useful contribution to the formal theorem proving community. The empirical gains are real but require substantial qualification, and the single-shot evaluation leaves the practical deployment claim incompletely demonstrated. The natural-language diagnosis task — a co-equal stated contribution — is essentially unevaluated, which is a meaningful gap given its prominence in the paper's stated contributions. The intellectual honesty about unsuccessful data-synthesis attempts is commendable.

**The single change that would most strengthen the paper:** Report multi-seed variance (≥3 seeds) for all main comparative results, enabling interpretation of whether the 1.3pp margin over Goedel-32B is within noise, and add systematic explanation quality evaluation (human evaluation or automatic metrics) for the natural-language diagnosis component.

### Lens 2 — Archaeologist (Prior Work)

APRIL occupies a specific niche at the intersection of two lineages: the supervised program repair lineage (DrRepair, Yasunaga & Liang, ICML 2020; Tfix, Berabi et al., ICML 2021) and the neural theorem proving lineage (Goedel-Prover-V2, Lin et al., 2025; SeedProver, Chen et al., 2025). DrRepair established that compiler-feedback-conditioned supervised repair is viable for Python/C++; Tfix extended this to C with a text-to-text transformer. APRIL is the first to apply this paradigm to Lean formal proofs with domain-specific mutation operators and a joint repair+explanation objective. The newer neighbor, SeedProver (2025), demonstrates that iterative proof refinement using compiler feedback is 32–128× more efficient than pass@k — this motivates APRIL's goal but also highlights the gap: APRIL evaluates only single-shot repair, not the iterative setting that motivates the work. The older anchor (DrRepair) provides the methodological template; APRIL's contribution is adapting this template to formal mathematics and demonstrating its viability at scale.

### Lens 3 — Academic Researcher (Forward Leverage)

The most concrete follow-up project enabled by APRIL is **repair-during-search integration**: using APRIL-trained models as feedback-conditioned repair operators within a Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) proof search framework. The paper shows that explanation-conditioned models produce natural-language diagnoses alongside formal repairs, and that these diagnoses improve downstream model performance (DeepSeek succeeds at 29% with APRIL-trained explanations vs. 4% without). A graduate student could integrate an APRIL-finetuned 4B model as the repair operator in an MCTS prover, use the natural-language diagnoses as search heuristics, and evaluate whether this combination outperforms standard proof planning alone. This follow-up is directly enabled by the dataset release and the joint repair+explanation training paradigm — neither existed before APRIL.

### Lens 4 — Industry Practitioner (Deployment)

For a practitioner working in formal verification or proof engineering, APRIL is valuable primarily as a **dataset and fine-tuned model weight release** rather than a deployable system. The 27.4% single-shot repair accuracy means that roughly 1 in 4 erroneous proofs is corrected in a single attempt — useful as a productivity aid but not sufficient for autonomous operation. Key blockers for autonomous deployment: (1) single-shot accuracy is below the reliability threshold for fully automated pipelines; (2) no failure-mode characterization is provided (what kinds of errors does the model systematically fail on?); (3) the synthetic error distribution may not match real proof-engineering errors, creating a distribution gap in production; (4) licensing terms for the released model weights are not clearly stated. The most practical near-term use case is as a **Lean IDE plugin** that suggests repairs and diagnoses for proof engineers, where human oversight provides the reliability floor.

### Lens 5 — Hacker (Reimplementability)

A competent ML graduate student could reimplement the core contribution — the APRIL dataset construction and the supervised finetuning pipeline — from the paper text alone in approximately one to two weeks. The mutation operators are described with sufficient specificity (theorem substitution using LeanExplore neighbors, tactic swapping within equivalence classes, line/multi-line mutation via DeepSeek-V3-0324 with compiler validation). The finetuning setup uses standard LoRA with disclosed hyperparameters (rank=32, lr=1e-4, batch=8, bfloat16). The primary opaqueness is the loss weighting between repair and explanation objectives, which is not disclosed — this makes exact reproduction of the 27.4% vs 31.2% trade-off figure impossible without guessing. The dataset itself is released on HuggingFace, which eliminates the need for reimplementation for anyone with access to the dataset.

### Lens 7 — Social Impact Assessor

The paper presents a positive social impact: improving neural theorem provers' ability to interpret and repair proof errors advances formal verification technology, which has safety-critical applications in software verification, cryptographic protocol validation, and mathematical research tooling. No direct negative social impacts are identified. The energy cost of training the APRIL models (disclosed as NVIDIA L40S and H200 GPUs, bfloat16) is reasonable for the model sizes involved (4B–8B parameters). The primary ethical consideration is the use of DeepSeek-V3-0324 as the error-generating model for line/multi-line mutations — if DeepSeek-V3-0324 was trained on data that includes Lean proofs from the same sources as APRIL, there is a potential contamination concern that is not addressed.

### Integrated Verdict

APRIL is a valuable dataset artifact for the neural theorem proving community: 260,125 supervised proof-repair tuples with joint repair+explanation objectives, constructed via a sound compiler-validated mutation pipeline and released publicly. The demonstrated 25× improvement from supervised finetuning (1.1% → 27.4%) is a genuine empirical finding that establishes the viability of feedback-conditioned supervision for formal proof repair. The paper's primary weaknesses are: (1) the headline 4B-vs-32B comparison is structurally unfair (Goedel evaluated in an unfamiliar single-shot protocol) and statistically uninterpretable (no variance reporting on a 1.3pp margin); (2) the natural-language diagnosis task — a co-equal stated contribution — is essentially unevaluated for quality or helpfulness; (3) the single-shot evaluation setting does not match the iterative-refinement setting the paper uses to motivate its practical importance. These are methodological and completeness gaps that warrant a Weak Accept rather than a stronger acceptance. The APRIL dataset artifact is the load-bearing contribution and is robustly supported; the empirical case for why the dataset matters is qualified by the above gaps.

### Specific, Actionable Feedback

1. **§Abstract / §1 (Framing):** The 4B-vs-32B comparison should be explicitly framed as "a finetuned 4B model marginally exceeds the 32B Goedel-Prover-V2 baseline under the same single-shot repair protocol (27.4% vs 26.8%, single-run)" rather than the current framing that leads with "outperforms" before the caveat. Move the protocol caveat into the same sentence as the comparison claim.

2. **§5 (Results):** Report multi-seed variance (≥3 seeds) for all main comparative results in Table 2. At minimum, report standard deviation or 95% confidence intervals for the headline 27.4% vs 26.8% comparison to establish whether the margin is statistically meaningful. This is standard practice in the neural theorem proving sub-field.

3. **§5.3 (Explanation quality):** Add systematic evaluation of natural-language diagnosis quality. Options include human evaluation of diagnosis accuracy and helpfulness (preferred), or automatic metrics (BERTScore, BLEU against ground-truth diagnoses) if human evaluation is infeasible. At minimum, report the accuracy of diagnosis generation (does the diagnosis correctly identify the error type and location?) alongside the repair accuracy.

4. **§5 (Results):** Add iterative repair evaluation (e.g., pass@k with k≥10) alongside the single-shot results to directly support the practical claim made in §1 that iterative refinement is 32–128× more efficient. Without this, the practical significance for proof development workflows is incompletely demonstrated.

5. **§3.1 (Data Collection):** Add a comparison of the APRIL error distribution against a sample of real human proof errors (if obtainable from mathlib development logs) to validate the claim that mutation errors "imitate errors that arise from subtle semantic mismatches."

6. **§5.3 (Explanation Ablation):** Disclose the relative weighting between repair loss and explanation loss in the joint training objective (the authors confirmed it is 1.0:1.0 in the author rebuttal), or provide an ablation across multiple weightings, to make the 3.8pp trade-off (27.4% → 31.2%) interpretable and reproducible.

7. **§5, Table 1:** Add an explicit column-header row inside the tabular environment for the five data columns (Raw, Filtered, Avg. Lines, Avg. have, Contain have), not only in the caption.

### Questions for Authors

1. The paper motivates the work with the claim that "iterative proof refinement using compiler feedback is 32–128× more efficient than pass@k" (citing SeedProver 2025), yet all evaluations are single-shot. Why was iterative repair not evaluated, and what would be required to demonstrate this efficiency claim with the APRIL-trained models?

2. What is the relative weighting between the repair loss and the explanation loss in the joint training objective? Without this, the 3.8pp trade-off from adding explanation supervision is not independently reproducible or interpretable.

3. Was any contamination check performed between the APRIL source datasets (Herald, Lean Workbook, NuminaMath-Lean) and the pretraining corpora of the baseline models (Goedel-Prover, Kimina-Prover, Qwen3)? If any of the baseline models were trained on APRIL source data, the baseline comparisons would be confounded.

The author rebuttal successfully defended the 4B-vs-32B framing (caveat present in §1), the synthetic error distribution (mutation design is realistic, downstream validation supports transfer), and the single-shot evaluation as a deliberate design choice. The author rebuttal conceded the variance reporting gap (cannot be retroactively produced) and the iterative evaluation gap (legitimate concern but defensible as scope). The adversarial's strongest surviving point is the explanation quality gap — the natural-language diagnosis is a co-equal stated contribution that is essentially unevaluated, and the draft's verdict does not fully account for this as a load-bearing concern. The residual risk that the verdict is wrong is low for the APRIL dataset artifact and the 25× improvement; moderate for the 4B-vs-32B comparison (depends on variance); and moderate for the practical deployment claim (depends on iterative evaluation and explanation quality).
