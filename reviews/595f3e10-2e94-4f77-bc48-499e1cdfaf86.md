## One-Sentence Summary

SSR (Self-expressive Sequence Regularization) is a training-free, plug-and-play operator for streaming 3D reconstruction that enforces Grassmannian manifold consistency on latent subspace representations during inference, using an analytical affinity matrix computed from the self-expressive property to pull noisy predictions back toward manifold-consistent trajectories and combat geometric drift.

---

## Claimed Contributions

1. **Grassmannian manifold framing** of latent persistent states in streaming 3D reconstruction as a novel theoretical lens for understanding and addressing geometric drift.
2. **SSR operator** — a training-free, plug-and-play method that enforces temporal coherence by regularizing current updates using an analytical affinity matrix computed from historical state windows.
3. **Comprehensive evaluation** on long-sequence benchmarks (ScanNet, 7-Scenes, RGB-D Streaming) demonstrating consistent drift reduction and improved reconstruction quality.

### Disagreement-Resolution Narrative

**Panel 1 — "Consistently reduces drift" overclaim:** The claim-auditor, narrative-coherence-auditor, figure-and-table-auditor, and technical-soundness subagents convened. All four agreed the abstract/conclusion language is imprecise: "consistently" implies universal coverage, but the body text uses "most metrics" where regressions appear. The figure-and-table-auditor identified a direct self-contradiction: Table 4 NC shows 0.625 (SSR) vs 0.641 (CUT3R baseline) — a regression. The technical-soundness subagent noted that affinity matrix degradation under sparse temporal context is structurally inevitable, making pose regressions unsurprising. However, the adversarial subagent and author rebuttal both correctly noted that the paper's primary task — video depth estimation — shows consistent improvement across ALL three datasets in Table 1 (Sintel: 38% reduction, Bonn: 22%, KITTI: 8%). Crucially, the paper explicitly discusses the sparse-input failure mode in §4.3 — this is an honest, self-initiated limitation analysis, not concealment. **Resolution:** The "consistently" claim is defensible for depth estimation but overclaimed for pose. Recommendation: "SSR reduces drift under sufficient temporal context in most evaluated depth and pose estimation settings."

**Panel 2 — Table 5 identical k=16/k=32 values:** The risk-of-fraud-detector, technical-soundness, and experimental-rigor subagents convened. All three agreed the most parsimonious explanation is a single copy-paste event (k=16 row copied to k=32 row), enabled by single-run reporting without variance. The panel unanimously agreed this does not warrant escalation to program chairs — the anomaly is confined to an ablation table. The risk-of-fraud-detector maintained "Concerning anomaly" labeling while technical-soundness and experimental-rigor classified it as "reporting flaw." **Resolution:** Copy-paste error in ablation table; main results (Tables 1-4) are unaffected. Author rebuttal claims these are genuine plateaus, which is theoretically plausible but unverifiable without per-seed data.

**Panel 3 — S=CS "enforces" framing gap:** The claim-auditor, technical-soundness, and hacker subagents convened. All three agreed on the factual finding: S=CS is motivational scaffolding, not an enforced constraint. The algorithm computes C via a fixed similarity function (Eq 9), not by solving the self-expressiveness optimization. However, the hacker subagent explicitly found this is "NOT a reimplementation blocker" — Algorithm 1 + Equations 9-10 are self-contained and recoverable. The adversarial subagent correctly noted this contradicts using the framing gap as a primary soundness justification. **Resolution:** Replace "enforces" with "motivated by" or "inspired by" throughout abstract, intro, and Section 3.3. The algorithmic core is sound; the framing is imprecise.

## Lens 1 — Scientific Peer Reviewer (Lead's Integrated Reading)

**Soundness: 2/4** — The experimental design is sound across 10+ datasets and three task types (depth, pose, 3D reconstruction). The algorithmic core (Algorithm 1 + Equations 9-10) is self-contained and produces real empirical improvements (Table 1: 38% depth error reduction on Sintel). However, three material gaps prevent a higher rating: (1) absent variance reporting across all tables prevents assessment of statistical significance; (2) the "consistently reduces drift" claim is imprecise for pose estimation where regressions exist; (3) Table 5's copy-paste error, while confined to an ablation table, reflects a reporting discipline gap that affects trust in the numerical claims.

**Presentation: 3/4** — The paper is well-structured with clear problem motivation, theoretical framing, and experimental validation. The Grassmannian mathematics is accessible to CV practitioners. The limitation discussion in §4.3 is notably honest and self-initiated — a scientific integrity strength. The one presentation weakness is the abstract/conclusion tension on "consistently" without qualification.

**Originality: 3/4** — The Grassmannian manifold framing for streaming 3D reconstruction is a fresh theoretical perspective connecting two subcommunities (NRSfM and streaming 3D reconstruction). The self-expressive property for affinity computation at inference time is novel — prior work (Kumar et al. 2018) established the foundation, but operationalizing it for online streaming inference without gradients is new. The training-free mechanism is genuinely novel relative to gradient-based TTT3R (ICLR 2026).

**Significance: 2.5/4** — The training-free, plug-and-play design addresses a real practical need in latency-constrained robotics and AR/VR deployment. The depth estimation improvements are substantial and genuine. However, the single-backbone evaluation limits the scope of the "plug-and-play" claim, and the pose regressions in Table 2 (while marginal) temper enthusiasm for the method's generality.

**Overall: 3/6 (Weak Reject)** — The paper's core empirical contribution (training-free depth improvement with substantial gains on canonical benchmarks) is genuine and valuable. However, the combination of absent variance reporting, imprecise "consistently" claim, and single-backbone evaluation means the paper falls short of acceptance at ICML. With qualified claims ("under sufficient temporal context"), multi-seed reporting, and multi-backbone validation, this would be a clear accept. As written, the reporting gaps and overclaiming prevent a positive verdict.

**The single change that would most strengthen the paper:** Add mean±std across ≥3 seeds for all tables, and revise the abstract/conclusion to qualify "consistently reduces drift" with task-conditional language (e.g., "in most evaluated depth estimation settings under sufficient temporal context").

## Lens 2 — Archaeologist (Prior Work)

From the archaeologist's R1+R2 reports: The paper builds on Kumar et al. CVPR 2018 (Self-expressiveness as a manifold prior) and connects to the streaming 3D reconstruction literature (CUT3R, MASt3R, MonST3R, TTT3R). SSR is the first to operationalize the self-expressive property at inference time without gradient-based optimization. The Grassmannian framing connects two subcommunities that rarely speak to each other: NRSfM (subspace learning) and streaming 3D reconstruction (online depth/pose estimation). This is intellectually valuable even if the specific mechanism (affinity matrix computation) is a direct application of Kumar's framework to a new domain.

## Lens 3 — Academic Researcher (Forward Leverage)

From forward-leverage's report: The most concrete follow-up is multi-backbone validation — demonstrating SSR on Point3R and Spann3R to establish the plug-and-play generality claim. A second natural follow-up is theoretical analysis of the window-size convergence properties: the paper shows k=16 reaches a plateau in Table 5, but the theoretical conditions under which longer windows stop helping are not characterized. A third follow-up is extending SSR to other streaming perception tasks (tracking, action recognition) where temporal drift is a known issue.

## Lens 4 — Industry Practitioner (Deployment)

From deployment's report: The training-free, gradient-free, parameter-free design is the paper's strongest practical feature. For latency-sensitive robotics deployment (e.g., drone navigation, AR hand tracking), not needing to run backpropagation at test time is a substantial practical advantage. The method adds minimal computational overhead (affinity matrix computation is O(n²) per frame) and integrates as a drop-in operator on top of any recurrent 3D reconstruction backbone. The single-backbone evaluation is a weakness for the "plug-and-play" claim, but the mechanism itself (affinity-based temporal regularization) is domain-agnostic and could generalize. The main deployment blockers are: (1) lack of theoretical convergence guarantees (when does SSR provably help vs. hurt?), (2) sparse-input failure mode is not addressed, and (3) no real-world robotics deployment results.

## Lens 5 — Hacker (Reimplementability)

From hacker's report: A competent ML grad student could reimplement the SSR algorithm from Algorithm 1 + Equations 9 and 10 in a weekend. The algorithm is self-contained: given a window of historical states, compute affinity via the self-expressive property, then regularize the current update. The main reimplementation gap is the CUT3R Interaction module specification — the paper does not describe how CUT3R's Interaction module works, which is needed to understand where SSR plugs in. This is a documentation gap, not an algorithmic gap. The Grassmannian framing is mathematically sound and correctly cites Kumar et al. 2018.

## Integrated Verdict

SSR is a method with a genuinely novel training-free mechanism and substantial empirical improvements on the primary task (depth estimation). The Grassmannian manifold framing connects two subcommunities in an intellectually valuable way, and the training-free design addresses a real practical need in latency-constrained deployment. However, three load-bearing concerns prevent acceptance: (1) absent variance reporting across all tables prevents assessment of statistical significance for marginal improvements; (2) the "consistently reduces drift" claim is overclaimed for pose estimation where regressions exist; (3) the single-backbone evaluation does not support the "plug-and-play" generality claim. The R3 panels resolved the most contentious issues (Table 5 anomaly as copy-paste error, S=CS framing as imprecise but not algorithmic failure), but the reporting gaps and overclaiming remain material barriers. With qualified claims and multi-seed reporting, this would be a clear accept.

## Specific, Actionable Feedback

1. **§Abstract, §Conclusion — Qualify "consistently reduces drift":** Replace with task-conditional language: "SSR reduces drift under sufficient temporal context in most evaluated depth and pose estimation settings." The pose regressions in Table 2 (Sintel ATE, RPE trans/rot, 7-Scenes NC) are real and documented in §4.3, but the conclusion's unqualified "consistently" overstates the claim.

2. **All tables — Add variance reporting:** Report mean±std across ≥3 seeds for all tables. This is the single most impactful improvement for reproducibility and for assessing whether marginal improvements (e.g., KITTI depth 0.118→0.109) are statistically meaningful. The paper's own R3 panel identified this as a material gap.

3. **§3.3–3.4, §Abstract — Replace "enforces" with "motivated by":** The S=CS constraint is motivational scaffolding, not an enforced computational constraint. The algorithm (Algorithm 1 + Equations 9-10) computes affinity via a fixed similarity function, not by solving the self-expressiveness optimization. Use "motivated by" or "inspired by" throughout.

4. **Table 5 — Verify and clarify k=16/k=32 values:** The author rebuttal claims these are genuine plateaus, but without per-seed data this is unverifiable. Verify the raw numbers, provide per-seed data in an appendix, and clarify whether the plateau is a real finding or a copy-paste error. If genuine, the plateau is a meaningful finding (method is robust to window size beyond k=16).

5. **§4.3 — Elevate the sparse-input limitation to the abstract:** The sparse-input failure mode (Table 3, §4.3) is a material limitation that affects the scope of the "consistently reduces drift" claim. Summarize it in the abstract rather than burying it in the body.

6. **CUT3R Interaction module — Add specification in appendix:** The paper does not describe CUT3R's Interaction module, which is needed for reimplementation. Add a brief description or cite the CUT3R paper's specification.

7. **Multi-backbone validation — Commit to camera-ready:** The "plug-and-play" claim requires demonstration on at least one additional backbone (Point3R or Spann3R). Commit to this in the revision plan.

## Questions for Authors

1. For the marginal improvements (KITTI depth: 0.118→0.109, ~8% reduction), what is the standard deviation across seeds? Are these improvements statistically significant at p<0.05?

2. Can you clarify whether the k=16=k=32 identical values in Table 5 are (a) a copy-paste preparation error, (b) a genuine plateau, or (c) a single-run artifact? Per-seed data would resolve this ambiguity.

3. The paper claims SSR is "plug-and-play" but validates only on CUT3R. What is the expected behavior when SSR is applied to a structurally different backbone (e.g., a non-recurrent, frame-by-frame approach)? Are there conditions under which SSR would hurt rather than help?

## Adversarial Audit Trail (R4)

The adversarial subagent mounted five counter-arguments: (1) "Consistently" is defensible for the primary depth task; (2) single-seed reporting is standard practice in CV; (3) S=CS framing is motivational, not a soundness failure; (4) Table 5 anomaly is a copy-paste error, not a credibility issue; (5) training-free design is genuinely novel and valuable. The author rebuttal reinforced these points while conceding the variance reporting gap.

**Did the adversarial counter-argument move the verdict?** Partially. The original draft assigned Overall 3/6 primarily on soundness grounds (S=CS framing gap as algorithmic failure, "consistently" as concealment). The adversarial subagent correctly identified that the R3 panels themselves reached nuanced conclusions that the draft flattened: the hacker subagent found the algorithm is NOT a reimplementation blocker, and Panel 2 found the Table 5 anomaly does not warrant chair escalation. These findings should have moderated the soundness assessment. The revised verdict maintains Overall 3/6 but with a more accurate characterization: the reporting gaps (variance) and overclaiming ("consistently") are the real barriers, not the algorithmic framing.

**Residual risk:** The verdict could be wrong if (1) the pose regressions in Table 2 are shown to be statistically significant across multiple seeds (making "consistently" harder to defend even for depth), or (2) the Table 5 anomaly extends to main-result tables (which the R3 panel found unlikely but could not definitively rule out). As things stand, the core empirical contribution (depth improvement in Table 1) is robust and the algorithmic core is sound. The paper needs revision on reporting rigor and claim precision, not on the fundamental contribution.
