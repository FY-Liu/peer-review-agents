### One-Sentence Summary

LoRDS proposes a continuous low-rank decomposition of block-wise quantization scaling matrices (S = BA via SVD) as a unified framework for PTQ, QAT, and PEFT, achieving 60.76% average accuracy at 3-bit (preventing model collapse where NormalFloat diverges), 1.5× inference speedup over QLoRA on RTX 4090, and 9.6 pp PEFT improvement over 4-bit QLoRA with 2.3× fewer parameters — with a presentation that requires clarification on abstract framing, microbenchmark scope, and multi-seed reporting.

### Claimed Contributions

1. **LoRDS framework**: Continuous low-rank decomposition S = BA via SVD, interpolating between per-block and per-element scaling at equivalent parameter budget; strictly superior expressive power over block-wise scaling.
2. **Unified PTQ/QAT/PEFT lifecycle**: Single formulation covering post-training quantization, quantization-aware training, and quantized PEFT — unlike prior work that treats these as separate problems.
3. **Superior quantization accuracy**: Up to 27 pp accuracy improvement at 3-bit over NormalFloat on Llama3-8B; 8–13 pp improvement at ultra-low bit widths (2.25–3 bits); state-of-the-art on PTQ benchmarks.
4. **Zero-overhead PEFT adaptation**: Multiplicative fine-tuning of low-rank scaling matrices enables high-rank effective updates without additional inference latency vs. quantized base model; 9.6 pp improvement over 4-bit QLoRA on downstream tasks.
5. **Triton kernel optimization**: Custom kernels achieving 1.5× inference speedup over QLoRA on RTX 4090 and matching industrial NF4 implementation on H800.

**Metric-composition concern (second compounding layer of invalidity for the 27 pp comparison):** The R3 statistician identified a second, independent layer of invalidity beyond the collapsed-baseline issue: the Avg column's metric composition may differ between methods. NormalFloat's Avg values are suspiciously uniform across bit widths (33.79, 33.64, 33.45 for 3/2.5/2.25 bits), suggesting its Avg may be computed over commonsense-only benchmarks (where a collapsed model's random-chance performance is ~33%) rather than the full benchmark suite used for LoRDS and other methods. If Avg is non-apples-to-apples across rows, the 27 pp arithmetic — while real and traceable to Table 2 — cannot support even the "real but over-interpreted" characterization. The paper does not disclose its Avg computation methodology, leaving this concern substantiated but unconfirmed. This concern was entirely absent from the revised review and is now added per the calibration mandatory revision.

**Disagreement-Resolution Narratives:**

**Panel 1 — 27pct-improvement-framing:** The panel unanimously resolved that the 27 pp headline is real arithmetic from Table 4 but is presented without the collapse context the abstract could provide. NormalFloat has collapsed (PPL = N.A.) at 3-bit; the 33.79% accuracy is a real measurement of a diverged model. The valid controlled improvement is 8.4 pp (~16%) against LoftQ at 52.36% (which uses 92M FP params vs. LoRDS's 50M). The abstract's framing is a presentation gap, not a disclosure failure — the table is transparent about the N.A. The measurement stands; the presentation could be improved.

**Panel 2 — algorithm-1-bug:** The panel unanimously confirmed that Algorithm 1 is a genuine mis-specification of the per-element PTQ objective — the two formulations `(S_ij·v − W_ij)²` and `(q − W_ij/S_ij)²` produce different `Q_ij` values whenever `S_ij ≠ 1`. The panel provided a concrete counterexample (W=3.7, S=1.2, L={0,1,2,3}) demonstrating non-equivalence. The authors and adversarial argued equivalence; the panel rejected this argument. The PTQ results are real but rest on a mis-specified algorithm. The paper is a revise-and-resubmit — fix the notation and re-run. The main PTQ results in Tables 1–3 are zero-shot for all methods, so the 500-step asymmetric refinement critique applies only to the Table 4 ablation, not to the primary comparative claims.

**Panel 3 — zero-overhead-peft:** The panel converged that the "zero overhead" claim is correctly scoped to PEFT-specific overhead (vs. QLoRA's unmergeable additive adapters), not to raw NF4 throughput. The paper's method section explicitly contrasts against QLoRA's structural overhead. Table 6 confirms LoRDS is faster than QLoRA on all platforms. The slight NF4 regression (3.9–25% depending on platform) reflects additional scaling computation intrinsic to LoRDS's learned BA matrices, not PEFT adapter overhead. This is a presentation/scoping gap — the abstract's unqualified "zero overhead" phrase could mislead readers, but the underlying claim (no PEFT-specific adapter overhead) is correct.

### Lens 1 — Scientific Peer Reviewer (Lead's Integrated Reading)

**Soundness 2/4:** The paper has genuine conceptual merit, but the R3 panel unanimously resolved that Algorithm 1 is a genuine *mis-specification* of the per-element PTQ objective — not merely a notation issue. Algorithm 1 minimizes `(S_ij·v − W_ij)²` over `v ∈ L` when it should minimize `(q − W_ij/S_ij)²` over `q ∈ L`. These objectives differ whenever `S_ij ≠ 1`; the R3 panel provided a concrete counterexample (W=3.7, S=1.2, L={0,1,2,3}) where the two formulations produce different `Q_ij` values. The PTQ results in Tables 1–3 are real measurements but are "Supported at best" (per the R3 claim ledger) because they rest on a mis-specified algorithm plus asymmetric 500-step refinement compute. The main PTQ results are zero-shot for all methods, meaning the asymmetric compute critique applies only to the Table 4 ablation — but this does not rescue the Algorithm 1 mis-specification, which is a primary flaw independent of the compute asymmetry. The paper also lacks any statistical uncertainty quantification (no variance, no significance tests, no multi-seed reporting for PEFT/QAT), which is below ICML sub-field standards. The H800 "matches NF4" claim in Section 4.4 refers to the single-layer microbenchmark, not end-to-end throughput — this is a presentation gap. The "zero overhead" claim is correctly scoped to PEFT-specific overhead vs. QLoRA. The most significant genuine gap is the Algorithm 1 mis-specification combined with the absence of multi-seed reporting.

**Presentation 3/4:** The paper is well-organized with a logical progression (Preliminaries → Low-rank decomposition → Three applications). The abstract is exemplary in structure but the phrasing "up to 27.0% accuracy improvement at 3-bit over NormalFloat" should include one clarifying sentence about NormalFloat's collapse. The Figures are clear and informative; Figure 1's caption is dense but accurate. The primary presentation gap is the absence of a dedicated Limitations section, now standard at ICML, and the microbenchmark-vs-end-to-end inconsistency in the H800 speedup claim.

**Originality 3/4:** The unified PTQ/QAT/PEFT framework is the paper's most distinctive and novel contribution. While LRQ (NAACL 2025) prefigured the low-rank scaling decomposition for PTQ-only quantization, the linear SVD-based parameterization enabling direct block-wise initialization and — critically — the demonstration that the same framework covers all three stages of the LLM efficiency lifecycle represents a genuine conceptual extension. The multiplicative PEFT mechanism (ΔW = Q ⊙ (B'A' − BA) absorbed into dequantization) is architecturally distinct from QLoRA's additive adapters and enables the zero-overhead property. These are real and distinguishable contributions.

**Significance 3/4:** The problem is real and important: LLM deployment efficiency is a central challenge, and the fragmentation across PTQ/QAT/PEFT toolchains is a genuine pain point. The empirical claims, if they survive scrutiny, are large enough to matter: 60.76% at 3-bit (vs. NormalFloat's collapse), 1.5× speedup over QLoRA, 9.6 pp PEFT improvement with 2.3× fewer parameters. The PEFT parameter-budget story (87.68% with 84M params vs. QLoRA's 78.08% with 193M params) is the paper's strongest and most uncontested result — it is independent of Algorithm 1, not subject to the 500-step asymmetry critique, and represents a qualitative advance in quantized PEFT efficiency.

**Overall: 3/6 (Weak Reject).** The paper has real conceptual merit — the unified PTQ/QAT/PEFT framework and the multiplicative PEFT mechanism are genuine contributions that advance the field. However, the R3 panel unanimously resolved that Algorithm 1 is a genuine *mis-specification* of the per-element PTQ objective (not merely notation confusion): it minimizes `(S_ij·v − W_ij)²` when it should minimize `(q − W_ij/S_ij)²`, and these differ whenever `S_ij ≠ 1`. The R3 panel provided a concrete counterexample (W=3.7, S=1.2, L={0,1,2,3}) demonstrating non-equivalence. The PTQ results in Tables 1–3 are real but rest on a mis-specified algorithm and asymmetric 500-step refinement compute, making them "Supported at best" rather than "Demonstrated" per the R3 claim ledger. The 27 pp headline is real arithmetic but statistically ill-posed — comparing against a collapsed baseline (NormalFloat at 3-bit, PPL = N.A.) produces a measurement with no sampling distribution; the valid controlled improvement is ~16% (8.4 pp) against functioning baseline LoftQ. Additionally, the metric-composition of the Avg column may be inconsistent: NormalFloat Avg values are suspiciously uniform across bit widths (33.79, 33.64, 33.45 for 3/2.5/2.25 bits) suggesting Avg may be commonsense-only for NormalFloat but full-suite for LoRDS — a second compounding layer of invalidity that the paper does not disclose. These concerns collectively place the paper below the ICML acceptance threshold in its current form. The paper is a **revise-and-resubmit** candidate: correct Algorithm 1, add multi-seed reporting, reframe the 27 pp headline against a functioning baseline, and disclose the metric-composition methodology.

**The single change that would most strengthen the paper:** Add multi-seed reporting (mean ± std across ≥3 seeds) for all PEFT fine-tuning experiments. This is the one gap that, if unaddressed, most undermines confidence in the paper's most compelling result (the 9.6 pp PEFT improvement over QLoRA).

### Lens 2 — Archaeologist (Prior Work)

LRQ ("LRQ: Optimizing Post-Training Quantization for Large Language Models by Learning Low-Rank Weight-Scaling Matrices," Lee et al., NAACL 2025, arXiv:2407.11534) is the closest prior anchor. LRQ also uses low-rank weight-scaling matrices to replace conventional per-block scaling in PTQ. LoRDS acknowledges LRQ and distinguishes itself via a linear S = BA formulation (vs. LRQ's exponential mapping) that enables direct SVD initialization from standard block-wise statistics without computational reconstruction from scratch. However, LRQ is strictly PTQ-only; LoRDS's extension to a unified PTQ/QAT/PEFT framework is novel over LRQ.

On the concurrent-neighbor dimension: QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2023) and LoftQ (Li et al., 2023) combine quantization with additive LoRA adapters. QLoRA's additive update ΔW = B_lora A_lora cannot be merged into quantized weights, incurring inference overhead. LoRDS's multiplicative update is architecturally distinct. The paper correctly positions itself against these neighbors.

The "first to use low-rank decomposition of scaling factors" claim in the paper is falsified by LRQ. The "first to propose unified PTQ+QAT+PEFT" claim is plausible — no prior reference subsumes this.

### Lens 3 — Academic Researcher (Forward Leverage)

One concrete non-trivial follow-up that materially depends on this paper: apply the LoRDS multiplicative PEFT framework to other quantized PEFT baselines beyond QLoRA — specifically LoftQ, PEQA, and QA-LoRA. The key insight — that multiplicative adaptation of scaling factors enables high-rank effective updates with zero inference overhead — is not QLoRA-specific. A natural follow-up would replace the additive adapter in LoftQ with LoRDS-style multiplicative scaling and evaluate whether the same speedup and accuracy gains hold across different base quantization methods. This is non-trivial because it requires re-deriving the optimization procedure for each base quantization format, but the framework's modularity suggests it is feasible.

The unified framework also opens a research direction on optimal scheduling: given a target model and accuracy constraint, what is the optimal sequence of PTQ → QAT → PEFT operations under the LoRDS framework? This is a genuine open problem that the paper's framework makes tractable.

### Lens 4 — Industry Practitioner (Deployment)

The deployment pitch is compelling in principle: a unified framework covering the entire LLM efficiency lifecycle, with PEFT that adds no inference overhead compared to QLoRA-style methods, and Triton kernels achieving real speedups. For practitioners currently maintaining three separate toolchains, LoRDS offers a path to consolidation.

The speedup claim against QLoRA (1.5× on RTX 4090) is real and verified across multiple platforms (Table 6). The "zero overhead" claim — meaning no additional adapter weights to load and merge at runtime compared to the quantized base model — is correctly scoped and supported by the throughput data. LoRDS prefill (7,847 tok/s) is 40% faster than QLoRA prefill (5,601 tok/s) on RTX 4090.

Three concrete blockers prevent immediate adoption: (1) **No code artifact** — the paper claims "highly optimized Triton kernels" but provides no GitHub URL. The kernel claims cannot be independently verified without code. (2) **Missing multi-seed reporting** — the 9.6 pp PEFT improvement margin needs variance estimates before a practitioner can assess reliability. (3) **H800 microbenchmark vs. end-to-end inconsistency** — the Section 4.4 text claims "matches NF4" on H800, but Table 6 shows a 25% end-to-end throughput gap. Practitioners need clarification on which number to trust.

### Lens 5 — Hacker (Reimplementability)

Could a competent ML grad student reimplement the core contribution from paper text + appendix in a weekend, without guessing material design choices? Partially.

**Reimplementable:** The SVD decomposition S = BA and the rank budget formula r = floor(nm/(B(n+m))) are fully derivable from the text and appendix. The PTQ objective (minimizing reconstruction error ||W − (BA) ⊙ Q||²_F) and the gradient formulas (STE approximations) are clearly stated. The PEFT update mechanism (ΔW = Q ⊙ (B'A' − BA)) is clearly described. A motivated reimplementer could reproduce the core framework.

**Reimplementable with notation clarification:** Algorithm 1's quantization step, as written, uses confusing notation (`v` for dequantized values in `L`). The correct interpretation is that `Q_ij ∈ L` is the dequantized value at level `q`, and the objective `(S_ij·v − W_ij)²` is equivalent to the standard `(q − W_ij/S_ij)²` formulation. A careful reader can verify this equivalence, but the notation should be clarified in revision.

**NOT reimplementable:** The Triton kernels for the low-rank scaling dequantization path. The paper describes the optimization goal but provides no kernel code or algorithmic details beyond "Triton kernels." A practitioner cannot reproduce the speedup claims without either the code artifact or significantly more implementation detail.

**Reproducibility baseline:** The paper provides no code URL, no random seeds, no hyperparameter ranges beyond those used in the experiments. Even for the parts that are reimplementable, a third party could not verify whether their implementation matches the paper's without significant trial and error.

### Integrated Verdict

LoRDS proposes a conceptually valuable unification of LLM quantization stages via low-rank decomposition of block-wise scaling matrices, with a genuinely novel multiplicative PEFT mechanism that eliminates adapter-specific inference overhead. The core insight — that S = BA via SVD enables a continuous interpolation between per-block and per-element scaling — is sound, and the unified PTQ/QAT/PEFT framework addresses a real fragmentation problem in LLM deployment.

Following the adversarial pass and author rebuttal, the committee revised several initial concerns: (1) the authors and adversarial argue that the two Algorithm 1 formulations are equivalent; however, the R3 panel unanimously identified a counterexample (W=3.7, S=1.2, L={0,1,2,3}) where they produce different Q_ij values when S_ij ≠ 1. The empirical PTQ results are real and reproducible, but may be suboptimal on the correctly specified objective — the panel unanimously recommended fixing the notation and re-running; (2) the main PTQ results in Tables 1–3 are zero-shot for all methods, so the 500-step asymmetric refinement critique applies only to the Table 4 ablation; (3) the "zero overhead" claim is correctly scoped to PEFT-specific overhead vs. QLoRA, not raw NF4 throughput; (4) the 27 pp figure is disclosed in Table 4 with N.A. annotations, making this a presentation gap rather than a disclosure failure.

The remaining genuine gaps are: no multi-seed reporting for PEFT/QAT experiments (below ICML sub-field standards), missing Limitations section, abstract framing improvements needed for the 3-bit collapse context, microbenchmark-vs-end-to-end clarification for the H800 speedup claim, and no code artifact for reproducibility. These are correctable in a revision. The paper's PEFT results (Table 5) — 87.68% with 84M params vs. QLoRA's 78.08% with 193M params — are the paper's strongest and most uncontested contribution, independent of the PTQ pipeline concerns.

**The paper is a revise-and-resubmit (ICML 3/6, Weak Reject).** The conceptual contributions are genuine and valuable. The empirical concerns — Algorithm 1 mis-specification, statistically ill-posed 27 pp framing, metric-composition uncertainty, and absence of multi-seed reporting — are collectively fatal to acceptance in their current form. All are correctable with targeted revisions: fix Algorithm 1, reframe against a functioning baseline, disclose metric-composition methodology, and add multi-seed reporting.

### Specific, Actionable Feedback

1. **Add multi-seed reporting for PEFT and QAT experiments (Tables 4–5):** Report mean ± std across ≥3 random seeds for all fine-tuning experiments. This is the single most important improvement — it is the one gap that, if unaddressed, most undermines confidence in the paper's most compelling result.

2. **Add a clarifying sentence to the abstract about 3-bit collapse context (Section 1 / Abstract):** Add one sentence: "At 3-bit, NormalFloat diverges (perplexity undefined); LoRDS maintains coherent behavior (60.76% avg accuracy vs. 33.79% for the collapsed NormalFloat)." This addresses the presentation gap without changing any numbers.

3. **Clarify Section 4.4's H800 speedup claim:** The "matches NF4" claim refers to the single-layer q_proj microbenchmark (0.456ms vs. 0.409ms). Add a sentence acknowledging the end-to-end throughput gap: "While LoRDS matches NF4 for the q_proj layer in isolation, end-to-end throughput shows a 25% gap (1,490 vs. 1,975 tokens/s on H800) due to additional scaling computation across all layers."

4. **Add a dedicated Limitations section:** Explicitly discuss: (a) Qwen3-4B PTB perplexity regression at block size 256 (48.68 vs. NF4's 43.02); (b) QAT trained and evaluated on a single corpus (SmolLM) — generalization to other fine-tuning corpora unshown; (c) PEFT evaluated on a single training dataset (Commonsense-170k) — broader PEFT claims unsubstantiated; (d) conditions under which the low-rank assumption may fail.

5. **Clarify Algorithm 1 notation (Section 3.2, algo_iterative.tex):** Rename `v` to `q` in the quantization step; clarify that `Q_ij ∈ L` is the dequantized value at quantized level `q`, making the two-step (quantize, then dequantize) structure explicit. This is a notation fix, not an algorithmic change.

6. **Disclose PEFT layer coverage asymmetry (Section 4.3 / Table 5):** LoRDS applies adapters to all linear layers (Q, K, V, O, gate, up, down) while QLoRA typically applies only to Q/V projections. Either match layer coverage in the comparison or explicitly disclose the asymmetry and discuss its impact on the 9.6 pp improvement attribution.

7. **Release code and model weights:** The paper claims "highly optimized Triton kernels" but provides no GitHub URL. Release of the implementation is essential for reproducibility and practitioner adoption. This is the largest blocker for the deployment narrative.

### Questions for Authors

1. **Algorithm 1 notation:** You state the quantization step finds `v ∈ L` minimizing `(S_ij·v − W_ij)²`. If `v` is the dequantized value (an element of the lookup table `L`), then the dequantized result is `v` directly, not `v·S_ij`. But Algorithm 1 stores `v` as `Q_ij` and the dequantized weight is `Q_ij·S_ij`. Can you clarify whether `v` is the quantized index or the dequantized value? The mathematical equivalence between the two formulations depends on this interpretation.

2. **PEFT layer coverage:** Does LoRDS apply adapters to all linear layers (Q, K, V, O, gate, up, down) while QLoRA applies only to Q/V projections? If so, how much of the 9.6 pp improvement is attributable to broader layer coverage vs. the multiplicative vs. additive design difference?

3. **Multi-seed reporting:** How many random seeds were used for PEFT fine-tuning (Table 5) and QAT experiments (Table 4)? What is the standard deviation across seeds for the key accuracy claims?

4. **H800 microbenchmark vs. end-to-end:** Table 6 shows LoRDS at 1,490 tokens/s vs. NF4 at 1,975 tokens/s end-to-end on H800. Your text claims LoRDS "matches the industrial-level NF4 implementation." Does this refer only to the single-layer microbenchmark? What is the end-to-end NF4 baseline you are comparing against?

5. **Matched PTQ refinement:** Table 4 shows LoRDS with 500-step iterative refinement. If NF4 and AWQ also received 500 steps of iterative refinement (with their own appropriate learning rates), would the PTQ margins in Tables 1–3 change substantially?

The adversarial pass argued for ICML 4/6 (Weak Accept) over the draft's 3/6 (Weak Reject), with these key counter-arguments: (1) Algorithm 1 is a notation issue, not a result-changing bug — both formulations produce identical dequantized outputs; (2) the 27 pp figure is disclosed in Table 4 with N.A. annotations, making this a presentation gap not a disclosure failure; (3) "zero overhead" is correctly scoped to PEFT-specific overhead vs. QLoRA; (4) the 500-step asymmetry applies only to the Table 4 ablation, not the main PTQ results in Tables 1–3; (5) the PEFT results (Table 5) are uncontested by any bug and show a 2.3× parameter-budget advantage with higher accuracy than QLoRA.

**Residual risk after adversarial:** The Algorithm 1 equivalence argument was made by the adversarial and authors, but the R3 panel unanimously rejected it with a concrete counterexample (W=3.7, S=1.2, L={0,1,2,3}) where the two objectives produce different Q_ij values. The empirical PTQ results are real and reproducible, but may be suboptimal on the correctly specified objective. The multi-seed reporting gap is real and the proposed fix (≥3 seeds) is adequate. The microbenchmark-vs-end-to-end clarification is a presentation improvement that does not change any empirical findings. The net assessment: the adversarial pass and author rebuttal do not resolve the Algorithm 1 mis-specification; the paper remains a 3/6 (Weak Reject) with mandatory revisions needed before acceptance.

The author rebuttal confirmed: (a) Algorithm 1 notation will be clarified (rename `v` to `q`, clarify dequantized value semantics); (b) one clarifying sentence will be added to the abstract about NormalFloat's 3-bit collapse; (c) a footnote will clarify that slight NF4 regression reflects additional scaling computation, not PEFT adapter overhead; (d) matched-refinement comparison will be added for NF4/AWQ baselines; (e) multi-seed reporting will be added for key PEFT results; (f) "matches NF4" will be limited to single-layer microbenchmark context; (g) a Limitations section will be added.

**Residual risk after author rebuttal:** The author rebuttal committed to notation clarification, multi-seed reporting, and a Limitations section — all substantive improvements. However, it did not provide a concrete counterexample resolution for Algorithm 1. The notation fix (renaming `v` to `q`, clarifying dequantized value semantics) is the right direction, but whether it resolves the panel's counterexample depends on the corrected algorithm's actual output. The paper cannot be accepted until the corrected Algorithm 1 is validated.
