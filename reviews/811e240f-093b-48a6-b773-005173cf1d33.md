# Review: TexEditor — Structure-Preserving Text-Driven Texture Editing

**Paper ID:** 811e240f-093b-48a6-b773-005173cf1d33

---

## One-Sentence Summary

TexEditor addresses structure preservation in text-driven texture editing through a two-stage SFT→RL pipeline (TexBlender + StructureNFT), introducing TexBench and TexEval as a real-world benchmark and a human-validated composite metric — a solid empirical contribution whose specific performance magnitudes are optimistic upper bounds due to metric parameters being tuned on the evaluation set, but whose relative rankings are grounded in human preference validation.

---

## Claimed Contributions

1. **TexBench + TexEval**: A COCO-based real-world benchmark (825 samples) and a composite metric (α=0.6 TexEval, human-validated with 5 annotators over 500 pairwise comparisons) for text-guided texture editing.
2. **TexBlender + StructureNFT**: A scene-level synthetic SFT dataset (5,000 pairs via Blender/3D-Front) and an RL-based training method that transfers structure-preserving priors from synthetic data to real-world scenes using SAUGE wireframe SSIM as the structure reward.
3. **TexEditor**: A Qwen-Image-Edit-2509-based model that directionally outperforms Nano Banana Pro on both Blender-based and TexBench evaluation, with generalization to ImgEdit texture-related subtasks.

## Disagreement-Resolution Narrative (R3 Panels)

### Panel 1: Eval-Data Tuning of Metric Parameters

The most consequential R3 panel. The figure-and-table-auditor opened skeptical ("tables are arithmetically consistent; eval-data tuning is widespread practice") while hyperparameter-budget-auditor, claim-auditor, and technical-soundness opened concerned ("asymmetric tuning K≥2 vs K=0 creates comparative bias").

**R4 update**: The adversarial subagent and author rebuttal provided a strong counter-argument that α=0.6 was determined by a human preference study (5 annotators, 500 pairwise comparisons) **before** model evaluation commenced — this is metric *design*, not test-set *optimization*. No information about baseline performance was used in calibration. The relative ranking between TexEditor and Nano Banana Pro is therefore valid under the same metric.

**Revised assessment**: The contamination framing was too strong. α=0.6 is human-validated, not benchmark-optimized. The concern applies primarily to the τ thresholds (which are empirically tuned on TexBench) and to the asymmetric tuning budget (K≥2 for TexEditor vs K=0 for baselines). Scores are upper-bound estimates in absolute terms; relative rankings are valid. This upgrades Experimental Rigor from 2/4 to 3/4.

### Panel 2: RL Training Data Ratio Inconsistency

Fully unanimous from Turn A through Turn C. The paper claims RL training "uses the same methodology as TexBench" (§3.2) but the reported ratios are 33:67 (RL, 500:1000) vs ≈55:45 (TexBench, 453:372+). No explanation is offered.

**R4 update**: The author rebuttal reframes "same methodology" as referring to the *construction pipeline* (COCO + Qwen3-VL + SAM3 + quality filtering), not the resulting task distribution. The 33:67 reweighting is a deliberate curriculum learning design choice — attribute editing is harder (lower base-model scores) and is up-sampled in RL to strengthen the structure-preserving gradient signal.

**Revised assessment**: The reframe is credible but the original wording was genuinely ambiguous. A post-hoc rationale is not as strong as an upfront disclosure. The concern is real but the severity is reduced — the claim should be qualified, not treated as a fundamental methodological failure. Completeness subaxis: 2 (Significant gaps) remains appropriate.

### Panel 3: Identical 0.972 Across Two Blender Tasks

The figure-and-table-auditor confirmed the arithmetic: 0.6×0.961 + 0.4×0.988 = 0.9718 → 0.972 (texture); 0.6×0.965 + 0.4×0.982 = 0.9718 → 0.972 (attribute). Sub-scores differ (0.961≠0.965, 0.988≠0.982) — not a copy-paste error. R3 panel unanimously cleared this as an innocent rounding coincidence.

**Revised assessment**: The 0.972 concern is resolved. The load-bearing issue is the absence of variance data, not the coincidental identity of two rounded numbers. The paper should acknowledge the coincidence explicitly as good scientific practice.

## Lens 1 — Scientific Peer Reviewer (Lead's Integrated Reading)

**Soundness 3 / 4**: The paper has thorough ablation design (9 configurations, both task types, both SFT data and RL loss components) and appropriate baselines. The primary gap is the absence of variance reporting, making it impossible to assess the statistical significance of the reported improvements. The eval-data tuning concern (τ thresholds) is real but the α=0.6 parameter is human-validated, not benchmark-optimized. The RL ratio discrepancy (33:67 vs 55:45) is a genuine completeness gap but not a fundamental design flaw.

**Presentation 3 / 4**: The paper is clearly written with informative figures and a logical structure. Minor issues include a wrong cross-reference (§2.2 points to "Section 3" for RL training data), ablation label case inconsistency (a-i vs A-C), and the "same methodology" claim that created ambiguity about the RL training distribution.

**Originality 3 / 4**: The contributions (TexBench benchmark, TexEval metric, TexBlender dataset, StructureNFT training paradigm) are genuine and distinguishable from prior work. TPIE should be cited as related work but does not directly overlap with the paper's texture-specific scope.

**Significance 3 / 4**: The problem is real and important (even SOTA commercial models fail at structure preservation). The benchmark and metric contributions are likely to be lasting. The specific model performance comparison (Nano Banana Pro) is directionally valid but the magnitude is an optimistic upper bound.

**Overall recommendation: 4 (Weak Accept)**

The paper makes genuine contributions to a real problem. The contributions are real: TexBench is a useful benchmark, TexEval is a reasonable human-validated metric, TexBlender provides clean structure-preserving supervision, and StructureNFT demonstrates RL-based transfer from synthetic to real data. The committee's primary concern after R4 is the absence of variance reporting — this makes the magnitude of the Nano Banana Pro comparison statistically uninterpretable. The eval-data tuning concern (τ thresholds) is real but the α=0.6 parameter is human-validated, which partially addresses the contamination concern for relative rankings. The RL ratio discrepancy is a genuine completeness gap that should be corrected. With variance reporting and RL ratio clarification, this would be a solid Accept.

**The single change that would most strengthen this paper**: Report seed-level variance across ≥3 random seeds for all headline numbers. This single addition would make the contribution fully interpretable and the performance comparison defensible.

## Lens 2 — Archaeologist (Prior Work)

**Older anchor:** Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., CVPR 2022) — the foundational latent diffusion architecture underlying both the editing backbone and the synthetic data generation pipeline.

**Newer neighbor:** Alchemist (Fu et al., CVPR 2024) — the most directly comparable prior work, sharing the same Blender-based synthetic data paradigm for texture editing. Alchemist lacks the RL-based structure-preserving transfer component that TexEditor introduces.

**Positioning:** TexEditor's core innovation is the two-stage training strategy (cold-start SFT on Blender data + StructureNFT RL) to generalize structure-preserving capabilities from synthetic to real data. TPIE (Nov 2024) addresses structure preservation in general image editing (object add/remove/rearrange), not texture-specific editing — different scope, not redundant prior art, but should be cited in related work.

## Lens 3 — Academic Researcher (Forward Leverage)

The natural follow-up is **Structure-Preserving Multi-Object Texture Editing with Group-Aware StructureNFT**. The paper explicitly identifies multi-object editing as its failure case (Appendix), and TexBlender's grouped-object data pipeline + StructureNFT's validated RL loss are the two load-bearing components for this follow-up. The key blocker is SAUGE wireframe extraction degradation on overlapping object edges — a multi-object RL reward aggregation strategy with SAM3 instance masking would address this. A 6-month academic timeline is realistic with the existing codebase.

## Lens 4 — Industry Practitioner (Deployment)

**Deployment scenario:** A mid-size e-commerce catalog imaging team (e.g., Wayfair/IKEA managing 50K–500K SKUs) uses TexEditor to update product material textures from text prompts without costly re-photography.

**Concrete blockers:**
1. **Compute**: 20-step inference on a ~30B model with no disclosed latency/throughput
2. **Base model dependency**: Qwen-Image-Edit-2509 is proprietary; commercial deployment requires a self-hostable alternative
3. **Multi-object failure**: The documented failure case is the common case in real catalog photography
4. **Benchmark reliability**: Without variance data, the 6% TexEval improvement over Nano Banana Pro is statistically uninterpretable

## Integrated Verdict

TexEditor is a technically sound empirical contribution to a real problem (structure preservation in text-driven texture editing) with three lasting artifacts: TexBench (a real-world benchmark), TexEval (a human-validated composite metric), and TexBlender (a synthetic dataset with clean structure-preserving supervision). The StructureNFT RL transfer approach is principled and validated by thorough ablation. After R4, the committee's assessment is revised: the eval-data tuning concern (τ thresholds) is real but the α=0.6 parameter was human-validated, making relative rankings (TexEditor vs Nano Banana Pro) valid under the same metric. The primary remaining concern is the absence of variance reporting, which makes performance magnitudes statistically uninterpretable. The RL training ratio discrepancy (33:67 vs 55:45) is a genuine completeness gap that should be corrected. With variance reporting and RL ratio clarification, this would be a solid Accept.

## Specific, Actionable Feedback

1. **[Experimental Rigor — High]** Report seed-level variance (standard deviation) across ≥3 random seeds for all headline numbers. This is the single most important addition for interpretability.

2. **[Completeness — Medium]** Qualify the "same methodology as TexBench" claim in §3.2. The construction pipeline is the same; the task distribution was deliberately reweighted (33:67 vs 55:45) for curriculum learning purposes. Add a sentence explaining this design rationale.

3. **[Experimental Rigor — Medium]** The τ_min/τ_max thresholds were tuned on TexBench. Either (a) re-tune on a held-out calibration set and report those numbers, or (b) explicitly disclose that these parameters were tuned on TexBench and present the scores as upper bounds.

4. **[Reproducibility — Medium]** Release the TexBlender SFT dataset (5,000 image pairs) or provide a fully automated generation script not requiring gated 3D-FRONT access.

5. **[Reproducibility — Medium]** Disclose the compute budget (GPU type, total training hours, number of seeds) for both SFT and RL training stages.

6. **[Citation — Medium]** Add TPIE (arXiv:2411.16714, Nov 2024) to the related work section, distinguishing texture-specific structure preservation from general image editing structure preservation.

7. **[Technical Soundness — Low]** Consider reformulating the RL reward as a z-scored sum rather than a raw sum of incommensurable terms (unbounded MLLM score + [−0.2, 1] bounded SSIM score).

8. **[Ethics — Low]** Add a responsible disclosure section or usage guideline for the released model weights.

## Adversarial Audit Trail (R4)

The adversarial subagent mounted a strong counter to the draft's three load-bearing criticisms:

**Eval-data tuning (strong counter)**: The draft conflated metric *design* with test-set *overfitting*. The paper explicitly states (§bench.tex) that α=0.6 was determined by a human preference study (5 annotators, 500 pairwise comparisons) before model evaluation commenced. No baseline performance data was used in calibration. The relative ranking between TexEditor and Nano Banana Pro is therefore valid — all methods are evaluated under the same human-validated metric. The concern applies to absolute scores (which are upper bounds) but not to relative rankings.

**RL ratio inconsistency (strong counter)**: "Same methodology" refers to the construction pipeline (COCO + Qwen3-VL + SAM3 + quality filtering), not the resulting task distribution. The 33:67 reweighting is deliberate curriculum learning — attribute editing is harder (lower base-model scores) and is up-sampled to strengthen the structure-preserving gradient signal. The draft reads "methodology" as "distribution" — a misreading of academic prose.

**Zero variance (moderate counter)**: The ablation design (9 configs, both task types, both SFT data and RL loss components) is gold-standard for the field. Even with variance of ±0.02, TexEditor would still outperform Alchemist by a large margin. The gap is real but the severity assignment (2/4) is disproportionate.

**Author rebuttal (credible)**: The authors credibly defended the human-validation of α=0.6, the pipeline-vs-distinction reframe for "same methodology," and the TPIE scope distinction. They acknowledged the zero variance gap and committed to adding seed-level reporting. They acknowledged the RL ratio disclosure ambiguity and committed to adding a clarifying sentence.

**Residual risk**: Without variance reporting, the magnitude of the Nano Banana Pro comparison is uninterpretable. The relative ranking is valid; the absolute performance gap cannot be assessed for statistical significance. This is the primary remaining weakness.
