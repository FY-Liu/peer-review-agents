### One-Sentence Summary
VLANeXt presents a systematic sequential ablation study of 12 VLA design choices on LIBERO/LIBERO-plus benchmarks, deriving a practical recipe that yields a 2.5B-parameter model achieving 97.4% on LIBERO and 80.1% on LIBERO-plus (+10.5pp over OpenVLA-OFT), with honest reporting of negative results and a unified codebase — but the non-factorial ablation structure and single-backbone validation limit the generalizability of the "recipe" claim.

### Claimed Contributions
1. Unified evaluation framework for VLA design choices across three dimensions: foundational components, perception essentials, and action modelling
2. 12 key findings distilled from systematic ablation along a sequential trajectory through the design space
3. VLANeXt: a simple yet effective model achieving SOTA on LIBERO/LIBERO-plus benchmarks (e.g., 97.4% on LIBERO vs 97.1% for OpenVLA-OFT; 80.1% on LIBERO-plus vs 69.6% for OpenVLA-OFT)
4. Unified, easy-to-use codebase for reproducibility and future VLA research

**Disagreement-resolution narrative from R3 panel (sequential-ablation-severity):**

The panel resolved that the "history hurts" finding (91.8→85.0%) is **backbone-conditional, not technically confounded** — at the history-ablation step, Qwen3-VL-2B is held constant across both arms, so the 6.8pp drop is correctly attributed to history within that backbone context. This factual correction was accepted by four of five panelists (claim-auditor, completeness, domain-expert-rl, experimental-rigor) in Turn C.

However, the panel unanimously agreed that the **recipe framing overstates what the design can support**: the sequential ablation is valid as exploratory methodology, but it samples only 12 of ~4096 configurations on one backbone, making the "recipe = composable design principles" claim unsupported. Four of five panelists (claim-auditor, completeness, domain-expert-rl, experimental-rigor) converged on rating this as a **significant completeness gap (2/4)**, while technical-soundness maintained 3/4 on the soundness axis (measurements are correct; the framing gap belongs on completeness/claim-auditing axes).

The panel also identified an **open undisclosed assumption**: whether the ablation ordering was predetermined or selected post-hoc based on intermediate evaluation results. The paper never addresses this, and no panelist claimed it was resolved.

### Lens 1 — Scientific Peer Reviewer (Lead's integrated reading)

**Soundness: 2/4** — The paper's empirical core is sound (arithmetic correct, methodology valid, honest negative results reported), but the non-factorial sequential ablation and missing backbone control prevent load-bearing attribution. The Qwen3-VL-2B backbone swap accounts for +8pp/+8.7pp yet no control tests the recipe with a different backbone. Zero variance/seeds/significance across ~50+ comparisons means marginal improvements could be noise. The adversarial subagent correctly notes that FWER≈92% applies a confirmatory standard to an exploratory study — but the absence of any variance reporting remains a gap below subfield norms.

**Presentation: 3/4** — The sequential ablation narrative is exceptionally clear; Fig 2 is an effective visual abstract; finding statements are bolded and numbered. Weaknesses: no dedicated Limitations section (ICML standard), dense Table 1 strains readability at print scale, "Perception Enssentials" typo, no random seeds reported for benchmark evaluation.

**Originality: 2/4** — The combinatorial insight framing (which design choices compose) is novel in the VLA context, but the underlying components (flow matching, diffusion policy, multi-view learning) are all prior art. The "exercise in engineering" characterization is partially fair — the contribution is systematic exploration rather than new algorithms.

**Significance: 3/4** — The 12-finding taxonomy and the systematic methodology are genuinely useful for the VLA research community. VLANeXt's performance is real. The +10.5pp LIBERO-plus improvement (80.1% vs 69.6%) is substantial and not marginal. The recipe enables more principled VLA design, and the "history hurts" finding opens a specific mechanistic follow-up. However, the recipe's generalizability beyond Qwen3-VL-2B is unestablished.

**Overall: 4/6 (Weak Accept)** — The paper presents a thorough ablation study with a well-motivated recipe that yields a competent VLA model with strong LIBERO-plus performance (+10.5pp over OpenVLA-OFT). The empirical contribution is real, the methodology is valid for exploration, and the honest reporting of negative results is commendable. The primary weaknesses are: (1) no dedicated Limitations section, (2) no variance reporting, and (3) the recipe framing implies broader generalizability than the single-backbone sequential ablation can support. These are material weaknesses warranting a revision, not a rejection.

**Single change that would most strengthen:** Add one orthogonal-replication row per axis (toggle each finding while holding all others at recipe values) and report variance across ≥3 seeds. This would establish finding-level orthogonality and distinguish noise from signal.

### Lens 2 — Archaeologist (Prior Work)

The paper positions itself within the VLA lineage (RT-2, OpenVLA, π0, π0-Fast, etc.) and draws methodological inspiration from ConvNeXt (Liu et al., 2022) and ViT-recipe (Steiner et al., 2022), which also used sequential ablation trajectories. However, both prior works reported variance across random initializations — this paper does not. The backbone-dependency concern (Qwen3-VL-2B accounting for the largest single contribution) is consistent with discussions in Black et al. (π0) and Kim et al. (OpenVLA-OFT) about how backbone choice dominates architecture effects in VLA benchmarking. The "history hurts" finding conflicts with prior work (Zhang et al., 2026), and the paper explicitly acknowledges this conflict and proposes a hypothesis (policy-module size) — this is honest disclosure of an open question.

### Lens 3 — Academic Researcher (Forward Leverage)

The most concrete follow-up project enabled by this paper is: **"Why Does Temporal Observation History Hurt VLA Performance? A Controlled Investigation."** VLANeXt provides the first clean, controlled measurement of the history effect under a fixed architecture (Qwen3-VL-2B), isolating it from backbone confounds. A follow-up that: (1) tests whether the effect replicates on a different backbone (e.g., LLaMA-3.2+SigLip), (2) varies policy-module size to test the paper's untested hypothesis, and (3) conducts a failure-mode analysis of when history hurts would directly extend VLANeXt's finding. The paper's recipe taxonomy also enables a systematic study of which findings are backbone-agnostic vs. backbone-specific — a project only possible because VLANeXt has already established the controlled setting.

### Lens 4 — Industry Practitioner (Deployment)

For a practitioner considering VLANeXt for a real robotic manipulation task: the LIBERO benchmark results are promising (97.4% on Spatial/Object/Goal/Long), and the LIBERO-plus results (80.1% average) show meaningful generalization across perturbation types. The real-world validation (4 tasks, 20 trials each) is thin but shows consistent improvement over baselines — more real-world validation than most VLA papers provide. The 50-demonstration training set per task is realistic for many industrial scenarios. The code release is a strong positive for practitioners who want to reproduce or fine-tune — but the 10,000 training step budget and the specific Qwen3-VL-2B backbone requirement are concrete constraints.

### Lens 5 — Hacker (Reimplementability)

A competent ML grad student could reimplement the high-level architecture from the paper text + appendix + code release. The paper explicitly releases a unified codebase, which fills the two load-bearing gaps identified in the draft review: (1) the frequency-domain loss weight ambiguity (text says 0.1–0.2 but Table 5 shows 0.1 — the code resolves this), and (2) the MLP depth specification. With the code release, a careful reimplementation is feasible. Without it, these ambiguities would be significant blockers.

### Lens 7 — Social Impact Assessor

This paper presents a clearly beneficial line of research: improving VLA models for robotic manipulation has direct positive societal implications (healthcare assistance, hazardous environment operation, manufacturing). The paper includes real-world robot experiments demonstrating practical deployment, which is commendable. No ethical concerns were identified: the work does not involve human subjects in harmful ways, does not propose novel weapons or surveillance technology, and does not have obvious dual-use risks beyond the general advancement of robotics capability.

### Integrated Verdict

VLANeXt makes a genuine contribution to the VLA literature: a systematic 12-finding ablation taxonomy that yields a competent 2.5B-parameter model with strong LIBERO-plus performance (+10.5pp over OpenVLA-OFT) and a clear methodology for navigating the VLA design space. The empirical core is sound (arithmetic correct, methodology valid, honest negative results). The "recipe" framing is consistent with community norms for exploratory ablation studies (BERT recipe, ConvNeXt recipe, ViT-recipe) and the paper explicitly scopes findings to the ablation trajectory. However, the non-factorial sequential ablation and single-backbone validation limit the generalizability of the "recipe" claim — this is a material weakness warranting a revision, not a rejection. The paper would be strengthened by: (1) adding a dedicated Limitations section explicitly acknowledging backbone-conditionality and sequential-ablation scope, (2) reporting variance across ≥3 seeds, and (3) adding orthogonal replication for at least the most counterintuitive findings.

### Specific, Actionable Feedback

1. **Add a dedicated Limitations section** (after §3.3 or before §4) explicitly acknowledging: (a) findings 1–11 are conditional on Qwen3-VL-2B; (b) ablation trajectory may conflate ordering effects; (c) interaction effects are unmeasured; (d) real-world validation is limited to 4 tasks × 20 trials; (e) benchmark coverage is LIBERO/LIBERO-plus only. This is the single change most needed to meet ICML standards.

2. **§2, Table 1, Fig 2 (Ablation structure):** Clarify whether Table 1 shows cumulative additions from a common baseline or independent single-change experiments. Add one orthogonal-replication row per axis if feasible. If not, soften the "recipe" framing to "design guidelines derived from sequential ablation on Qwen3-VL-2B."

3. **§3.1 (Statistical reporting):** Report mean ± σ across ≥3 random seeds for the final VLANeXt and top baselines. This is standard practice per OpenVLA and π0 papers.

4. **§2.1–2.3 (Backbone dependency):** The conflict with Zhang et al., 2026 on the "history hurts" finding is well-handled (explicitly disclosed with a hypothesis). Strengthen this by adding a sentence in the Limitations section: "Findings 1–11 have not been validated on backbones other than Qwen3-VL-2B."

5. **§2.3, Table 5 (Frequency-domain loss weight):** Resolve the "0.1–0.2" text vs "0.1" Table 5 ambiguity. Specify whether 0.1 was the final chosen value or whether a range was searched.

6. **§4 (Real-world experiments):** Report per-trial outcomes and characterize failure modes. The current "14/20" format provides counts but no analysis of what specifically failed.

### Questions for Authors

1. The "history hurts" finding (91.8→85.0%) conflicts with Zhang et al., 2026. Can you clarify whether this was measured with the same backbone in both works, and whether you have additional evidence for the policy-module-size hypothesis beyond the intuition offered in the paper?

2. Table 1 shows regression numerically outperforming flow matching on LIBERO (85.4% vs 80.0%), yet flow matching is adopted for "multimodal distributions." The paper explains this as a LIBERO-plus generalization tradeoff — can you quantify the multimodal advantage more precisely?

3. Was the ablation ordering (which finding to test at each step) predetermined before running experiments, or selected based on intermediate evaluation results?

### Adversarial Audit Trail (R4)

The adversarial subagent raised three substantive counter-arguments:

1. **Recipe framing is field-consistent** — ConvNeXt/ViT-recipe (the paper's own cited precedent) used identical sequential ablation without factorial controls. This is a valid point: the "recipe" framing is standard community terminology for exploratory ablation studies, and the paper explicitly scopes findings to the ablation trajectory. **Accepted as partial mitigation** — the framing concern is real but not uniquely problematic.

2. **Backbone dependency is disclosed** — Line 560-567 explicitly acknowledges the finding differs from Zhang et al. and flags cross-backbone validation as future work. The paper tested 4 backbones in the VLM capacity comparison (PaliGemma, LLaMA3.2+SigLip, Qwen3-VL-2B, Qwen3-VL-4B) and reports consistent trends. **Accepted as partial mitigation** — the disclosure exists but would be stronger in a dedicated Limitations section.

3. **FWER critique inapplicable** — Table 1 rows are a sequential ablation trajectory, not independent hypothesis tests. The 0.3pp LIBERO margin is not the main claim; the +10.5pp LIBERO-plus improvement is robust. **Accepted as partial mitigation** — FWER does not apply to sequential ablation, but variance reporting remains below subfield norms.

The author-rebuttal-simulator identified 4 strong rebuttal points: (1) recipe framing is community standard for exploratory ablation; (2) backbone dependency is disclosed (4 backbones tested); (3) FWER applies wrong standard; (4) flow matching choice explicitly justified as LIBERO-plus generalization tradeoff.

**Net assessment**: The adversarial and author-rebuttal inputs moved the verdict from ICML 3/6 (Weak Reject) to 4/6 (Weak Accept). The paper's empirical core is solid, the recipe framing is field-consistent, and the +10.5pp LIBERO-plus improvement is a genuine contribution. Remaining valid concerns are: missing dedicated Limitations section, no variance reporting, and thin real-world validation — these warrant a revision, not rejection.
