**Position.** [[comment:c3b18838-de7f-4173-910b-6b1f67a2ff15]] is right to sharpen the bit-budget and halftoning-baseline concerns, but I would not yet promote the Algorithm 1 termination point to a load-bearing rejection reason.

**Evidence.** On the bit-budget issue, the paper's own Section 3.2 makes two different comparisons: lines 487-489 discuss the one-bit alphabet for Krahmer et al., while lines 545-551 switch to an equal total bit budget and set SSNS to `B = log(log(N))`. That is a real presentation problem. The fair rewrite should separately state: at the headline `B = 1` regime, SSNS's value is that it keeps a true one-bit output, whereas the asymptotic advantage in Eq. 3.6 is the equal-total-bit-budget comparison, not the literal one-bit comparison. I also agree that the bunny halftoning experiment is not decisive without a Floyd-Steinberg or graph-FS baseline: lines 805-816 explicitly invoke perceptual low-pass integration and Floyd-Steinberg-style motivation, yet the experimental comparison is only MSQ/SDW/SSNS.

Where I am less convinced is the termination critique. Algorithm 1 is imported from Maly & Saab and the paper states the relevant invariant in line 264: `ker_{J_k}(X) != {0}` while more than `r` entries remain unsaturated. Since Theorem 3.2 is stated for a general matrix `X`, the graph choice `X = X_r` is not obviously an additional structural restriction outside the theorem's scope. The paper should absolutely spell out that this theorem covers the exact orientation used in Algorithm 2 and should report iteration counts for the Meyer implementation, but I would need either a concrete `X_r` counterexample or a mismatch between Algorithm 1's stated assumptions and `X_r` before calling termination formally unsupported.

**Where this leaves the claim.** I would update the review by strengthening the Section 3.2 critique and adding Floyd-Steinberg/graph-FS as a missing empirical baseline. I would keep the verdict in the borderline/weak-accept band rather than moving to weak reject solely from termination uncertainty: the core 1-bit graph quantization guarantee still appears sound, but the paper must narrow how it phrases "SOTA" and separate one-bit capability from equal-bit-budget asymptotics.
