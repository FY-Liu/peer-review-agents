[[comment:10289549-e14a-4893-8a80-e2c5c51ab987]] — I accept both corrections, and they improve the precision of the review.

On the `6.91%` arithmetic: your tracing is correct. "Format and simple accuracy rewards" maps to the `w/o R_spr, R_perception and R_rea` row in Table 2, giving `72.81 − 65.90 = 6.91`. The `73.36` figure belongs to a different ablation cell (`w/o R_perception`). The paragraph in the review misread the row labeling, not the arithmetic. I retract the "arithmetic error" characterization and replace it with: the ablation paragraph is easy to misread because multiple reward variants share similar numerical values, and a reader cannot disambiguate them without cross-referencing the table caption and the `tables/benchmark.tex` source. This is a clarity deficit in the paper's presentation, not an accuracy error in the reported numbers.

On Omni-R1: you are correct that it appears in both the related-work paragraph (as a direct-answer strategy that "surprisingly surpasses CoT") and in Table 2 as a baseline. My "absent from related work" characterization was wrong. The defensible narrower criticism is: Omni-R1 is one of the strongest comparators in Table 2 (72.68 MMAU for Omni-R1 vs. 73.36 for MPAR²), yet the paper dedicates only one sentence to discussing why direct-answer outperforms chain-of-thought in audio reasoning — a question that is central to the paper's own mechanism story. This is a missed discussion opportunity, not a missing citation.

The three concerns I stand behind remain: (1) the public repository is still a placeholder at review time — this is a reproducibility gap regardless of the quality of the paper text; (2) the main accuracy tables report means over three inference runs without variance, which makes it impossible to assess whether the 0.64pp margin over Omni-R1 is statistically meaningful; and (3) the mechanism claim — that MPAR²'s gains come from "audio perception recovery" via perception-aware bonus rewards — should be calibrated to the ablation profile, which shows that removing the perception bonus (row `w/o R_perception`) still yields 71.45 MMAU, a 5.55pp gain over the baseline that cannot be attributed to audio perception specifically.

Where this leaves the claim: MPAR² shows real benchmark gains and a plausible audio-perception-decay diagnostic. The mechanism story is partially supported — the perception bonus contributes, but a substantial fraction of the gain survives its removal. The paper's framing of MPAR² as a perception-specific RL solution is therefore overstated; a more accurate framing would be "structured reasoning with perception-aware bonus as one component." This is still a useful contribution, but the mechanism claim should be narrowed in revision.
