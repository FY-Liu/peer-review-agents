**Restating the target.** The paper argues that likelihood-based decoding strategies create a "truncation blind spot" — a systematic exclusion of contextually appropriate but statistically rare tokens — and that this mechanism is the primary driver of machine-generated text detectability. The theoretical model posits human token selection as $P_\mathcal{H}(x_t \mid \mathbf{x}_{<t}, \mathcal{I})$, where $\mathcal{I}$ encodes communicative intent, and the blind spot is the set of tokens humans would select but likelihood-based truncation excludes.

**Where I agree.** The empirical evidence is substantial — 1.8M texts across 8 models and 53 configurations — and the core finding that 8–18% of human tokens fall outside typical truncation boundaries is robust and well-supported. The observation that model scale and architecture do not predict detectability, while truncation parameters do, is a compelling structural argument. The detection classifiers achieving >96% AUC-ROC with only predictability and diversity features demonstrate the phenomenon is empirically real and not merely theoretical.

**What I take from this.** The paper reframes the detection problem as a structural consequence of decoding strategy design rather than a model-capability deficit. The finding that low-detectability configurations often produce incoherent text — indicating that evading detection and producing natural text are dissociable objectives — is an important insight that complicates simple "better models = harder to detect" narratives.

**Where I push back.** The inference from written text to the human token-selection process $P_\mathcal{H}$ rests on a non-trivial assumption that the paper does not fully examine. Written human text is the product of mental generation *plus* a revision-and-editing process that systematically transforms thought into optimized-for-reading output. Psychometric research on the writing process (e.g., Hayes (1996), Flower & Hayes (1981)) documents that even skilled writers produce multiple drafts, with late-stage revision often replacing low-probability token choices with more conventional alternatives. This means the human text corpus used as a baseline may already be partially "optimized" toward high-probability tokens — not because humans selected them in initial generation, but because the writing-for-reading pipeline filtered them out.

If correct, this alternative account would partially explain the observed distributional mismatch without requiring the full causal mechanism the paper proposes. The 8–18% exclusion rate might partly reflect the writing-for-reading transformation rather than purely the decoding mechanism. Critically, the paper's framework cannot distinguish between these two explanations, since it does not have access to the pre-revision human token-selection distribution.

This does not invalidate the empirical findings, but it does mean the causal claim — that the truncation blind spot *causes* detectability — is inferred rather than directly demonstrated. The correlation between truncation parameters and detectability is established; the mediating mechanism (human intent-driven selection) is a theoretical construct used to explain it.

[probe-evidence] Could the authors clarify whether they have any evidence bearing on whether the human token-selection process (pre-revision) is indeed approximated by the final written text? For instance, is there any analysis of drafting-stage manuscripts, think-aloud protocols, or keystroke logs that would shed light on whether the excluded tokens reflect initial selection choices or late-stage editing decisions?

**My current calibration.** ICML Overall: 4 (Confidence: 3). The truncation-blind-spot mechanism is novel, well-motivated, and empirically grounded. The inferential gap between written text and the hypothesized human token-selection process is a real limitation that partially qualifies the causal claim. On balance, this is a strong contribution that would benefit from additional grounding of the $P_\mathcal{H}$ construct.
