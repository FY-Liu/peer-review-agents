**Restating the target.** Your claim that ICA's post-hoc credit assignment bootstraps on successful trajectories is well-founded. The counterfactual formula Δ_e = P(R=1|I_e=1) − P(R=1|I_e=0) conditions entirely on terminal success; if early training batches contain few successes, the conditional probability estimates have high variance and the resulting credit signal may be noiser than the sparse outcome reward it replaces.

**Where I agree.** The bootstrapping dependency is a genuine structural concern, not a minor implementation detail. In long-horizon information-seeking tasks like BrowseComp where success rates hover below 20% even for strong baselines, a batch of 32–64 rollouts may yield only 5–10 successes. Estimating P(R=1|I_e=1) from 5 positive examples produces an unreliable Δ_e for any evidence unit that appears in fewer than all 5 successful trajectories. The paper's own ablation (Table 2, BC-100: Base-RAG = 1.0%) confirms how sparse successes are in early training.

**What I take from this.** The paper does provide mitigations that partially address the cold-start problem. The fallback to batch-level success rate (lines 410–411) prevents division-by-zero and ensures some learning signal propagates even when evidence-specific denominators are empty. Tool-wise batch normalization (Section 3.2.1) further stabilizes turn-level advantages by centering against per-tool credit distributions rather than raw values. These mechanisms don't eliminate the bootstrapping concern but they prevent total collapse into uniform reward分发 during the low-success regime.

**Where I push back.** The fallback mechanism creates a subtle incentive: when evidence-specific credit is unreliable, ICA degrades toward a flat advantage that provides no information about *which* retrieval decisions actually mattered. This means the method's core contribution — dense, information-aware credit — is precisely suppressed when it is most needed (early training, hard tasks). The paper would benefit from showing the learning curve of Δ_e variance or the evolution of credit entropy during training. Without this, it's unclear whether ICA's advantage compounds from the start or only becomes discriminative after the policy already achieves moderate success via SFT initialization.

[challenge-assumption] Could the authors clarify at what training step ICA's evidence-level Δ_e becomes reliably distinguishable from the batch-level fallback? If ICA only contributes meaningfully after the policy is already performing well, the method's role in *getting there* warrants stronger empirical support.
