**Restating the target.** The paper evaluates open-ended video aesthetics perception by using GPT-5.2 as a judge: given a question, a ground-truth caption, and a model's response, GPT-5.2 assigns a score of 0/1/2 based on semantic equivalence to the reference answer (§Appendix A). The authors treat this as a valid, principled evaluation methodology for comparing LMM capabilities.

**Where I agree.** Open-ended questions are the right way to probe explainable video aesthetics understanding — closed-ended formats like MCQ can only test recognition, not generation or reasoning. The authors are right to include this format, and the scoring rubric (0/1/2 with defined semantic criteria) is thoughtfully designed. The broader goal of benchmarking LMMs on video aesthetics is genuinely valuable and填补 a real gap in the literature.

**What I take from this.** The evaluation of open-ended responses is one of the hardest unsolved problems in LMM benchmarking. Using an LLM as judge is a pragmatic solution that many benchmarks adopt (e.g., for story generation, captioning, VQA). The authors' prompt design — strict semantic comparison against reference, no outside knowledge — is a reasonable attempt to operationalize this. I take seriously the difficulty of the problem they are solving.

**Where I push back.** The critical concern is that GPT-5.2 appears in two roles simultaneously: it is both the evaluator (judge) for open-ended questions and one of the 23 models being evaluated. In Table 1 (main results), GPT-5.2 achieves 69.20% on open-ended questions — a metric that GPT-5.2 itself computed. This creates a potential circularity: if GPT-5.2's internal evaluation preferences are systematically aligned with its own response style, it may score its own answers higher than comparably capable models, inflating its apparent performance. The paper does not report any inter-annotator reliability between GPT-5.2's scores and human judgments on open-ended responses, so we cannot verify whether the judge is calibrated. A Posner-Strike alternative reading is that the 69.20% figure for GPT-5.2 is an upper bound on performance, not a comparable metric to Claude-Sonnet-4.5's 64.91% MCQ scores, because the judge and the model are the same system in the open-ended case. Could the authors clarify whether any human validation of GPT-5.2's open-ended scoring was conducted, or whether the 69.20% GPT-5.2 result should be interpreted with additional caveats given the judge-model overlap?
