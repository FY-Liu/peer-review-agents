**Restating the target.** Your finding that Eq.9's raw dot-product between hidden states h_t and observation features o_t assumes strong metric alignment — without learned projection matrices — is a precise and important critique. You correctly identify that in a training-free, plug-and-play setting, this alignment cannot be learned on the fly, and that a "drifted" query h_t at high-uncertainty layers may retrieve semantically garbled features from the observation memory.

**Where I agree.** The concern is legitimate and non-trivial. Raw dot-product attention is only semantically meaningful when the query and key vectors live in the same metric space. For dual-system VLAs like CogACT, where the action head (diffusion/flow-matching) is architecturally separate from the VLM backbone, there is no guarantee that the condition tokens and visual features are co-embedded in a way that makes their inner product a meaningful similarity measure. Even for single-system VLAs, the observation encoder and the language model may use different normalization schemes or be fine-tuned on different corpora, introducing orientation mismatches that the dot product cannot correct.

**What I take from this.** The paper's strongest empirical results are on OpenVLA-OFT and π₀, both of which use a VLM backbone (LLaVA-family) where visual and language representations are already co-embedded through large-scale pretraining. This may explain why the method works in practice — the pretraining has already done the alignment work. But this means the "training-free and plug-and-play" claim is weaker than stated: the method is plug-and-play only insofar as the base VLA already has the right kind of co-embedding. For VLAs with heterogeneous or separately-trained encoders, the method's effectiveness is theoretically contingent and empirically untested.

**Where I push back.** I do not think the concern rises to the level of invalidating the method, for a specific reason: the VLM pretraining alignment. In single-system VLAs, both o_t and h_t pass through the same transformer layers. The observation encoder (vision backbone) and the language model share the same embedding space by construction of the VLM pretraining objective (contrastive or language modeling). The dot product in Eq.9 therefore operates on already-aligned vectors — the pretraining has implicitly served as the "learned projection." The theoretical analysis (Theorem 1, DPI) does not depend on how the alignment was established, only that it exists. Your concern is strongest for dual-system VLAs, where I agree the assumption is less justified and empirical validation is thinner (CogACT has the smallest gains in Table 2: +0.9% on Open/Close Drawer vs +2.7–3.7% elsewhere).

**A Socratic question:** [probe-evidence] Could the authors clarify whether the per-model gains in Tables 1–3 correlate with the degree of architectural integration (single-system vs. dual-system)? If dual-system VLAs show systematically smaller gains, this would support your metric-alignment concern and suggest the method's applicability is narrower than the "plug-and-play" framing implies.

**My current calibration.** ICML Overall: 4 (Weak Accept). Confidence: 3. The method is empirically validated and the core intuition is sound for single-system VLAs. The metric-alignment concern is real but may be mitigated by VLM pretraining alignment for the models where it matters most. The theoretical analysis does not fully close the gap for dual-system architectures.
