**Restating the target.** You raise three structural concerns about SoLA: (1) inference latency and accuracy degrade linearly with edit count N because routing requires a full scan over stored keys with no approximate-NN indexing; (2) the reversibility guarantee is unsound for causally chained edits — if Edit_j is trained after Edit_i, gradients for Edit_j flow through representations that include Edit_i's LoRA contribution, so revoking Edit_i leaves Edit_j's weights pointing at an inconsistent residual space; (3) the benchmarks (COUNTERFACT, ZsRE) are single-hop, leaving open whether the router correctly handles compositional multi-hop reasoning. You argue these gaps mean SoLA's claims are overstated relative to what was actually evaluated.

**Where I agree.** All three concerns are legitimate empirical gaps, not nitpicks. The routing scalability issue is real: a linear scan over N key vectors at inference is O(N) in compute and memory bandwidth, and for a system designed for *lifelong* editing with potentially thousands of edits, this is a practical concern the paper does not profile or address. The multi-hop evaluation gap is also genuine — MQuAKE and similar compositional benchmarks would have been informative, and the absence of such evaluation leaves a question mark over generalization beyond single-hop retrieval. Your framing of the reversibility concern is the most interesting: the revocation experiment in Table 5 uses independent edits (questions about distinct entities), which is exactly the regime where revocation is expected to work. The failure mode you describe — chained edits where Edit_j's gradients flow through Edit_i's activation — is real and unprobed.

**What I take from this.** The paper's contribution is more defensible than your critique implies, for a specific reason: SoLA's design is *conservative by philosophy*, not limited by oversight. The frozen LoRA + frozen key design explicitly sacrifices the ability to handle edit dependencies in exchange for a clean reversibility guarantee. The paper never claims to handle chained causal edits; it explicitly positions SoLA as addressing the orthogonal problem of preventing semantic drift and forgetting in sequential independent edits. The revocation mechanism is sound for the intended use case (independent factual corrections), and the paper's benchmarks probe exactly that case. Your concern about chained edits is a legitimate extension request, not a refutation of the current claims.

**Where I push back.** Your multi-hop framing conflates two distinct questions. SoLA is not trying to be a general reasoning architecture — it is a model editing technique for updating discrete factual knowledge. Multi-hop compositional reasoning would require a fundamentally different system (one that can route to multiple LoRA modules simultaneously and compose their outputs), which SoLA does not claim to provide. Asking whether SoLA handles multi-hop is like asking whether ROME handles multi-hop: it's a category error. The right question is whether SoLA correctly handles the single-hop case it claims to address, and the paper's ERR/TRR numbers on SCOTUS and zsRE say yes. [probe-evidence] Could you clarify whether the reversibility failure mode you describe (Edit_j trained on Edit_i's residual) has been observed empirically in any existing system, or is it a theoretical risk? The MELO paper you implicitly reference does update cluster centers continuously, which is a different failure mode than what SoLA's frozen-key design would produce.
