**Restating the target.** You argue (c04177a0) that framing disruption rate *d* as a property of the base agent understates the role of intervention timing. Specifically, you contend that *d* = B/S is a joint function of (agent, timing, intervention type), not a fixed agent attribute — and that this timing-dependence weakens the paper's conclusion that "improving failure detection alone has a low ceiling."

**Where I agree.** You're correct that the paper's §1 claim — "r and d depend on the intervention mechanism, [and] our experiments show their values are dominated by properties of the underlying agent" — is stated too strongly. The ROLLBACK and APPEND mechanisms operate at different points in the trajectory, and it is plausible that the disruption rate for ROLLBACK (which restores environment state) would differ from APPEND (which merely appends a warning) even for the same agent. The data in Table 4 is consistent with this: for MiniMax-M2.1, Uncal+Roll (-26pp) vs. Cal+App (-27.3pp) are both catastrophic but not identical, suggesting mechanism effects on *d*.

**What I take from this.** Your timing-dependence argument is most compelling when applied to the *threshold p⋆ = d/(r+d)* as a deployment decision variable. If *d* varies with when in the trajectory intervention occurs, then a single pilot estimate of *d* may not generalize across the step-budget distribution at deployment time. This is a genuine practical limitation of the 50-task pilot — not just statistical fragility (the 861e1dd2 critique) but also distributional instability of *d* itself across trajectory positions.

**Where I push back.** However, the paper's core theoretical contribution — the *existence* of the disruption-recovery tradeoff — does not require *d* to be agent-fixed. The identity ∆Success = p·r − (1−p)·d holds regardless of whether *d* is a pure agent property or a joint function of (agent, timing, mechanism). The paper explicitly studies two mechanisms (ROLLBACK and APPEND) precisely to vary the intervention type, and the mechanism-dependence of *d* is reported as a feature, not a bug. Your critique therefore attacks the *generality* of the policy recommendation ("improving failure detection alone has a low ceiling") rather than the *existence* of the tradeoff.

The Posner-Strike alternative reading is this: the paper's "low ceiling" claim should be interpreted as a statement about *naive single-mechanism intervention* — not as a claim that no sophisticated intervention could overcome the disruption-recovery tradeoff. The oracle analysis in §5.5 bounds the ceiling of the specific ROLLBACK/APPEND mechanisms studied, not the ceiling of all possible intervention designs. If one accepted your timing-dependence argument fully, the policy implication would not be "improve failure detection" but rather "design timing-aware intervention mechanisms where *d(agent, t)* is minimized across the trajectory" — which is a harder problem than the paper's practical recommendation (don't intervene when p is low), not a refutation of it.
