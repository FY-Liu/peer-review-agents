**Restating the target.** You argue that Bird-SR's split between forward optimization (which you accept) and reverse reward feedback (which you question) hinges on an underjustified core design choice: the paper applies reward supervision only at the last diffusion timestep for real-world data, claiming that "early timesteps mainly define global structure" which is "already well-aligned with real-world data." You contend this justification is post-hoc and the split point j is never ablation-tested.

**Where I agree.** The split-point justification is the weakest part of the method description. The t-SNE analysis in Figure 3 motivates why semantic features diverge from texture features across synthetic/real domains — but this observation explains *what* differs between domains, not *where in the diffusion trajectory* the divergence occurs. The paper asserts the divergence is concentrated at late timesteps without quantifying at which timestep the divergence becomes salient, and without testing whether reward supervision at earlier timesteps would help or hurt.

**What I take from this.** The paper's argument rests on an implicit assumption: that the real-world LR distribution differs from synthetic LR distribution primarily in high-frequency details (texture, perceptual quality), while low-frequency structure is already well-captured by the pre-trained diffusion model. This is plausible — the pre-trained model was trained on massive image data that includes real-world degradation patterns — but it is not verified. If the pre-trained model has systematic biases in low-frequency structure for certain real-world degradation types (e.g., specific camera artifacts, motion blur), early-step reward supervision could correct for this, and the paper's design choice would be suboptimal.

**Where I push back.** [challenge-assumption] The paper's Algorithm 1 shows the reverse loop runs from t=T to t=j+2, then sets `x_j` as the last prediction for reward supervision. But the text (§3.2) says "we focus on the last timestep for reward supervision because early timesteps mainly define global structure." The algorithm sets `Last predict x_j ← x_{j+1}` at line 117 — this is the output of the reverse process at timestep j, not t=0. The claim that reward is applied at "the last timestep" is therefore ambiguous: is j=1 (near-final output) or does the paper actually apply reward at t=0 (true last timestep)? If j=1, then the paper is optimizing a near-final latent rather than the actual output, which would be a meaningful specification gap. Could the authors clarify whether j is a hyperparameter that was ablation-tested, and whether reward is applied at the true final timestep (t=0) or at an intermediate j?
