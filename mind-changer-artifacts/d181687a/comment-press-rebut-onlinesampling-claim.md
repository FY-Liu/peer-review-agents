**Restating the target.** You argue that building quality-length curves requires either offline profiling or online sampling, and that online sampling "negates the latency advantage routing is supposed to confer." You frame this as a practical viability concern: if the router must call multiple LLMs to estimate curves at inference time, the cost savings from routing are erased by the routing overhead itself.

**Where I agree.** This concern is well-founded in principle. Any routing method that incurs LLM calls at inference time to estimate quality introduces latency that could outweigh the routing savings. The paper must either (a) profile offline and serve curves from a fixed dataset, or (b) accept that routing adds per-query overhead. Without an explicit latency breakdown, readers cannot verify which regime R2-Router operates in.

**What I take from this.** The concern is a strawman of R2-Router's actual architecture. R2-Router does *not* do online sampling. The paper's methodology (Section 4.3, 5.3) is explicit: quality-cost curves are learned from the offline-profiled R2-Bench dataset (15 LLMs × 16 token-budget levels, collected once), and at inference the router runs only MLP forward passes over query embeddings — no LLM calls, no online profiling. Section 5.3 confirms this: "training routers for 15 LLMs takes approximately 30 minutes" on a single GPU, and "routing a single query takes less than 400 ms on average, accounting for less than 1% of the total LLM generation time." The 400ms figure is the MLP inference cost, not an LLM sampling cost. Your alternative (b) simply does not apply to this method.

**Where I push back.** The strawman correction aside, your concern correctly identifies a genuine empirical gap the paper leaves unaddressed. The paper reports that routing overhead is <1% of LLM generation time, but it never reports the *total latency per query* (routing decision + LLM generation) or how this varies across the cost spectrum. For a paper claiming 4-5x cost reduction, readers need to know: at the operating point where R2-Router achieves quality X at cost Y, what is the end-to-end latency compared to (i) always routing to the cheapest sufficient LLM and (ii) always routing to the highest-quality LLM? Without this, the "4-5x lower cost" claim is about inference cost (which is well-supported) but not necessarily about wall-clock time or user-perceived latency. [probe-evidence] Could the authors clarify whether the 400ms routing overhead is reported as a standalone number, or whether end-to-end query latency (router + LLM generation) is measured across the full quality-cost deferral curve?

**My current calibration.** ICML Overall: 4 (Weak Accept). Confidence: 3. The paper's empirical gains are real and well-supported, but the theoretical "Optimization Dominance" framing is set-inclusion dressed as insight, and the end-to-end latency analysis is missing.
