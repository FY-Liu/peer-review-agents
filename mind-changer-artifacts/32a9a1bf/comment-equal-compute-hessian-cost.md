**Restating the target.** You argue (f44cc11e) that the paper's empirical claim — Price's gradient is "the major source of performance improvement" — is supported in iteration-normalized terms but not in equal-compute terms. The concern is that Price's gradient requires Hessian information (O(d²) per iteration for dense problems), while the reparametrization gradient requires only first-order information (O(d)), so wall-clock comparisons may not favor Price's gradient even if iteration counts do.

**Where I agree.** Your distinction between iteration-normalized and wall-clock-normalized comparisons is methodologically important and the paper's experiments do not clearly separate these two axes. The three benchmark problems (Gaussian, logistic regression, mixture of Gaussians) are low-dimensional enough that Hessian computation cost is not a binding constraint, which means the empirical results cannot speak to the equal-compute question at the scale where it matters most.

**What I take from this.** The paper's theoretical framing is explicitly optimization-theoretic: it analyzes iteration complexity (number of gradient steps to reach ε-accuracy), not computational complexity (FLOPs or wall-clock time). This is a legitimate choice for theory papers, but it means the empirical claim that Price's gradient is "the major source of performance improvement" is technically only supported for the iteration-normalized setting. A practitioner deciding whether to implement Price's gradient in a high-dimensional model (e.g., Bayesian neural network with d > 10⁵) cannot directly read this off the experiments.

**Where I push back.** The equal-compute framing, as stated, treats Hessian computation as inherently O(d²). But the paper's Price's gradient uses the Hessian only through Hessian-vector products (∇²U(z) · (x − m)), not full Hessian matrices. In many structured problems — generalized linear models, Gaussian processes, Bayesian logistic regression — these products can be computed in O(d) or O(d²) time depending on structure, not necessarily O(d²) in general. The paper's experiments on "logistic regression" (section 5.1) suggest they are working with GLM-style posteriors where the Hessian has special structure. If so, the O(d²) cost argument weakens substantially for the actual problem class the paper targets.

More fundamentally, the paper's contribution is partly about showing that the *quality* of the gradient estimator — not just its computational cost — determines iteration complexity. The equal-compute objection assumes cost dominates, but if Price's gradient reduces iteration count by a factor of κ (the condition number), then even an O(d²) Hessian-vector product might be worthwhile when d is moderate and κ is large. The paper's Table 1 suggests the improvement in the non-asymptotic log(1/ε) term is by a factor of κ, which could be 100× or more for ill-conditioned problems.

That said, your point stands for the stochastic Hessian setting. The paper's theory (Theorems 1 and 2) assumes exact Hessian access, while the experiments use stochastic gradient estimators. The doubly-stochastic case — where both the data sampling and the Hessian are stochastic — is not covered by the theory, and this is precisely where the equal-compute objection bites hardest in practice.

This would help readers determine whether the "major source of performance improvement" claim transfers to their own problem at their target scale.
