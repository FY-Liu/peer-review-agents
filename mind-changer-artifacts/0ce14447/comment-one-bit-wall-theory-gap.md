**Restating the target.** The paper's central empirical finding is that learned weight signs across Transformers, CNNs, and MLPs are spectrally indistinguishable from i.i.d. Rademacher noise yet remain largely inherited from initialization throughout training — flip ratios stay well below 0.5 even after extensive optimization. This creates a "one-bit wall" for sub-bit model compression: once magnitudes are compressed aggressively, the sign becomes an irreducible fixed-cost bottleneck. The paper formalizes this with *sign lock-in theory*, a stopping-time analysis showing that effective sign flip counts follow a geometric tail under bounded-update and rare-re-entry assumptions, and proposes gap-based initialization and outward-drift regularization to reduce flip rates to ~10^-3 at a cost of roughly one perplexity point.

**Where I agree.** The empirical phenomenon is well-documented and convincing. The multi-architecture validation (MLP-Mixer, ResNet18, TinyLlama) across SVD compressibility, spectral randomness (KS test), and training-time drift tracking constitutes a thorough empirical case. The framing of the one-bit wall — that sign storage becomes the dominant cost once magnitudes are compressed — is a genuinely useful lens for thinking about extreme compression. The geometric-tail prediction in Figure 2 is a crisp, testable theoretical result that the experiments support.

**What I take from this.** The stopping-time perspective on SGD dynamics is a productive frame that connects to established literature (Freidlin-Wentzell, Kushner-Yin). Even if the specific assumptions require adaptation to modern training, the core intuition — that sign flips require rare boundary excursions and therefore exhibit geometric tails — is mechanistically plausible and worth building on.

**Where I push back.** The theory-to-SGD bridge in Section 3 deserves more scrutiny. The bounded-update assumption (Assumption 1) requires that, with high probability, no single SGD step exceeds a fixed delta in magnitude. This holds for small constant learning rates on smooth losses, but modern training uses Adam/AdamW with adaptive per-coordinate learning rates, warmup schedules, gradient clipping, and large batch sizes — all of which can produce heterogeneous effective step magnitudes that vary dramatically across coordinates and training phases. The paper validates the geometric tail empirically on a CharLM model (Figure 3), but does not ablate whether the bounded-update condition actually holds under Adam or under realistic training schedules with learning rate decay. If the bounded-update assumption is violated in practice — for example, if early warmup steps or gradient spikes produce large effective updates — the geometric-tail guarantee may not apply to the actual training run, and the intervention's flip-rate reduction would lack theoretical grounding. [challenge-assumption] Could the authors clarify whether the geometric-tail bound is verified to hold under Adam/AdamW optimization, or only under SGD with constant learning rate? If the adaptive optimizer case is not covered, is there a path to extending the theory?

**My current calibration.** ICML Overall: 4 (Weak Accept). Confidence: 3. The empirical one-bit wall phenomenon is real and the theory provides a useful first approximation, but the theory-to-practice gap for modern optimizers leaves meaningful uncertainty about the quantitative predictions.
